Task Description: The objective of this project is to determine whether the reduced mechanical forces associated with microgravity lead to osteoarthritis.

Although the effects of microgravity on bone are well known and appear to be reversible, the effects reduced mechanical forces on the cartilage surfaces of articulating joints are less well understood. Studies in humans and animals clearly demonstrate that osteoarthritis results from abnormal (both increased and decreased) mechanical forces on cartilage. Based on these findings, we hypothesize that appropriate mechanical forces are critical for the health of articular cartilage. Following on from this, we further hypothesize that the absence of significant biomechanical forces affects that ability of the chondrocyte to maintain healthy functioning cartilage. This may lead to accelerated cartilage breakdown and osteoarthritis.

There have been no studies that specifically investigate whether microgravity results in the degeneration of articular cartilage. Articular cartilage breakdown could be a major consequence of long-term space travel because, unlike bone, once cartilage degradation begins it cannot be reversed, typically leading to osteoarthritis. The mobility of an individual affected by even mid-stage osteoarthritis would be compromised leading to a failure to complete the full range of mission duties.

The availability of joint tissue exposed to an extended period of microgravity is an ideal opportunity to address these hypotheses. Therefore, we have designed a focused Aim that will examine the joints of mice returned from microgravity for altered gene and protein expression and evidence of cartilage breakdown. This will be achieved using a range of robust and well-established methods including histology for joint surface integrity, immunohistochemistry using antibodies against a range of cartilage proteins, and gene expression analysis tools.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: We expect to determine whether cartilage degradation occurs in space. In essence, we are looking at the changes that occur under conditions of altered biomechanical forces. The effects of increased biomechanical forces have been documented for many years but the effects of greatly reduced biomechanical forces are largely unknown. We expect that these studies will provide new insights into mechano-sensitive pathways in chondrocytes, the cells that make cartilage. This in turn may lead to the development of novel therapies and treatment options for patients with osteoarthritis and other conditions where biomechanical forces on the joint are altered.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2015 
Task Progress: This grant supported research involving NASA's 15-day STS-131 flight and the 30-day Russian Bion-M1 mission.

Mechanical forces are critical for maintaining healthy articular cartilage. Altering the mechanical forces on weight-bearing articulating joints accelerates articular cartilage degeneration leading to osteoarthritis. Conversely, the lack of mechanical forces also leads to osteoarthritis. It is clear, therefore, that normal mechanical forces are critical for the health and maintenance of articular cartilage.

The long-term objective of this project is to understand the effects of microgravity on articular cartilage and specifically, whether the absence of significant mechanical forces due to microgravity results in cartilage breakdown. The presence of spaceflight-related cartilage degradation will impact multiple aspects of skeletal biology related to the failure to accommodate dynamic load, such as joint damage, bone fragility, and muscle atrophy.

Summary of STS-131 findings: 15 days exposure to microgravity: For the STS-131 mission, animals were subjected to 15 days of microgravity and the joints harvested three hours following landing. There was no difference in toluidine blue staining between flight and ground articular cartilage. We conclude that 15 days exposure to microgravity does not alter proteoglycan content of articular cartilage in mouse knee joints. miRNA data: Bioinformatic analysis of the miRNA data demonstrated that 10 miRNAs were upregulated (p>0.001) and 26 were downregulated in flight compared to control mice.

mRNA data: Thirteen mRNAs were upregulated >1.5 fold in flight compared to the ground control animals and one downregulated >1.5 fold. Of the 13 upregulated genes, 7 have been implicated in either osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, or both.

Summary of Bion-M1 findings: Effects on cartilage of 30 days exposure to microgravity. There was significant proteoglycan loss, as shown by reduced toluidine staining in the knee cartilage of flight animals compared to the non-flight animals (p=0.008, students t-test). Interestingly, many of the chondrocytes within the middle zone remain blue. Despite this proteoglycan degradation, no clear structural changes to the articular cartilage surface of the flight mice were present suggesting that, with 30 days of unloading, the collagen II network remains intact.

Conclusions

Extensive literature exists in humans and in animal models suggesting that a normal range of biomechanical forces is required for cartilage homeostasis. Consistent with this is our finding that the reduced biomechanical environment of microgravity induces cartilage breakdown. Although there was no proteoglycan loss after 15 days of microgravity on STS-131, there is clear evidence of proteoglycan loss after 30 days on Bion. In addition, the mRNA changes in Bion are consistent with reduced cartilage matrix synthesis and further support the finding that cartilage atrophy occurs in spaceflight.

Based on these data we speculate that the maintenance of biomechanical loading during spaceflight will reduce cartilage destruction. We believe exercise is the countermeasure.

Several publications are in preparation for submission to peer-reviewed journals.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2013 
Task Progress: The major progress of the past 12 months was the tissue sample collection for the BION-M1 mission as part of the Biospecimen Sharing Program (BSP). BION-M1 is an effort by the Russians to study the effect of an extended period of exposure to microgravity on biological organisms â€“ the first such mission since the mid 70s. The goal was to expose mice, gerbils, lizards, fish, and freshwater snails to microgravity for 30 days at an altitude of 575 kms on an unmanned Soyuz II spacecraft launched on April 19, 2013.

The US-based BSP investigators travelled to Moscow for the May 19 landing of Soyuz II near Orenburg. The animals were shipped immediately to Moscow and the Institute for Biomedical Problems (IMBP) where the dissections were to take place in a laboratory set-up for us by the Russians. The BSP investigators were assigned flight 6 mice and we were to dissect the knees and elbows from these and a cohort of vivarium controls. The right elbows and knees from each mouse were placed in formalin in preparation for histological and immunohistochemical analyses. The left elbows and knees were placed in RNALater for RNA preservation for gene expression analysis. The dissections took 12 hours to complete.

The team will return to Moscow in August to complete the ground control dissections.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2012 
Task Progress: Articular cartilage is located at the ends of long bones in the weight-bearing articulating joints of the knee, ankle, shoulder, hip, hand, foot and spine. It is a tissue that is precisely engineered to absorb and distribute both tensile and compressive biomechanical forces. These biomechanical forces are important for maintaining healthy articular cartilage and any deviation from the normal range of forces accelerates cartilage degradation. Alterations in the normal mechanical forces can be caused by injuries that destabilize the joint, such as meniscal cartilage or ligament damage. In addition, there is evidence from joint immobilization studies that reduced mechanical forces also lead to cartilage atrophy. Breakdown of articular cartilage initiates an irreversible cascade of degradative changes that typically leads to osteoarthritis (OA), a painful and debilitating condition that reduces mobility and has a high impact on daily activities. In humans, cartilage breakdown due to limb immobilization is rare but during long-duration spaceflight, where the normal biomechanical forces due to gravity are significantly reduced, cartilage breakdown and osteoarthritis may be a major concern. Based on these findings, we hypothesize that:

The reduced biomechanical forces due to microgravity impair the ability of chondrocytes to maintain healthy articular cartilage, leading to increased cartilage breakdown. The STS-131 mission, which will expose mice to a 15-day period of microgravity, provided an ideal opportunity to test this hypothesis. We proposed to conduct cell and molecular analyses on articular cartilage of mice exposed to an extended period of microgravity, for evidence of cartilage degradation. We expect to determine whether cartilage degradation occurs as a result of microgravity. Together, the histological and immunohistochemical, and gene expression studies represent a comprehensive and robust approach that will allow us to monitor the extent and severity of cartilage breakdown. These studies will provide new insights into the role of biomechanical forces in cartilage structure and function. We have designed the study in such a way that maximizes the data generated from the small amount of tissue available. While several studies have examined the behavior of chondrocytes in simulated microgravity, there are no published studies that have investigated cartilage characteristics in mouse articular cartilage expose to microgravity aboard an orbiting vehicle.

In 2012 we continued our analyses on articular cartilage and skeletal muscle tissue obtained from animals flown on STS-131. Our data indicated that 15 days exposure to microgravity does not alter proteoglycan content of articular cartilage in mouse knee joints. Longer duration exposure to spaceflight is required to adequately address the issue of whether cartilage breakdown can be detected at the tissue level. This will be examined in the 30-day Bion-M1 flight opportunity later in 2012. Gene expression analyses demonstrate that exposure to 15 days of microgravity results in a number of articular cartilage- and skeletal muscle-specific differences in gene expression. However, we found that there were six genes that were significantly increased in microgravity in both tissues. This is a significant finding because it indicates that there is a common response to microgravity between different tissues. These genes have been implicated in cellular stress response pathways. These stress response networks typically function to stop or slow down cell proliferation to give cells the opportunity to divert resources to addressing the cellular stress. This is a new and exciting finding and one that will be followed up with additional gene and protein expression experiments. These data will also allow us to frame new hypotheses for future spaceflight experiments in rodents and humans.

In addition, the PI organized a workshop, entitled "NEW INSIGHTS INTO THE EFFECTS OF SPACEFLIGHT IN MUSCULOSKELETAL TISSUES," at the 2012 Orthopaedic Research Society annual meeting in San Francisco (Feb 4-7, 2012) around the STS-131 experiments:

NEW INSIGHTS INTO THE EFFECTS OF SPACEFLIGHT IN MUSCULOSKELETAL TISSUES. Organizers: Jamie Fitzgerald, PhD and Stavros Thomopoulos, PhD. The response of skeletal tissues to microgravity is poorly understood. While it is recognized that bone mineral density decreases and muscles atrophy in spaceflight, very little is known about the molecular underpinnings of these effects. In addition, virtually nothing is known about the effects of microgravity on other musculoskeletal tissues such as cartilage and tendon. The speakers in this workshop all participated in a NASA mouse tissue Biospecimen Sharing Program on two Space Shuttle flights in 2010 and 2011. The purpose of this workshop is to educate the audience about the latest insights into the effects of spaceflight on bone and muscle and two previously under-investigated tissues: cartilage and tendon. The application of new cellular and molecular techniques to the analysis of these tissues provide new insights into the effects of microgravity on musculoskeletal tissues.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2011 
Task Progress: These results are from experiments conducted on mouse tissues obtained from animals flown on STS-131 in April 2010. The focus of the study was on articular cartilage and whether the microgravity of spaceflight caused increased cartilage breakdown and potentially osteoarthritis (OA). Since there was sufficient muscle tissue surrounding the dissected joints, we expended the RNA expression analysis to include skeletal muscle in addition to articular cartilage. This allowed a comparison of gene expression changes due to spaceflight to be conducted on two biomechanically-relevant tissues.

Histological analysis of joint cartilage: Joints from four flight and four ground animals were paraffin-embedded, sectioned and sections stained for proteoglycan using toluidine blue. Proteoglycan degradation is a key indicator of cartilage breakdown and one of the first stages in osteoarthritis. There was no difference in toluidine blue staining between flight and ground articular cartilage. We conclude that 15 days exposure to microgravity does not alter proteoglycan content of articular cartilage in mouse knee joints. Longer duration exposure to spaceflight is required to adequately address the issue of whether cartilage breakdown can be detected at the tissue level. This will be examined in the 30-day Bion-M1 flight opportunity in 2012.

RNA expression analysis in articular cartilage (and skeletal muscle): Total RNA was prepared from articular cartilage (AC) and skeletal muscle (SkM) samples of four flight and three (AC) or four (SkM) ground control mice. There were 15 samples in total. The average RNA yield for AC and SkM was 1.2 and 28.9 microgram respectively and all samples were intact. Amplified and labeled cDNA samples were each hybridized to a Mouse Gene 1.0 ST array.

Articular cartilage: In AC, 13 mRNAs were upregulated >1.5-fold in flight compared to the ground control animals and one downregulated >1.5 fold with P<0.0001. Of the 13 upregulated genes, 7 have been implicated in either osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis or both. This suggests that the genetic networks response to microgravity is similar to that reported for increased cartilage breakdown. The highest upregulated gene was Vdr (vitamin D receptor). Vdr is a key genetic and biochemical marker of osteoarthritis (OA). Several genetic association studies on large populations of OA patients have linked the Vdr gene locus with knee osteoarthritis. The link between Vdr and OA is further highlighted by the finding that Vdr mRNA is upregulated in severe osteoarthritis. Consistent with this is the report that, vitamin D, the Vdr ligand, upregulates matrix metalloproteinase (MMP) production. MMPs are the major class of matrix-degrading enzymes in cartilage.

We conclude that exposure of cartilage to the unloaded biochemical conditions of spaceflight initiate gene expression changes that are consistent with cartilage breakdown. However, at the gross level, these changes are not significant enough to have altered the proteoglycan content of cartilage â€“ a key biochemical indicator of cartilage degradation. Longer duration spaceflight are needed to assess whether this will ultimately occur. While we have not proven our hypothesis, that increased cartilage breakdown will occur in response to microgravity, our gene expression findings suggest that cartilage breakdown has been initiated at the gene level. Tissue level changes may take longer to occur.

Skeletal muscle: In SkM, 20 mRNAs were upregulated >1.5-fold (p< 0.0001) in flight compared to ground and two were downregulated. Sox4 was the highest upregulated gene. Our skeletal muscle gene expression data had good concordance with a previous report on the analysis of muscle gene expression changes in spaceflight. All the major genes upregulated in that study were upregulated in this study.

Comparison of cartilage and muscle gene expression alterations in spaceflight: Interestingly, when the gene expression changes in the two tissues were compared it was clear that six genes were upregulated in both tissues. These were Nfkbia, Ddit4, Cirbp, Mt1, Sik1 and Cdkn1a. Notably, all six genes have been implicated in cellular stress response pathways and respond to a variety of stresses including genotoxic, cold, heat and oxidative stress. These stress response networks typically function to stop or slow down cell proliferation to give cells the opportunity to divert resources to addressing the cellular stress. Alternatively the cell responds by undergoing apoptosis.

Our data suggests that 15 days of spaceflight induces a generalized stress response. The implication is that microgravity is recognized as a form of stress by cartilage and muscle and stress response genetic networks are engaged as with other types of cellular stress. This is a new and exciting finding and one that will be followed up with additional gene and protein expression experiments. These data will also allow us to frame new hypotheses for future spaceflight experiments in rodents and humans.

Our current model for the effect of microgravity on cartilage and skeletal muscle is as follows: In both tissues one of the central effects to the reduced biomechanical forces of microgravity is a cellular stress response. The cell recognizes that the environment is altered and integrates a series of signals that result in activation of the core stress response pathway. This persists for the duration of microgravity (or at least for the 15 day duration of STS-131). At the cellular level, this persistent activation of stress response networks results in a reduction in cell proliferation and/or apoptosis. In time, this causes muscle atrophy and cartilage breakdown, although cartilage degradation remains to be proved. For the astronaut, our data suggest that dampening stress response pathways may ameliorate the negative effects of spaceflight on skeletal muscle.

Task Description: The objective of this project is to determine whether the reduced mechanical forces associated with microgravity lead to osteoarthritis.

Although the effects of microgravity on bone are well known and appear to be reversible, the effects reduced mechanical forces on the cartilage surfaces of articulating joints are less well understood. Studies in humans and animals clearly demonstrate that osteoarthritis results from abnormal (both increased and decreased) mechanical forces on cartilage. Based on these findings, we hypothesize that appropriate mechanical forces are critical for the health of articular cartilage. Following on from this, we further hypothesize that the absence of significant biomechanical forces affects that ability of the chondrocyte to maintain healthy functioning cartilage. This may lead to accelerated cartilage breakdown and osteoarthritis.

There have been no studies that specifically investigate whether microgravity results in the degeneration of articular cartilage. Articular cartilage breakdown could be a major consequence of long-term space travel because, unlike bone, once cartilage degradation begins it cannot be reversed, typically leading to osteoarthritis. The mobility of an individual affected by even mid-stage osteoarthritis would be compromised leading to a failure to complete the full range of mission duties.

The availability of joint tissue exposed to an extended period of microgravity is an ideal opportunity to address these hypotheses. Therefore, we have designed a focused Aim that will examine the joints of mice returned from microgravity for altered gene and protein expression and evidence of cartilage breakdown. This will be achieved using a range of robust and well-established methods including histology for joint surface integrity, immunohistochemistry using antibodies against a range of cartilage proteins and gene expression analysis tools.
