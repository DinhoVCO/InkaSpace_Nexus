NOTE: Extended to 6/30/2014 per A. Chu/ARC (Ed., 5/28/2013)

Task Description: Plants provide a complete and economical means for human life support for long-term space exploration and habitation. However, since the space environment is not optimal for plant growth, an understanding of how plants sense and respond to changes in their environment is of fundamental importance. The phosphoinositide (PI) pathway is highly conserved among eukaryotes and functions in the regulation of a multitude of cellular pathways. The lipid-derived second messenger inositol 1,4,5-trisphosphate (InsP3) increases in response to many different stresses. We have shown that InsP3 levels increase with gravistimulation prior to visible bending in both monocot and dicot systems. We have generated transgenic Arabidopsis plants expressing the mammalian type I inositol polyphosphate 5-phosphatase (InsP 5-ptase), an enzyme that specifically hydrolyzes InsP3 and terminates the signal. The transgenic plants have normal growth and morphology; however, they exhibit altered responses to many environmental stimuli including gravity, drought, and cold. While rapid changes in transcript levels occur in wild type Arabidopsis within 5 min of gravistimulation, the expression of several of the fastest responding genes does not change in the InsP 5-ptase roots in response to reorientation.

Our hypothesis is that InsP3 is an important second messenger in the sensing and signaling of stimuli (including gravity). The plants with compromised InsP3 signaling therefore provide a valuable tool for dissecting the role of the InsP3 pathway in plant responses to the microgravity environment encountered on the International Space Station.

Our primary aim is to identify the molecular changes that are specifically mediated by InsP3 in the space environment. We will compare transcript and protein profiles of wild type and transgenic plants that are grown in both microgravity and 1g in space as well as on the ground. The long term goal is to understand the molecular mechanisms plants use to sense and respond to changes in their environment. This knowledge will help design plants which are better able to withstand space flight and microgravity conditions.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: The InsP 5-ptase transgenic plants provide a good model system for understanding several issues including plant responses to gravity, plant responses to environmental stress, and fundamental aspects of PI signaling. Our previous work has implicated InsP3 as a key player in gravity sensing and signaling and the transgenic plants have attenuated InsP3 signaling and reduced gravitropism. In addition these plants have altered responses to different environmental stresses. Therefore this study will help us understand plant signaling during environmental stress, which has long term implications with improving crops to better withstand unfavorable conditions. Furthermore, the phosphoinositide pathway is highly conserved among plants, yeast, and mammals. These studies using the transgenic plants with attenuated InsP3 signaling will provide valuable insights on the regulation and downstream effectors of this pathway and could also contribute to our understanding of the role of the PI pathway in regulating human physiology.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2015 
Task Progress: We have carried out a second RNA-Sequencing experiment on the Illumina platform utilizing root samples from position 3 cassettes. We are currently analyzing/processing the data and will compare the differential gene expression results from both RNA-Seq trials. We have also set up collaborations with colleagues in the College to further analyze the results. We will determine the effect of the fractional g gradient by comparing the results from the different cassette positions. We are also looking to develop a gene regulatory network for root development under microgravity.

The follow-up proposal for flight experiments in response to the NASA solicitation NNH14ZTT001N submitted in March 2014 was selected for funding and is entitled, "Transcriptional and Post Transcriptional Regulation of Seedling Development in Microgravity."

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2014 
Task Progress: We have successfully isolated RNA from root samples from the flight experiment and carried out a comprehensive transcriptomic analysis of wild type and transgenic roots using RNA-Seq. Preliminary results show that there are significant changes between 1 g and µg conditions as well as between wild type and transgenic plants. Work is underway to compare differential gene expression in shoot samples. We anticipate that the Plant Signaling experiment will provide a comprehensive dataset that will be a valuable resource for other researchers in the field.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2013 
Task Progress: 1. The Plant signaling post-flight Ground Test was carried out at NASA ARC from 08/15/2012- 08/20/2012. The purpose of this test was to try and duplicate the environmental parameters encountered in the space flight (ramping up of CO2 levels). At the end of the test the samples were shipped to the PI lab for further analysis.

2. Work has been focused on growth measurements from the downloaded images obtained from the Plant signaling experiments. In particular, the goal is to compare root growth parameters between the 1g and micro g samples. Interestingly, preliminary results suggest that there were no obvious differences in root growth rates or cumulative growth between 1g and micro g samples; nor were there any significant differences between wild type and transgenic root growth (which is consistent with their growth on the ground).

3. The PI attended the ASGSR meeting in New Orleans, LA in November 2012; She served as a judge for the student poster competition.

4. The flight samples were finally transported back to Earth from the ISS on the Space X-2 on March 26, 2013. The samples were delivered safely to the PI’s lab on April 2, 2013. In the PI’s lab the seed cassettes were removed from the cold stowage bags, cataloged, and stored in the -80ºC freezer. Upon arrival in the PI’s lab the samples were frozen and appeared to be intact. The major focus of the next few months will be to process the flight samples for RNA-Seq analysis to compare expression of wild type plants with the transgenic plants which have compromised InsP3 signaling.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2012 
Task Progress: The major part of last year was spent with preparing the flight build and with the execution of the experiment on board the ISS.

1. The research associate spent 10 days at NASA ARC to participate in the flight build. During this time, she sterilized the wild type and transgenic seeds, mounted the seeds on the membranes and assembled the seed cassettes. (June 2011)

2. The Plant Signaling Experiments were carried to the ISS onboard the final shuttle flight STS-135. The PI and PI research team were fortunate to be able visit Florida and witness the launch. (July 2011)

3. The 2 experimental runs were conducted onboard the ISS during July-Aug 2011. The PI visited NASA ARC and was present for the first experimental run. This included monitoring the start of the experiment (by hydration of the seed cassettes) and monitoring images of seedling growth downloaded every 6hs. The PI was present to witness the end of experimental run 1, EC disassembly by the astronaut and transfer to the MELFI freezer for storage. Experimental runs 1 and 2 were both carried out without any issues. Germination rates and seedling growth were very good in both experimental runs. The samples were stored safely in the freezer and are awaiting return back to Earth.

4. The PI and research associate attended the ASGSB meeting in CA in November 2011 and presented a poster describing the Plant Signaling experiments.

5. A ground control experiment is scheduled to be conducted at NASA ARC in May/June 2012 which will closely mimic the environmental parameters (temperature, CO2) that were encountered during Plant Signaling on orbit runs. The samples from this test will be sent to the PI for analysis.

6. It is anticipated that the flight samples will be returned to Earth on the SpaceX Dragon vehicle and is scheduled for fall of 2012.

Task Description: Plants provide a complete and economical means for human life support for long-term space exploration and habitation. However, since the space environment is not optimal for plant growth, an understanding of how plants sense and respond to changes in their environment is of fundamental importance. The phosphoinositide (PI) pathway is highly conserved among eukaryotes and functions in the regulation of a multitude of cellular pathways. The lipid-derived second messenger inositol 1,4,5-trisphosphate (InsP3) increases in response to many different stresses. We have shown that InsP3 levels increase with gravistimulation prior to visible bending in both monocot and dicot systems. We have generated transgenic Arabidopsis plants expressing the mammalian type I inositol polyphosphate 5-phosphatase (InsP 5-ptase), an enzyme that specifically hydrolyzes InsP3 and terminates the signal. The transgenic plants have normal growth and morphology; however, they exhibit altered responses to many environmental stimuli including gravity, drought and cold. While rapid changes in transcript levels occur in wild type Arabidopsis within 5 min of gravistimulation, the expression of several of the fastest responding genes does not change in the InsP 5-ptase roots in response to reorientation.

Our hypothesis is that InsP3 is an important second messenger in the sensing and signaling of stimuli (including gravity). The plants with compromised InsP3 signaling therefore, provide a valuable tool for dissecting the role of the InsP3 pathway in plant responses to the microgravity environment encountered on the International Space Station.

Our primary aim is to identify the molecular changes that are specifically mediated by InsP3 in the space environment. We will compare transcript and protein profiles of wild type and transgenic plants that are grown in both microgravity and 1g in space as well as on the ground. The long term goal is to understand the molecular mechanisms plants use to sense and respond to changes in their environment. This knowledge will help design plants which are better able to withstand space flight and microgravity conditions.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: The InsP 5-ptase transgenic plants provide a good model system for understanding several issues including plant responses to gravity, plant responses to environmental stress, and fundamental aspects of PI signaling. Our previous work has implicated InsP3 as a key player in gravity sensing and signaling and the transgenic plants have attenuated InsP3 signaling and reduced gravitropism. In addition these plants have altered responses to different environmental stresses. Therefore this study will help us understand plant signaling during environmental stress which has long term implications with improving crops to better withstand unfavorable conditions. Furthermore, the phosphoinositide pathway is highly conserved among plants, yeast, and mammals. These studies using the transgenic plants with attenuated InsP3 signaling will provide valuable insights on the regulation and downstream effectors of this pathway and could also contribute to our understanding of the role of the PI pathway in regulating human physiology.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2011 
Task Progress: The major focus for the past year was with ground studies in preparation for the flight experiment. Some of this work was carried out in the PI’s lab and some of the work was carried out in collaboration with the scientists and engineers at NASA-AMES Research Center.

The major milestones are as follows:

1. The PI visited NASA-AMES and met with the ARC science team. The feasibility of the experiment as well as the time for preparations for flight was assessed.

2. Ground based studies were carried out to select seed batches with good germination and establish protocols for seed sterilization and media preparation as well as test seed viability with extended storage and optimal conditions for growth.

3. The PI and research associate participated in a ground test conducted at ARC using the EUEs and experimental containers (EC)s. This test helped establish the light conditions for germination and growth and examined the timeline for the experiment.

4. The PI participated in a ESA Biology Investigators working group meeting in Cologne, Germany in December 2010 and presented an overview of the Plant Signaling flight experiment.

5. Schedule Test at N-USOC in Trondheim, Norway in Jan 2011. This test was conducted in the engineering model (EM) which is the ground model of the EMCS present on the ISS. The purpose of this test was to check and verify EMCS Schedules for the Plant Signaling experiment and also monitor germination and growth of the plants during the experiment. The research associate was involved in setting up the EUEs at ARC and the PI was present in Norway for the Schedule test.

6. Operation verification Test (OVT) in Trondheim, Norway in March 2011. This test was carried to according to flight-build procedures and requirements and the experiment was run as it would be on flight. Based on the OVT the timeline for the experiment has been refined. The research associate and ARC team were involved in the flight build and the PI was present at ARC for the OVT.

7. The flight build will be carried out in May 2011 by the PI team and ARC team. The experiment is scheduled to be on board STS-135 (scheduled launch date in June). It is expected that the experiment will be carried out in the EMCS on the ISS during July/August 2011.

8. The PI team has carried out several ground tests in flight like hardware and worked on optimizing protocols for recovery of RNA and proteins from small amounts of tissue.
