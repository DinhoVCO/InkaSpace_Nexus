NOTE: Extended to 7/1/2017 per NSSC information (Ed., 4/5/16)

Task Description: The long-term goal of this research is to identify and characterize mechanisms of plant vacuole biogenesis that are critical for gravity perception. A role of the vacuole in controlling gravity perception is well accepted, but the molecular mechanisms by which the vacuole interacts with amyloplasts or regulates their sedimentation during gravitropism are yet to be identified. We recently uncovered a novel vacuole phenotype in agravitropic mutant alleles of VTI11 in Arabidopsis. We predict that this vacuole phenotype of vti11 mutants has negative effects on the organization of actin cables or the cortical endoplasmic reticulum, and therefore prevents amyloplast sedimentation. In addition, we have uncovered a novel regulation of vacuolar membrane fusion by phosphoinositides. Based on our results, we developed the hypothesis that mechanisms of vacuole remodeling that are mediated by phosphoinositide signaling are critical for the perception or transduction of the gravitropic signal in plants. A unique assay for vacuole fusion that we developed will be used to determine the role of specific cellular components in membrane fusion and gravity perception. The specific aims of this research project are to: 1) Determine subcellular phenotypes that impair gravity perception in itt3/vti11. 2) Identify the role of phosphoinositides in gravitropism. 3) Determine the role of the Adaptor Protein (AP-3) complex on vacuole biogenesis and gravitropism.

Overall, this research will improve our understanding of vacuole biogenesis, the interactions between the vacuole and other cellular components involved in gravity perception, and the role of phosphoinosidides in gravitropism.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: This work will contribute to the understanding of the molecular mechanisms that control the response of plants to gravity. In addition, the function of the vacuole in establishing the morphology and dynamics of other organelles will be characterized. These results will enhance the fundamental understanding of how plant cells organize into a defined set of independent organelles.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2018 
Task Progress: This last year was focused on submission and multiple revisions of a major publication that is now available in PNAS (Brillada et al. 2018). Dr. Brillada developed new fluorescent protein fusions to HOPS subunits VPS41 and VPS33 that had better signal and complemented mutant phenotypes. This was achieved using a GoldenBraid approach. In addition, she improved protocols for VPS33 and VPS41 protein expression in bacteria and assayed their binding to liposomes in vitro. This allowed her to conclude that VPS33 and VPS41 binding to phosphoinositides is regulated by the curvature of the cellular membranes.

The Principal Investigator and Dr. Brillada also generated new double marker lines for quantitative co-localization of these proteins with other endomembrane markers.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2017 
Task Progress: We finalized a revision for Alvarez et al. (2016) manuscript, and this was accepted in October 2017 in the Journal of Experimental Botany. This publication described the consequences of the fragmented vacuole of zig1 for amyloplast movement, cytoskeleton organization, and ER (endoplasmic reticulum) in membrane dynamics. A major conclusion of this work was that amyloplasts in zig1 are extremely static and even resist sedimentation after a force of 10g. We hypothesized that this characteristic of zig1 mutants was due to the physical tethering of amyloplasts to vacuoles, which we proposed is indirect and involves physical associations between both amyloplasts and vacuoles with the ER. In fact, we showed that neither actin nor microtubule cytoskeleton were involved in this tethering. This last year we have focused on testing this hypothesis using forward and reverse genetic approaches. In addition, we have continued to characterize the interaction between VTI11 SNARE and the HOPS complex and a manuscript that describes this work has been finalized.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2016 
Task Progress: We visualized the tonoplast and the ER (endoplasmic reticulum) in endodermal cells using endodermis specific organelle markers, and determined that the association of amyloplasts with vacuoles is abnormal in vti11 alleles.

We also used mutants carrying fluorescent markers that label the cytoskeleton and determined that the vacuole is critical for the localization of both actin and microtubule cytoskeletal structures, as well as the ER.

In collaboration with the laboratory of Simon Gilroy, we showed that even exposure to a 10 g force cannot displace amyloplasts in itt3/vti11 seedlings. We hypothesized that this is the result of a tight association or tethering of amyloplasts to the vacuolar membranes, and this association is probably indirect.

Trans-vacuolar strands (TVS) have been proposed as conduits for amyloplast sedimentation in shoots. Given that itt3/vti11 mutants have very few or no TVS, we wanted to test if this was important for their agravitropic phenotype. Our results indicate that Wortmannin treatment not only promotes vacuole fusion, but has a positive effect on vacuole membrane dynamics as well. These results suggest that Wm-induced vacuole fusion restores the ability of itt3/vti11 to form TVS which could become conduits for amyloplast sedimentation.

Jiameng Zheng, a graduate student supported by this grant has continued the characterization of a member of the HOPS complex (for HOmotypic fusion and Protein Sorting) in Arabidopsis. The HOPS complex is a good candidate for a role in gravity sensing because zip2, a mutant allele of the HOPS subunit VPS41, is a suppressor of zig-1 for gravitropism. Jiameng has used reverse genetics and fluorescent protein fusion experiments to identify the functions of HOPS as well as its localization in plant cells. Other experiments that we have carried out identified the affinity of HOPS for phosphoinositides and its ability to bind SNAREs.

We have made good progress on this project during the third year of the award. The focus this last year was to finalize all experiments for a publication. We have submitted the following manuscript (under review): Alvarez A, Han SW, Toyota M, Brillada C, Zheng J, Gilroy S, and Rojas-Pierce M. Wortmannin induced vacuole fusion enhances amyloplast dynamics in Arabidopsis zigzag1 hypocotyls and implicates tonoplast in the gravitropic response. Submitted.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2015 
Task Progress: Personnel

Jiameng Zheng is a fifth year graduate student. Jiameng was involved in the identification and characterization of vps41 loss of function alleles, generation of many of the clones for protein expression and localization in plants, and for protein-protein interaction analysis. She is the first author in a small publication where we showed that the itt3 vacuoles are independent organelles. In addition to conducting research, Jiameng presented a poster and gave a talk at the Mid West Cell Biology meeting in May 2014.

One postdoctoral scholar, Dr. Ashley Alvarez, has been involved in gravity perception studies in Arabidopsis. She has used the vertical microscope to quantify amyloplast sedimentation in itt3 and zig-1 mutants under different conditions. She has also analyzed organelle phenotypes in these mutants that result from abnormal vacuoles, and generated a suite of markers for visualizing cellular organelles in the shoot endodermis of Arabidopsis.

Two undergraduate students were involved in this research during the reporting year. Kimberly Baker assisted with technical support and gained some experience with molecular biology. Kimberly graduated in May 2014 with a B.Sc. in Plant Biology. Jennifer Kalinowski joined the lab in Fall 2014 and is a student in the Plant Biology program as well. She is continuing to get research experience in the laboratory.

Research progress

The specific aims of this research project are to:

1) Determine subcellular phenotypes that impair gravity perception in itt3/vti11.

2) Identify the role of phosphoinositides in gravitropism.

3) Determine the role of the Adaptor Protein (AP-3) complex on vacuole biogenesis and gravitropism.

We have made good progress on this project during the second year of the award:

a) We published an article that demonstrates that the vacuoles of vti11 mutants are independent organelles. Jiameng Zheng, a graduate student supported on this grant since Fall 2013, is the first author in this publication.

b) We have introgressed a series of subcellular organelles into vti11 alleles to determine the effect of the fragmented vacuole phenotype on the organization and function of other cellular compartments or structures. We have determined that the morphology of the vacuole has dramatic consequences for cellular organization, but this effect is restricted to a subset of cellular compartments.

c) We have used our unique vertical stage microscope for live-cell imaging of amyloplasts in wild type and in vit11 mutant alleles. We have determined that, similar to the inflorescence stem, sedimentation of amyloplasts from vti11 mutant hypocotyls is severely restricted. Treatment with Wortmannin resulted in a significant increase in amyloplast sedimentation in these cells as a result of vacuole fusion. Therefore, we conclude that the fragmented vacuole phenotype of vti11 mutants is the cause of their agravitropic phenotype, and that mechanisms of vacuole dynamics may regulate gravi-perception.

d) The endodermis was previously identified as the cell layer responsible for gravi-perception in shoots. As part of this work, we determined that the entire endodermis along Arabidopsis hypocotyls does not display amyloplast sedimentation and therefore could not be gravi-perceptive. We have identified specific regions of the hypocotyl endodermis that are responsible for the perception of gravity signals. Our work is now focused on these cells of the hypocotyl.

A new postdoc, Dr. Ashley Alvarez, was hired on February 1st. Nonetheless, significant progress has been made during the first year:

a) We completed the submission of an article describing the vacuole phenotype of itt3 mutants and the possible role of vacuole fusion in gravity response. Jiameng Zheng, a graduate student supported on this grant since Fall 2013, is the first author in this publication.

b) We have generated expression constructs for the visualization of subcellular markers in the shoot endodermis, and have started to introduce those into itt3 mutants.

c) We have set up the vertical stage microscope for live-cell imaging of vacuoles and amyloplasts in wild type and in itt3 mutants during vacuole fusion experiments and during gravi-stimulation. This system allows us to image Arabidopsis seedlings in time-lapse experiments using a water objective with high numerical aperture.

d) Started to identify the effects of vacuole fragmentation in itt3 on other subcellular organelles.

e) We have acquired mutants for the AP-3 complex and initiated experiments to visualize their vacuole morphology. We have initiated crosses of several of these mutants to the vti11 alleles to determine if these two complexes genetically interact to control the morphology of the vacuole.

Outreach:

We hosted 15 students from a local Middle School in Wake County to expose them to research in plant biology. One of the activities that they did was comparing the vacuole morphology between wild type and itt3 mutants. Using these tools, we discussed the role of the vacuole in plant gravity response.

Task Description: The long-term goal of this research is to identify and characterize mechanisms of plant vacuole biogenesis that are critical for gravity perception. A role of the vacuole in controlling gravity perception is well accepted, but the molecular mechanisms by which the vacuole interacts with amyloplasts or regulates their sedimentation during gravitropism are yet to be identified. We recently uncovered a novel vacuole phenotype in agravitropic mutant alleles of VTI11 in Arabidopsis. We predict that this vacuole phenotype of vti11 mutants have negative effects on the organization of actin cables or the cortical endoplasmic reticulum, and therefore prevent amyloplast sedimentation. In addition, we have uncovered a novel regulation of vacuolar membrane fusion by phosphoinositides. Based on our results, we developed the hypothesis that mechanisms of vacuole remodeling that are mediated by phosphoinositide signaling are critical for the perception or transduction of the gravitropic signal in plants. A unique assay for vacuole fusion that we developed will be used to determine the role of specific cellular components in membrane fusion and gravity perception. The specific aims of this research project are to: 1) Determine subcellular phenotypes that impair gravity perception in itt3/vti11. 2) Identify the role of phosphoinositides in gravitropism. 3) Determine the role of the Adaptor Protein (AP-3) complex on vacuole biogenesis and gravitropism. Overall, this research will improve our understanding of vacuole biogenesis, the interactions between the vacuole and other cellular components involved in gravity perception, and the role of phosphoinosidides in gravitropism.
