Task Description: The in-flight video recording capabilities planned for the Bion-M1 Project make possible a new generation of science on unmanned satellites. The present proposal is to analyze rodent behavior across the one-month duration of the orbital flight of Bion-M1. The specific, testable hypotheses for Bion-M1 include microgravity effects on the kinds and amounts of behavior that rodents will express. General activity and its circadian pattern will be examined, and specific functional categories of individual and group social behaviors will be characterized and quantified. Kinematic measures of limb and trunk movements will also be taken.

Modification of individual and group behavior can alter immune, endocrine, and physiological function. By integrating behavioral data with those from the same animals studied by various primary and secondary study PIs, it will be possible to test alternative explanations for alterations in immune, musculoskeletal, neural, and endocrine changes with spaceflight.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: The planned scientific studies for Bion M-1 are primarily in the realm of basic, "fundamental biology," but the studies will be pioneering in many ways and are capable of spawning exciting Earth benefits, mostly in the realm of insights into fundamental biological processes related to immune function, muscle and bone integrity, and adaptation of the nervous system.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2015 
Task Progress: [Ed. note: compiled in July 2019 from PI final report submitted to the NASA Shared Services Center in November 2015 ; received in July 2019 by NASA Ames and then to Task Book]

The Bion M-1 mission presented an exciting opportunity for studying adult mice during a relatively long-duration (one month) exposure to spaceflight conditions and immediately upon recovery on Earth. The design of this Russian flight experiment was based on group-housed male mice (three adult male, C57/Bl6 mice per compartment). Some of the mice were equipped with implanted telemetry equipment that provided heart rate and blood pressure data. Significantly for the present report, the Bion compartments provided on-board video surveillance of the mice, with significant volume of daily storage. Upon landing, the present effort included post-flight behavioral testing of the mice.

Conventional wisdom has long held that unrelated adult male mice cannot be housed together without competition over resources such as space and access to resources, pathological stress, and fighting. NASA has incorporated such concerns into their life sciences operations, thus limiting the use of this valuable mammalian model for flight-based investigations. Bion M-1 procedures and results contributed an important alteration to the conventional wisdom, and the video data and direct observations that comprised the present investigations provide valuable information.

We learned that the Russian scientists employed several innovative, yet simple techniques to produce triads of unrelated adult male mice that lived amicably in the small housing compartments for one month. This was accomplished by establishing, in their laboratory vivarium, triads of adult males. They were housed in a standard cage and provided a shelter apparatus to share. Mice that did not share the shelter were thus screen and eliminated from the pool. There followed a regime of daily handling. These animals were also maintained at about 90% ad lib body weight for purposes of operant training. These procedures yielded groups of adult males that lived together amicably in the Bion habitats in microgravity and under Earth-based control conditions. Their excellent performance violated the conventional wisdom and provides an important example of how we might well use group-housed male mice in future studies.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2014 
Task Progress: The Bion-M1 mission was large, long and complex. We assembled a library of video recordings from the two habitat assemblies consisting of 2,476, 30-min segments of individual habitats, captured during both light and dark phases of each day of the mission. We reviewed the entire body of video recordings and classified quality of each segment. As might be expected, video quality was superior in the early phases of the flight and visibility was compromised over time, as floating liquids and debris tended to obscure the camera lens and the general atmosphere of the habitats. More specifically, at the beginning of first week of spaceflight we categorized as clear with maximal visibility 76% of the video segments. There was a precipitous decrease in visibility at the beginning of the ensuing weeks: the proportion of such clear segments was only 3% and 6% for the 2nd and 3rd weeks, respectively. On the first day of the final week none (0%) of the habitats were clearly visible in the video records. One of our initial efforts was to help better understand and interpret the fatalities and injuries sustained by some of the mice in the in the two functioning habitat assemblies.

For comparison, we applied the same criteria to the Ground Control habitats for the same days during the simulated flight. In all, these video samples documented from the first day of experimentation, both in microgravity and on Earth, that male mice aggregated non-randomly at the feeder wall of the cylindrical habitat. We have also made significant progress analyzing video data collected during post-flight testing of the mice.

I am interacting with my Russian collaborator, Dr. Alexander Andreev-Andrievskly, on the analysis and interpretation of other results from post-flight testing and some additional write-ups for publications. To date, one manuscript has been completed and submitted for publication:

Andreev-Andrievskly, A., Popova, A., Boyle, R., Alberts, J., Shenkman, B., Vinogradova, O., Dolgov, O., Anokhin, K., Tsvirkun, D., Soldatov, P., Nemirovskaya, T., Ilyn, E., Sychev, V. Mice in Bion-M1 space mission: Training and selection. PLoS, in press, 2014.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2012 
Task Progress: [Editor's note 8/16/2012: No Task Book report received. Progress section and Bibliography compiled from PI's Annual Technical Report dated July 2012]

The project, ‘Individual and group behavior of Bion-M1 Rodents,’ has advanced since the last report, although progress has been modest, mainly because the launch date for the Bion flight has slipped from approximately 9/2012 to 4/2013. This was determined just last month (June) during a recent visit of NASA-Ames personnel to the Institute for Biomedical Problems (IMBP), in Moscow.

I plan to visit NASA ARC in July 2012 to meet with both Richard Boyle and Paula Dumars. At that time I expect to be able to share the results of correspondence with Russian scientists and hone the preparations for a successful flight experiment.

It is worth reiterating that the Bion-M1 project is fascinating and valuable because it is a relatively long spaceflight exposure (30-days) for mammals (gerbils and mice). In particular, video data from the mice -- the focus of my proposed effort – ought to contribute significantly to nascent plans for ISS research and could enhance the science of many other researchers in the short term.”

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2010 
Task Progress: NASA-Ames Research Center is continuing its tradition of collaborating with the Institute for Biomedical Problems (IMBP) to conduct orbital spaceflight experiments that are designed to address fundamental problems in gravitational and space biology. The Bion M-1 flight, presently scheduled for 2012, is particularly exciting, for the plan is to maintain the specimens in orbital spaceflight for about one month. As a participating PI for the Bion M-1 mission, I participated in a NASA delegation to Institute of Biomedical Problems (IMBP), in Moscow, where we met and conducted focused discussions of experiment aims, potential methods, data collection, and interpretations. A very important outcome of that trip was to have face-to-face meetings with some of the Russian PIs and benefit from direct exchanges of information - the kinds of discussions that cannot occur easily without direct contact. We have been able to follow-up with some email exchanges since.

Prior to and after the IMBP visit, I have conducted a few feasibility trials in my laboratory, in order to answer some questions that arose in the preliminary considerations of the flight experiments. At this early stage of planning, it appears that the Russians may invite my involvement in studies of group-living (9-12) gerbils (Meriones unguliculatus) and trios of mice (Mus musculus). In both cases, my contribution is likely to involve detailed analyses of social interactions, which is useful for interpreting physiological and anatomical results and integrating across other investigations. These preliminary trials were aimed at determining whether we could employ long-lasting identification markers on the animals. Preliminary results were positive: We can maintain individual identifiers for the anticipated flight durations.
