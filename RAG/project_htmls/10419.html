Flight Assignment/Project Notes: NOTE: Extended to 10/01/2017 (9/30/17) per NSSC information (Ed., 9/8/16)

NOTE: Extended to 10/31/2016 per NSSC information (Ed., 9/10/15)

Task Description: The amount of bone loss between individual astronauts is highly variable with some astronauts losing large amounts of bone while others are largely unaffected. This variability is accounted for, at least to a large extent, by genetic variations between individuals. Unfortunately, the specific genes that modulate this genetic sensitivity of bone loss in microgravity are unknown. To this end, we have recently completed a genetic study in which we identified specific regions on chromosomes (QTL or Quantitative Trait Loci) that either add to or reduce the propensity of an individual to lose bone when normal weightbearing is removed from the musculoskeleton. While providing critical information, these QTL still contain hundreds of genes, rather than a single one. Here, we propose first to greatly reduce the number of candidate genes with bioinformatics tools and then experimentally test the shortlist of candidate genes using knockout mice available from the Jackson Laboratory’s (JAX) Genetic Resource Repository and the JAX Knock Out Mouse Project (KOMP) production center. The identification of single genes modulating bone loss during weightlessness can be used as a critical diagnostic tool for the identification of astronauts most in need of countermeasures as well as to unravel novel drug targets that preserve bone health in space.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: This proposal directly addresses one of the six highest priority recommendations made in the National Research Council’s 2011 Decadal Survey Report, “Recapturing a Future for Space Exploration: Life and Physical Sciences Research for a New Era” ( http://www.nap.edu/catalog/13048.html ), namely recommendation AH3: “Bone loss studies of genetically altered mice exposed to weightlessness are strongly recommended.” Defining some of the specific genes that cause differences in the skeletal sensitivity to weightlessness will not only provide novel diagnostic and therapeutic means of combating bone loss in space but also on Earth in individuals subjected to bedrest or suffering from paralysis.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2018 
Task Progress: In this final no-cost extension period, we attempted to resolve the hurdles that came with breeding tissue-specific LRP4 transgenic mice. While we solved the issues that had us held back, we could not re-start the breeding protocol because funds had been depleted.

During the entire funding period, we made significant progress towards the goal of identifying specific genes and pathways that determine the amount of unloading-induced bone loss based on an individual’s genetic make-up. We also explored how aging may influence this genetic relationship. Our principal manuscripts resulting from this work can be seen in the Cumulative Bibliography link below.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2017 
Task Progress: Year Two and Year Three progress reports discussed microcomputed tomography indices from CD44, FGF2, ESR1, ESR2, FAS, and NOD2 genes. Here, we report our results from Pak 6 (p21 activated kinase 6) mice. We also report osteoblastic and osteoclastic activity in ESR1, ESR2, and Pak6 null mice.

Physiological levels of estrogen are critical for maintaining bone health. This is evident from extensive bone deterioration observed in women with low circulating estrogen post menopause or after surgical removal of ovaries [1, 2]. Estrogen is a steroidal hormone that exerts most of its bone specific actions via its interaction with its receptors (estrogen receptors, ESR; ESR1 and ESR2) [3]. We previously reported that ESR1-/- mice had greater trabecular and cortical microcomputed tomography indices at baseline and were protected from the catabolic effects of hindlimb unloading (HLU). ESR2 -/- mice had greater trabecular and cortical indices at baseline and were more susceptible to hindlimb unloading losses than wildtype (WT) C57BL/6J mice. Here we report that the resistance to HLU in ESR1 -/- mice were due to lower suppression of osteoblastic number and surface during unloading, while ESR2 -/- mice were more susceptible to losses because of greater suppression of osteoblastic number and surface during unloading when compared to their WT counterparts. Pak6 (p21 activated kinase 6) gene has been known to interact with ESR1 and suppress the transcriptional activity of ESR1 [4, 5]. Therefore, we hypothesized that Pak6 null mice will exhibit lower susceptibility to unloading similar to the response exhibited by ESR1 -/- mice.

To test the function of ESR1, ESR2, and Pak6 genes during mechanical unloading, we divided ESR1 KO (ESR1-/-, n=36), ESR2 KO (ESR2 -/-, n=20), and Pak6 KO (n=20) into baseline control (BC), age-matched control (AC), and hindlimb unloaded (HLU) groups. All KO mice were backcrossed to C57BL/6J (B6J) mice for at least 8 generations. Therefore, B6J mice were used as wildtype (WT) controls. At the end of the experiment, the mice were sacrificed and femurs were harvested and fixed in 10% neutral buffered formalin and then stored in 70% ethanol for ex vivo microcomputed tomography scanning (µCT 40, Scanco Medical, SUI) and histology. Ex vivo µCT scanning was performed on the distal femoral metaphysis to quantify trabecular and cortical microarchitecture at a 10 µm resolution. After microcomputed tomography scanning, femora were decalcified in 14% EDTA (ethylene diamine tetra acetic acid) and the bones were embedded in paraffin, and at least one 5µm section per bone was stained with either toluidine blue or tartrate resistant acid phosphatase (TRAP). In a region similar to the one analyzed with µCT, osteoblast surface per bone surface (Ob.S/BS, %) and osteoblast number per bone surface (Ob.N/BS) were quantified using toluidine blue stain, while TRAP stain quantified osteoclast surface to bone surface (Oc.S/BS %). In cortical bone, these osteoblastic and osteoclastic indices were quantified and normalized separately either to the endocortical or periosteal perimeter (Ec.Pm/Pe.Pm). Ob.S/Ec.Pm, Ob.N/ Ec.Pm, Oc. S/ Ec.Pm, Ob.S/Pe. Pm, Ob.N/ Pe.Pm, and Oc. S/ Pe.Pm were measured using Osteomeasure (Osteometrics, Inc., Decatur, GA, USA). The effect of gene deletion was tested by comparing age matched control KO mice to WT mice using two-tailed, independent samples T tests. The impact of HLU within a given genetic strain was tested for significance via one-way ANOVA and Tukey post-hoc test. Differences in magnitude of bone deterioration between WT and KO mice were tested by two-way ANOVA.

We had previously reported that 11 wk old age-matched ESR1-/- mice and ESR2-/- mice had greater trabecular and cortical µCT indices than WT control. Further, we also reported that trabecular bone of HLU ESR1-/- mice were resistant to the catabolic effects with no differences in bone volume fraction and minimal responses in trabecular thickness (Tb.Th) while trabecular bone of ESR2 -/- were extremely susceptible to HLU with significantly greater losses than WT mice. Here we report that Pak6 -/- mice during normal cage ambulation, similar to ESR1 and ESR2 -/- mice had 31%, 7%, 9%, and 9% greater connectivity density (Conn.D), trabecular number (Tb.N), cortical area (Ct.Ar), and cortical thickness (Ct.Th) when compared to WT AC mice (all P<0.05). HLU WT mice had 31% lower BV/TV, 5% lower Tb.N, and 19% lower Tb.Th while HLU Pak6-/- mice had 32% and 18% lower BV/TV and Tb.th when compared to corresponding AC mice (all P<0.05). HLU Pak6 -/- mice had 16% and 10 % lower Ct.Ar and Ct.Th, while WT mice had 15% lower Ct.Ar and 7% lower Ct.Th when compared to corresponding AC mice. However, the HLU response of the Pak6 KO mice was statistically not different from WT mice in both trabecular and cortical compartments.

ESR1, ESR2, and Pak6 null mice had lower bone resorptive TRAP activity (Oc.Pm/B.Pm) in trabecular bone. However, ESR1 -/- (3%) and Pak6 -/- (9%) mice had greater osteoclastic activity than WT mice (2%) on the endocortical surface of the cortical bone (all P<0.05). Periosteal TRAP activity on the other hand, was lower in ESR1-/- (0.6%) when compared to WT (1.6%) mice (P<0.05). Pak6 -/- mice had 22%, and 40% lower osteoblastic counts on the trabecular and on the periosteal cortical surface (P<0.05). In ESR2-/- mice, osteoblastic activity on trabecular, endocortical, and periosteal surfaces were not different from WT mice. These data suggest that under physiological conditions, the greater trabecular and cortical indices in ESR1-/-, ESR2 -/-, and Pak6 -/- mice were primarily due to lower osteoclastic activity. The differences in HLU trabecular response of ESR2 -/- mice were due to greater suppression of Ob.N/BS (56% vs 30%) and Ob.S/BS (58% vs 40%) when comparing unloaded mice to AC groups in ESR2 -/- mice and WT mice (Gene*HLU, P<0.05). Resistance to HLU observed in ESR1 -/- mice was due to lack of suppression of osteoblastic indices in this KO strain (nsd) when subjected to hindlimb unloading. HLU Pak6 -/- mice had 47% and 50% lower Ob.N/BS and Ob.S/BS when compared to corresponding AC mice (all P<0.05) but the response was not different from WT mice. These results suggest that the trabecular differences in HLU responses between ESR1 -/-, ESR2 -/-, and WT mice are mediated largely by differences in osteoblastic activity rather than osteoclastic activity. In cortical bone, HLU did not affect osteoblastic number and activity on the endocortical or periosteal surface of KO mice. In WT mice, Ob.N, and Ob.S were 30% and 41% lower in HLU when compared to corresponding AC mice (all P<0.05), while no differences were observed on the periosteal surface. No differences in osteoclastic activity was observed on the endocortical surface of both KO and WT mice. On the periosteal surface, no differences in osteoclastic activity were detected between HLU and AC ESR1-/- mice but HLU WT B6J mice had 300% greater osteoclastic activity than AC mice (Gene*HLU, P<0.05). Osteoclastic activity of HLU ESR2 -/- and Pak6 -/- mice was not different from WT mice on the periosteal surface.

Taken together, these results highlight the importance of ESR1, ESR2, and Pak6 genes in skeletal function during physiological and hindlimb unloading conditions. Further, the results suggest that the greater susceptibility and resistance to HLU in ESR2 -/- and ESR1 -/- mice was largely due to greater/lower suppression of osteoblastic indices rather than changes in osteoclastic activity.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2016 
Task Progress: Normal circulatory levels of estrogen are critical for maintaining bone health. Estrogen depletion post menopause or after surgery is detrimental to bone [1, 2]. Estrogen signals via two nuclear receptors: ESR1 and ESR2. ESR2 null mice reportedly overexpress ESR1 and core binding factor alpha 1 (cbfa1), a protein that promotes osteoblastic cell differentiation [3, 4]. Female ESR2 KO mice exhibit enhanced cortical bone sensitivity to mechanical signals [5]. We hypothesized that ESR null mice will exhibit an enhanced sensitivity to the removal of mechanical signals, leading to more pronounced losses in cortical bone when compared to wild type (WT) mice. Fas, a cell surface death receptor is expressed both in osteoblasts and osteoclasts [6, 7]. Fas deficiency protects mice from ovariectomy induced bone loss [7]. Hence, we hypothesized that Fas null mice will be spared from hindlimb unloading induced bone deterioration. Because some of the KO strains were bred on a B6N rather than a B6J background, we recently showed that even subtle genetic variations between C57BL/6J (B6J) and C57BL/6N (B6N) mice can alter bone morphology and response to hindlimb unloading. As detailed below, here, we report that these variations are due to differences in dynamic indices of bone formation, rather than bone resorption.

To test the skeletal functions of ESR2 and Fas, ESR2 KO (ESR2-/-, n=20) and Fas KO (Fas-/-, n=20) were assigned to baseline control, age matched control, and hindlimb unloaded (HLU) groups. C57BL/6J (B6J, n=34) and C3H/HeJ (C3H, n=34) mice, the background strain of the ESR2 and Fas Kos respectively, were used as wild type (WT) controls. At the end of the experiment, the mice were sacrificed and femurs were harvested, fixed, and stored in 70% ethanol. The distal femoral metaphysis was subjected to microcomputed tomography (µCT) scanning and indices describing trabecular and cortical bone were quantified. The effect of gene deletion was tested by comparing age matched control KO mice to WT mice using two-tailed, independent samples T tests. The impact of HLU within a given genetic strain was tested for significance via one-way ANOVA and Tukey post-hoc test. Differences in magnitude of bone deterioration between WT and KO mice were tested by two-way ANOVA.

ESR2 null mice had 17% greater bone volume and 7% more trabecular struts while trabecular thickness was 4% lower than WT-B6J mice at 11 weeks. ESR2 null mice had 11% greater cortical area but cortical thickness was similar to WT mice at 11 weeks (all P<0.05). ESR2 null mice were more susceptible to hindlimb unloading induced bone loss with 56%, 14%, and 27% losses in bone volume fraction (BV/TV), trabecular number (Tb.N), and trabecular thickness, while WT-B6J mice exhibited only 31%, 5%, and 19% lower BV/TV, Tb.N, and Tb.Th following HLU (Gene*HLU, P<0.05). Cortical response to HLU was similar between ESR2 null and WT mice.

Fas deficiency did not alter trabecular or cortical bone morphology at 11 weeks of age. HLU Fas-/- mice had 49%, 21%, and 18% lower BV/TV, Tb.N, and Tb.Th when compared to their respective age matched control (all P<0.05) while WT-C3H mice had 53%, 26%, and 16% lower BV/TV, Tb.N, and Tb.Th (all P<0.05). However, no differences in the magnitude of the response were detected between Fas and WT-C3H mice for trabecular bone loss. Cortical bone responses were also similar between Fas and WT mice.

We recently reported that trabecular architecture of B6N mice was more susceptible to HLU than in B6J mice. Consistent with this data we found that HLU B6N mice have a 29% and 21% lower bone formation rate (BFR/BS) and mineralizing surface (MS/BS) when compared to baseline control mice. In contrast, HLU did not suppress bone formation indices beyond the levels of baseline control in the B6J substrain (Gene*HLU, P<0.05). Osteoblast surface and osteoblast number were 59% and 51% lower in HLU B6N mice when compared to baseline control, while in the B6J substrain, these differences amounted to only 26% and 19% (Gene*HLU, P<0.05). No differences in osteoclastic activity were observed between the two substrains.

These data highlight the role of ESR2 and Fas genes in maintaining bone health during physiological conditions and during reduced mechanical demand. Further, the differences that we observed between the two substrains, B6J and B6N, emphasize that differential levels of bone formation may have to be taken into account when using B6J and B6N.

Task Description: The amount of bone loss between individual astronauts is highly variable with some astronauts losing large amounts of bone while others are largely unaffected. This variability is accounted for, at least to a large extent, by genetic variations between individuals. Unfortunately, the specific genes that modulate this genetic sensitivity of bone loss in microgravity are unknown. To this end, we have recently completed a genetic study in which we identified specific regions on chromosomes (QTL or Quantitative Trait Loci) that either add to or reduce the propensity of an individual to lose bone when normal weightbearing is removed from the musculoskeleton. While providing critical information, these QTL still contain hundreds of genes, rather than a single one. Here, we propose first to greatly reduce the number of candidate genes with bioinformatics tools and then experimentally test the shortlist of candidate genes using knockout mice available from the Jackson Laboratory’s (JAX) Genetic Resource Repository and the JAX Knock Out Mouse Project (KOMP) production center. The identification of single genes modulating bone loss during weightlessness can be used as a critical diagnostic tool for the identification of astronauts most in need of countermeasures as well as to unravel novel drug targets that preserve bone health in space.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: This proposal directly addresses one of the six highest priority recommendations made in the National Research Council’s 2011 Decadal Survey Report, “Recapturing a Future for Space Exploration: Life and Physical Sciences Research for a New Era” ( http://www.nap.edu/catalog/13048.html ), namely recommendation AH3: “Bone loss studies of genetically altered mice exposed to weightlessness are strongly recommended.” Defining some of the specific genes that cause differences in the skeletal sensitivity to weightlessness will not only provide novel diagnostic and therapeutic means of combating bone loss in space but also on Earth in individuals subjected to bedrest or suffering from paralysis.

Progress of Specific Aim 2

Introduction

Specific Aim 1 provided a short of list of candidate genes that may modulate the magnitude of bone’s response to unloading and reambulation. Over the past 12 months, we continued to test specific transgenic mouse models for their degree of bone’s susceptibility to unloading. Recent targets were fibroblast growth factor 2 (FGF-2), estrogen receptor 1 (ESR-1), and nucleotide binding oligomerization domain containing protein 2 (NOD-2).

Previous in vitro studies indicated a role for FGF2 in proliferation and differentiation of cells (1). Specifically, FGF2 is expressed in osteoblasts and alteration in expression levels of FGF2 leads to an imbalance in bone remodeling. For instance, overexpression of FGF2 stunted growth and induced chondrodysplasia (2, 3). Further, administration of fibroblast growth factor can be anabolic in ovariectomized rats while parathyroid hormone administration lost its efficacy in the absence of FGF2 (4, 5). We therefore hypothesized that impaired bone remodeling following loss of FGF2 function will be further exacerbated during disuse.

Another one of our candidate genes, estrogen receptor 1 (ESR1) is the primary receptor for estrogen. Bone loss after menopause or surgical removal of ovaries has been well established (6). Moreover, bone’s anabolic response to mechanical stimuli is reduced when estrogen signaling though ESR1 is blocked (7). We hypothesized that bone’s diminished response to mechanical signals would lead to an impaired response to the loss of mechanical signals, resulting in less bone loss during unloading (8). NOD2 is an intracellular protein associated with inflammatory and immune processes and responses (9). Microgravity alters the B/T cell ratio, and may cause an impaired immune response (10). To determine whether these variations in other systems contribute to losses in the musculoskeletal system during disuse, we employed NOD2 deficient mice.

While contributions of single genes are pursued by employing genetic KO, the effects of small variations of the genome on bone’s response to disuse are yet to be explored. WT-B6J and C57BL/6N (WT-B6N) are sub-strains of B6 mice that exhibit slight variations in their genome including 34 single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNP) and 2 insertion-deletion (INDELS) in coding regions and 15 gene linked chromosomal structural variations (SV).(11) We recently identified one SNP and one SV as potential modulators of bone’s sensitivity to unloading. We therefore hypothesized that B6J and B6N will respond differently to hindlimb unloading (HLU).

Methods and Results

To determine the role of FGF2 and ESR1, 36 FGF2-/- (STOCK Fgf2tm2Doe/J), 36 ESR1-/- (B6.129P2-Esr1tm1Ksk/J), 20 NOD2-/- mice (B6.129S1-Nod2tm1Flv/J) null mice were divided into baseline controls, sacrificed at 9 weeks of age to quantify baseline phenotype (BC, n=4), age matched controls, sacrificed at 11 weeks to determine ambulatory cage control phenotype (AC, n=16 for ESR1 and FGF2 and n=8 for NOD-2) and hindlimb unloaded groups, sacrificed at 11 weeks after 14 days of hindlimb unloading (HLU, n=16 for ESR1 and FGF2 and n=8 for NOD-2). These mice were compared to wild type C57BL/6J (WT-B6J, n=34) that were divided into the same three groups described above (AC, HLU, n=12 and BC, n=10). Thirty-four, 9 week old female B6N mice were divided into groups similar to B6J mice.

Bone from the distal metaphysis was analyzed via microCT scanning (Scanco µCT 40) at 10µm resolution. Bone indices within the same genotype were tested for statistical significance using one way ANOVA and Tukey post-hoc tests. Differences in baseline bone morphology (KO vs WT) were tested for significance using two-tailed, independent samples t-tests. Gene x Treatment (hindlimb unloading) interactions were analyzed using two-way ANOVA.

At baseline, FGF2-/- mice had a 14% lower trabecular number (Tb.N) and 17% greater Tb.Th when compared to wildtype B6J mice (P<0.05). All other trabecular architectural indices remained similar. However, these micro-architectural differences were abolished as the mice aged (at 11 weeks). Following 2 weeks of hindlimb unloading, the response of the trabecular compartment of the distal metaphysis was similar between FGF2 null mice and WT-B6J mice.

At baseline, ESR1-/- mice had a 43% greater trabecular bone volume fraction (BV/TV), 32% greater trabecular number (Tb.N), and trabecular spacing (Tb.Sp) was 25% lower when compared to wildtype B6J mice (P<0.05). Similar differences were seen in all other trabecular architectural indices except trabecular thickness (Tb.Th) which did not change. These differences in micro-architectural differences persisted as the mice aged (at 11 weeks). Following 2 weeks of hindlimb unloading, the response of the trabecular compartment of the distal metaphysis was severely stunted in ESR1 null mice with just a 8% loss in Tb.Th, while the WT-B6J mice had 19% lower thickness compared to age matched controls (Gene x Treatment, P<0.01). Similar trends were seen in losses of BV/TV, and Tb.N between ESR1-/- and WT-B6J mice (12 % vs 32% and 2% vs 5%). However these differences did not reach statistical significance.

At baseline, NOD2-/- mice had 35% lower trabecular BV/TV, 14% lower Tb.N, and Tb. Sp was 17% greater (all P<0.01). Similar trends persisted as mice aged. Following hindlimb unloading, the degree and pattern of loss in trabeculae of the distal femoral compartment was similar between NOD2-/- mice and WT-B6J mice.

In the metaphysis, at baseline, B6N mice had 21% lower bone volume (BV), bone volume fraction and 6% lower trabecular number and thickness when compared to B6J mice (all P<0.05) while tissue volume (TV) and connectivity density (Conn.D) were similar between strains. Upon HLU, tissue losses did not significantly differ between B6J and B6N in the trabecular metaphysis when compared to respective age matched control mice.

Discussion

Together, these data emphasize the importance of FGF2, ESR1, and NOD2 in maintaining trabecular architecture under physiological loading conditions. Results also suggest that ESR1 plays a large role in modulating the sensitivity of the trabecular compartment to the lack os mechanical signals). The contribution of FGF2 and NOD2 towards influencing bone’s response to disuse was mostly insignificant. Finally, this data highlights the importance of subtle variations in the genome (B6J vs B6N) in regulating complex bone morphology and microarchitecture.

Currently, we are pursuing the next three target genes (ESR2, PAK6 and FAS) on our shortlist.

Task Progress: Progress of Specific Aim 1

Specific Aim 1 has been largely completed. Using methods described further in the grant proposal, we performed extensive bioinformatics analyses to reduce the number of candidate genes controlling changes in trabecular BV/TV during unloading from more than 1000 to 25. Briefly, genes with clear evidence that they are not related to bone were removed. Single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) that are harbored in synonymous coding regions were also removed. Genes with multiple SNPs were integrated. ConceptGen (NCIBI) with data from the Panther Pathway, Kegg Pathway, Biocarta Pathway, Gene Ontology, and MeSH was then employed to filter genes with putative functions in Wnt, Wnt-Receptor, Wnt-Protein Interactions, B-Catenin, Actin Cytoskeleton, NF-KB, TGF-B, ESR, BMP-BMPR, osteoblast, bone formation, bone resorption, and bone remodeling pathways. Inherently, this search was not exhaustive but it is likely that true regulators are among the current 25 candidate genes, particular because our gene list was also vetted against microarray data from in vitro and in vivo unloading experiments.

Progress of Specific Aim 2

Specific Aim 1 one provided a short of list of candidate genes that may modulate the magnitude of bone’s response to unloading and reambulation. Our first target was CD44. In vitro studies indicate that the protein produced by this gene is associated with ‘cell-cell’ and ‘cell-extracellular’ matrix interactions (1). CD44 has also been reported in the context of osteoclast fusion and formation of multinucleated cells (2, 3). Despite the association of CD44 with osteoclast fusion, no high bone mass phenotype has been observed in CD44 KO mice (2, 4). Substrate specific CD44 roles has been suggested (2). To determine the role of CD44 in unloading induced bone loss, 26 9wk old, female B6.129 (Cg)-Cd44tm1Hbg/J (CD44-/-) and 34 C57Bl/6J (B6J) mice were assigned to three groups – baseline controls, age-matched controls and hindlimb unloaded HLU). Baseline CD44-/- (n=6) and B6J (n=10) were sacrificed at 9wk of age to determine baseline phenotypes. CD44-/- (n=10 each) and B6J (n=12 each) were subjected to 2wk of HLU (CD44_HLU and B6J_HLU) or normal cage ambulation (CD44_C and B6J_C). Ex vivo µCT scanning was performed at 10µm resolution at the distal femoral metaphysis. A 1.5 mm region proximal to the distal femoral growth plate was chosen as region of interest. Bone indices within the same genotype were tested for statistical significance using one way ANOVA and Tukey post-hoc tests. Changes in baseline bone morphology were tested for significance using a two tailed, independent samples t-test. Gene x Treatment (hindlimb unloading) interactions were analyzed using two-way ANOVA. CD44-/- mice had a baseline bone phenotype compared to wild type B6J mice with a 38% lower bone volume fraction (BV/TV), a 17% smaller trabecular number (Tb.N) and a 14% lower trabecular thickness (Tb.Th) (all p< 0.005). These differences persisted 2wk later when age-matched controls were compared between the two strains (all p<0.001). Thus, no Age x Gene interaction was observed. Two weeks of hindlimb unloading caused rapid deterioration in trabecular quantity and architecture in CD44-/- mice with 44% less BV/TV, 13% lower Tb.N, and 21% lower Tb.Th compared to age matched controls of the same genotype. Bone loss in B6J mice due to HLU was comparable to CD44-/- mice with 33% less BV/TV, 6% lower Tb.N and 19% lower Tb.Th. Because of the only small differences in the skeletal response to HLU between CD44-/- and B6J, Gene x Unloading interactions were not significant. These data highlight the importance of CD44 in acquiring trabecular morphology under normal physiological loading conditions but the contribution of CD44 towards modulating bone response to unloading is, at least based on this preliminary analysis, insignificant. We are currently testing the next two candidate genes on our shortlist.

Task Description: A principal physiologic hurdle to man's extended presence in space is the osteopenia which parallels reduced gravity. On average, the extent of the loss is extremely high, approaching a decrease in bone mineral density (BMD) in the lower appendicular skeleton at a rate of 2% per month. Interestingly, the amount of bone loss between individual astronauts is highly variable with some astronauts losing large amounts of bone while others are largely unaffected. This variability is accounted for, at least to a large extent, by genetic variations between individuals, consistent with observations on bone loss in postmenopausal women and during aging. Unfortunately, the specific genes that modulate the magnitude of bone's response to weightlessness are currently unknown. To address this question, we recently completed a genetic study in mice bred for a large variability in their susceptibility to unloading induced bone loss. Like astronauts, some mice subjected to unloading lost very large amounts of bone, some lost very little, and many lost moderate amounts of skeletal tissue. Data from this study allowed us to identify the specific regions on the chromosomes (QTL or Quantitative Trait Loci) that either add or reduce the propensity of an individual to lose bone when normal weightbearing is removed from the musculoskeleton. Here, we propose to use bioinformatics tools to first reduce the number of candidate genes within each QTL interval and then to experimentally identify causative genes via knockout mice subjected to hindlimb unloading. This candidate gene approach has recently been made feasible through the generation of several thousand knockout mouse strains by the Genetic Resource Science Repository at the Jackson Laboratories and the KOMP (Knockout Mouse Project) Production Center. Approximately 24 candidate genes within the four strongest QTL will be validated by their ability to alter an individual's susceptibility to bone loss induced by weightlessness. This identification of single genes modulating bone loss during weightlessness can be used as a critical diagnostic tool (genetic sensor) for the identification of astronauts most in need of countermeasures as well as to unravel novel drug targets that can be targeted towards the preservation of bone's normal anabolic and catabolic activity in space.
