Flight Assignment/Project Notes: NOTE: Extended to 9/14/2016 per A. Chu/ARC and NSSC information (Ed., 7/16/15)

Task Description: Spaceflight, by impairing the differentiation program of stem cells residing within bone marrow results in bone loss, increased bone marrow adiposity, anemia, and impaired immune function. Because these disturbances may potentially compromise the success of long-duration flights, there exists an urgent need to identify the underlying mechanisms and implement effective countermeasures. We propose to test the novel hypothesis that negative energy balance during spaceflight results in 1) bone marrow dysfunction and increased marrow adiposity and 2) altered signaling by peripheral adipokines (cytokines produced by adipocytes) all of which, in turn, contribute to negative bone turnover balance and bone loss. The proposed research will focus on the precise role of adipose tissue and the important adipokine leptin in mediating the detrimental effects of simulated spaceflight (hindlimb unloading (HU)) on bone marrow cells in mice by accomplishing the following 2 specific aims: Specific Aim 1 will test the hypothesis that increased bone marrow adiposity plays a causal role in mediating simulated spaceflight-induced changes in bone metabolism. This will be accomplished by comparing the skeletal response to hindlimb unloading of wild type (WT) mice with mice incapable of generating bone marrow adipocytes (kit-receptor-deficient KitW/W-v mice). Specific Aim 2 will test the hypothesis that the adipokine leptin, by targeting the differentiation of bone marrow stem cells, plays a key role in mediating energy sensitive changes in bone metabolism during simulated spaceflight. This will be accomplished by examining whether transplantation of mesenchymal or hematopoietic stem cells from leptin receptor-deficient db/db mice into WT mice alters bone loss during simulated spaceflight. Completion of the proposed project is expected to increase our understanding of the role of energy metabolism in mediating bone loss during simulated spaceflight and suggest specific non-pharmacological and pharmacological interventions to mitigate the detrimental effects of spaceflight on bone marrow.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: Osteoporosis is a major health concern. To our knowledge, all forms of osteoporosis involve infiltration of fat into bone marrow. If the increased marrow fat alters the magnitude of bone loss, as hypothesized, this research will have significance to human health that transcends protecting astronauts from bone loss during spaceflight.

Successful completion of the proposed research involved substantial model development and validation and this work will have impact far beyond the specific aims of this project. To accomplish Specific Aim 1, it was necessary to perform adoptive transfer of WT bone marrow cells into kitw/w-v mice (Specific Aim 1). While bone marrow transplantation is routinely performed in the field of immunology, the impact of whole body sub-lethal and lethal radiation typically used to perform the procedure on the skeleton has received little attention. In the course of validating our model we performed several pilot studies that led to the unexpected finding that high dose radiation with photons, while having devastating effects on the hematopoietic compartment actually induces bone lining cells to become osteoblasts, leading to a transient increase in bone formation. Based on the likelihood that this activation of bone lining cells represents a potential therapeutic target we hope to pursue the mechanism for this response. Additionally, we made major advances in optimizing adoptive transfer for bone research: we have developed a protocol where following lethal irradiation we have quantitatively replaced hematopoietic cells in bone marrow with donor cells with no bone loss. This was accomplished with as few as 750 purified hematopoietic stem cells (publication in review and publication in preparation).

Mice exhibit premature age-related cancellous bone loss. Specifically, as much as 2/3 of the cancellous bone in the distal femur metaphysis is resorbed prior to establishment of peak total bone mass. The negative implication of this bone loss for HU studies is obvious. The mechanism, however, was unknown. In the course of performing studies in which mice were housed at thermoneutral (32Â°C) to minimize adaptive thermogenesis, we noted that the mice housed at this warm temperature showed unusually high cancellous bone volume fraction. Further investigation confirmed that premature cancellous bone loss in mice housed at room temperature is caused by mild cold stress (Iwaniec et al., 2016). While the low cancellous bone mass at clinically relevant sites in skeletally mature mice is an issue for preclinical studies, an even more important issue is whether physiological adaptation to mild cold stress influences the outcome of preclinical studies. This is of particular concern to spaceflight studies where mice housed at subthermoneutral temperatures during microgravity are likely to be more dependent upon adaptive thermogenesis than weight bearing controls, leading to bone loss independent of reduced weight bearing.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2016 
Task Progress: Specific Aim 1: Test the hypothesis that increased bone marrow adiposity plays a causal role in mediating simulated spaceflight-induced changes in bone metabolism.

We evaluated the impact of a 14d spaceflight (in slowly growing ovariectomized rats) and a 14d simulated spaceflight (hindlimb unloading (HU) in adult male mice) on bone marrow adiposity. As described in detail in Keune et al., 2016, bone marrow adipose tissue (MAT) increased in the distal femur metaphysis during spaceflight. During the same interval there was a reduction in cancellous bone volume fraction. The observed bone loss was due to increased bone resorption; osteoblast number, osteoblast activity and osteoblast turnover were normal. In agreement, HU resulted in bone loss in male mice and the bone loss was due to increased bone resorption. However, we were surprised to find that HU did not result in an increase in MAT (publication under review) in WBB6F1/J mice. It should be noted that this unexpected finding was replicated in HU C57BL6 mice (publication in preparation). We conclude from these results that increased MAT is not a prerequisite for bone loss in HU mice.

We originally hypothesized that mice incapable of producing MAT would be protected from HU-induced cancellous bone loss whereas mice with excessive MAT would exhibit increased bone loss. We tested these hypotheses using kitw/w-v (MAT-deficient) mice and ob/ob (excessive MAT) mice. As expected, decreased MAT was associated with increased bone formation and increased MAT was associated with decreased bone formation. However, HU-induced cancellous bone loss was exaggerated in MAT- mice and not impacted by excessive MAT (publication in preparation).

Taken together, our studies demonstrate that spaceflight-induced cancellous bone loss in ovariectomized rats and HU-induced cancellous bone loss in male mice each occur as a result of increased bone turnover, where the increase in resorption is inadequately compensated for by bone formation. While lineage decision (mesenchymal stem cells to osteoblasts or adipocytes) was shown to influence bone formation and MAT levels, our findings do not support a causal relationship between MAT infiltration and bone loss during spaceflight. Indeed, our studies in MAT-deficient mice suggest that MAT may have a protective role in limiting bone loss. Further research will be required to verify these findings and determine the underlying mechanisms.

Specific Aim 2: Test the hypothesis that the adipokine leptin, by targeting the differentiation of bone marrow stem cells, plays a key role in mediating energy sensitive changes in bone metabolism during simulated spaceflight.

We performed a series of studies to test this hypothesis. Initial studies were focused on establishing the effects of leptin on MAT and bone turnover in normal rats and leptin-deficient ob/ob mice (Turner et al., 2014; Turner et al., 2015; Philbrick KA et al., 2015; Lindenmaier et al., 2016). We also addressed the controversy as to whether the physiological actions of leptin on bone are mediated through peripheral or central nervous system (CNS)-mediated signaling (publication under review). This question is important because leptin regulates energy metabolism through CNS signaling. Subsequent studies focused on how leptin impacts bone and energy metabolism during HU (publication in preparation).

Taken together, our studies provide strong evidence that leptin administered subcutaneously and hypothalamic leptin gene therapy have surprisingly similar effects on bone metabolism in ob/ob mice; specifically, leptin decreased MAT, increased osteoblast number and activity, and increased osteoclast activity. Dose response studies revealed that the skeletal effects of leptin occur at very low circulating levels of the hormone (levels having minimal effects on food intake or body weight), suggesting that previously published reports demonstrating skeletal effects of leptin delivered into the hypothalamus were due to transport of leptin into peripheral circulation and not due to CNS mediated actions. If confirmed, our finding that the actions of peripheral leptin on bone and central actions of leptin on energy metabolism are dissociable from one another would represent a paradigm shift.

It is well established that there is a close association between the magnitude of mechanical loading of the skeleton and bone mass, and that bone loss often accompanies weight loss induced by dieting (Iwaniec and Turner, 2016). Potentially, this is due to combined decreases in peripheral and CNS leptin levels. In support, we have shown that increasing hypothalamic levels in adult rats can reduce body weight but, in contrast to caloric restriction, promote weight loss without bone loss (Turner et al., 2015). It is well established that weight loss due to reduced food intake contributes to bone loss during HU and for this reason, weight bearing controls are normally pair-fed to HU rodents. However, as shown for weight bearing rodents (Turner et al., 2015), leptin treatment of HU ob/ob mice, although inducing weight loss, did not result in further bone loss (publication in preparation).

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: Osteoporosis is a major health concern. To our knowledge, all forms of osteoporosis involve infiltration of fat into bone marrow. If the increased marrow fat alters the magnitude of bone loss, as hypothesized, this research will have significance to human health that transcends protecting astronauts from bone loss during spaceflight.

Our findings suggest that hematopoietic cells inhibit transition of lining cells to osteoblasts. As such, loss of this negative regulation by hematopoietic lineage cells is a plausible mechanism for increased bone turnover following irradiation. An important implication of the putative negative regulation of bone turnover by hematopoietic lineage cells would be that restoration of normal hematopoiesis following irradiation may be essential to restore normal bone turnover. Additionally, blocking the inhibitory actions of hematopoietic cells on osteoblast differentiation may be an attractive approach toward normalizing bone formation during spaceflight. In support of these hypotheses, our findings to date suggest that delivery of a very small number of highly purified hematopoietic stem cells to mice can restore normal hematopoiesis following lethal radiation and limit bone damage.

Prevention of morbid obesity in ob/ob mice further exacerbated many of the abnormalities associated with leptin deficiency. Because morbid obesity normally present in ob/ob mice attenuates the abnormal skeletal phenotype associated with leptin deficiency, the role of leptin in regulating postnatal bone growth, maturation, and turnover may have been underestimated. These findings suggest that a reduction in leptin levels due to a negative energy balance during spaceflight could contribute to bone loss in astronauts. Results to date strongly support this hypothesis.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2015 
Task Progress: Progress on Specific Aim 1

Experiment 1 was designed to evaluate differences in skeletal response of wild WT and adipocyte-deficient kitw/w-v mice to simulated spaceflight. The hindlimb unloading portion of the study was begun in Year 1 and completed in Year 2. Progress in Year 2 included: 1) analysis of body composition using duel energy x-ray absorptiometry (DXA); 2) analysis of femur, tibia, and lumbar vertebra microarchitecture using microcomputed tomography (microCT); 3) measurement of serum glucose at necropsy using a glucometer; and 4) embedding femurs in plastic for sectioning and quantitative histomorphometry. Progress in Year 3 included sectioning plastic embedded tissue and performing histological analysis on stained and unstained sections. The results are detailed in Abstracts submitted to American Society of Bone and Mineral Research (ASBMR) annual meeting to be held in Seattle, WA (Oct, 2015) and American Society for Gravitational and Space Research (ASGSR) annual meeting to be held in Alexandria, VA (Nov, 2015).

Experiment 2 was designed to evaluate differences in skeletal response of WT mice to simulated spaceflight following transplantation of bone marrow from WT or kitw/w-v mice. As was pointed out in our Year 1 annual report, the radiosensitivity of mice varies markedly among mouse strains. Before performing Experiment 2, we initiated a pilot study in Year 2 to verify feasibility of transplanting hematopoietic stem cells into WT mice.

Experiment 2 was modified based on the results of our feasibility study. As noted above, adoptive transfer of Kitw-w/v hematopoietic stem cells into WT mice did not rescue the mice from lethal irradiation. We therefore transferred purified stem cells from WT mice into WT and Kitw-w/v and performed hindlimb unloading following successful bone marrow reconstitution. We performed proposed Experiment 2 in Year 3 and have completed analysis of the bones by DXA and microCT.

Progress on Specific Aim 2

Experiment 1 was designed to determine whether a high level of fat within bone marrow is associated with accelerated bone loss during simulated spaceflight. The animal portion of this study was initiated and completed during Year 2. Specifically, weanling (1-month-old) WT and leptin-deficient ob/ob mice were maintained for 3 months under thermoneutral (32ÂºC) housing conditions to equalize resting energy expenditure between WT and ob/ob mice. The ob/ob mice were pair-fed to the WT mice to prevent development of morbid obesity in the ob/ob mice. Additionally: 1) DXA measurements were performed to measure body composition and the results analyzed; 2) femur, tibia, and lumbar vertebra were scanned ex vivo by microCT and the 3D reconstructions analyzed for determination of bone microarchitecture; 3) serum glucose, P1NP (bone formation marker), osteocalcin (bone turnover marker), and corticosterone (stress hormone) levels were measured at necropsy; and 4) femurs were embedded in plastic for sectioning and quantitative histomorphometry. Progress in Year 3 included sectioning of plastic embedded tissue in preparation for histomorphometric analysis.

Experiment 2: This study was designed to test the hypothesis that leptin signaling, acting on mesenchymal stem cells, reduces hindlimb unloading-induced bone loss, whereas leptin signaling, acting on hematopoietic stem cells, promotes bone loss. We have revised this experiment as originally designed based on: 1) our findings in Years 2 and 3 that morbid obesity attenuates the skeletal abnormalities associated with leptin deficiency in mice (J Endocrinol. 2014 Oct;223(1):M1-15); and 2) new evidence that the effects of leptin to increase bone formation, suppress bone marrow adiposity, and regulate appetite and energy expenditure can be readily disassociated from one another based on circulating levels of the hormone. We will now evaluate the important question as to whether hindlimb unloading blocks the anabolic and antiadipogenic actions of leptin. However, the experiment will also position us to answer our original question. This work will be completed during the no-cost extension.

Summary

To date we have made excellent progress in testing our hypothesis that negative energy balance during spaceflight results in bone marrow dysfunction and increased marrow adiposity and altered signaling by peripheral adipokines (cytokines produced by adipocytes). These changes contribute to negative bone turnover balance and bone loss. We have completed 3 of the 4 proposed studies (and several additional feasibility studies). We anticipate completing the final hindlimb unloading study (Specific Aim 2) during the no cost extension. Task Description: Spaceflight, by impairing the differentiation program of stem cells residing within bone marrow results in bone loss, increased bone marrow adiposity, anemia, and impaired immune function. Because these disturbances may potentially compromise the success of long-duration flights, there exists an urgent need to identify the underlying mechanisms and implement effective countermeasures. We propose to test the novel hypothesis that negative energy balance during spaceflight results in 1) bone marrow dysfunction and increased marrow adiposity and 2) altered signaling by peripheral adipokines (cytokines produced by adipocytes) all of which, in turn, contribute to negative bone turnover balance and bone loss. The proposed research will focus on the precise role of adipose tissue and the important adipokine leptin in mediating the detrimental effects of simulated spaceflight (hindlimb unloading) on bone marrow cells in mice by accomplishing the following 2 specific aims: Specific Aim 1 will test the hypothesis that increased bone marrow adiposity plays a causal role in mediating simulated spaceflight-induced changes in bone metabolism. This will be accomplished by comparing the skeletal response to hindlimb unloading of wild type (WT) mice with mice incapable of generating bone marrow adipocytes (kit-receptor-deficient KitW/W-v mice). Specific Aim 2 will test the hypothesis that the adipokine leptin, by targeting the differentiation of bone marrow stem cells, plays a key role in mediating energy sensitive changes in bone metabolism during simulated spaceflight. This will be accomplished by examining whether transplantation of mesenchymal or hematopoietic stem cells from leptin receptor-deficient db/db mice into WT mice alters bone loss during simulated spaceflight. Completion of the proposed project is expected to increase our understanding of the role of energy metabolism in mediating bone loss during simulated spaceflight and suggest specific non-pharmacological and pharmacological interventions to mitigate the detrimental effects of spaceflight on bone marrow.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: Osteoporosis is a major health concern. To our knowledge, all forms of osteoporosis involve infiltration of fat into bone marrow. If the increased marrow fat contributes to bone loss, as hypothesized, this research will have significance to human health that transcends protecting astronauts from bone loss during spaceflight.

Our findings suggest that hematopoietic cells inhibit transition of lining cells to osteoblasts. As such, loss of this negative regulation by hematopoietic lineage cells is a plausible mechanism for increased bone turnover following irradiation. An important implication of the putative negative regulation of bone turnover by hematopoietic lineage cells would be that restoration of normal hematopoiesis following irradiation may be essential to restore normal bone turnover. Additionally, blocking the inhibitory actions of hematopoietic cells on osteoblast differentiation may be an attractive approach toward normalizing bone formation during spaceflight.

Prevention of morbid obesity in ob/ob mice further exacerbated many of the abnormalities associated with leptin deficiency. Because morbid obesity normally present in ob/ob mice attenuates the abnormal skeletal phenotype associated with leptin deficiency, the role of leptin in regulating postnatal bone growth, maturation, and turnover may have been underestimated. These findings suggest that a reduction in leptin levels due to a negative energy balance during spaceflight could contribute to bone loss in astronauts.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2014 
Task Progress: Progress on Specific Aim 1

As described in our Year 1 Progress report, Experiment 1 was designed to evaluate differences in skeletal response of WT and adipocyte-deficient kitw/w-v mice to simulated spaceflight. The hindlimb unloading portion of the study was begun in Year 1 and completed in Year 2. Additionally progress was made in Year 2: 1) duel energy x-ray absorptiometry (DXA) measurements were performed to measure body composition and the results analyzed; 2) femur, tibia, and lumbar vertebra were scanned ex vivo by microcomputed tomography (microCT) and the 3D reconstructions analyzed for determination of bone microarchitecture; 3) serum glucose levels were measured at necropsy; and 4) femurs were embedded in plastic for sectioning and quantitative histomorphometry.

Experiment 2 was designed to evaluate differences in skeletal response of WT mice to simulated spaceflight following transplantation of bone marrow from WT or kitw/w-v mice. As was pointed out in our Year 1 annual report, the radiosensitivity of mice varies among strains. We performed a pilot study to verify feasibility of transplanting hematopoietic stem cells into kitw/w-v and WT mice. Although found to be more radiosensitive, we developed a successful protocol for stem cell transplantation into the mutant mice.

Progress on Specific Aim 2

Experiment 1 was designed to determine whether a high level of fat within bone marrow is associated with accelerated bone loss during simulated spaceflight. The animal portion of this study was initiated and completed during Year 2. Specifically, weanling (1-month-old) WT and leptin-deficient ob/ob mice were maintained for 3 months under thermoneutral (32ÂºC) housing conditions to equalize resting energy expenditure between WT and ob/ob mice. The ob/ob mice were pair-fed to the WT mice to prevent development of morbid obesity in the ob/ob mice. Additionally: 1) the hindlimb unloading portion of the study was performed; 2) DXA measurements were performed to measure body composition and the results analyzed; 3) femur, tibia, and lumbar vertebra were scanned ex vivo by miroCT and the 3D reconstructions analyzed for determination of bone microarchitecture; 3) serum glucose, P1NP (bone formation marker), osteocalcin (bone turnover marker), and corticosterone (stress hormone) levels were measured at necropsy; and 4) femurs were embedded in plastic for sectioning and quantitative histomorphometry.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2013 
Task Progress: During spaceflight and simulated spaceflight (extended bed rest in humans and hind limb unloading in rodents) bone marrow becomes infiltrated with adipose tissue. Specific Aim 1 of this proposal is designed to test the hypothesis that increased bone marrow fat plays a causal role in mediating simulated spaceflight-induced detrimental changes in bone metabolism. The changes in bone metabolism include increased bone resorption and decreased bone formation. The net result is rapid bone loss.

The first step of the proposal was to build and test cages for performing hind limb unloading of mice. We have accomplished this task. Additionally, students working on the project were trained to handle experimental animals and perform the skeletal analyses that will be used in this research.

We have also initiated the first major experiment of the project. This 4-month-long study involved comparing the skeletal response to hind limb unloading of mice with a mutation preventing production of bone marrow fat with normal mice. If our hypothesis is correct than the mutant mice will exhibit less bone loss during simulated spaceflight then normal mice. We have completed the animal portion of experiment and are in the process of analyzing the skeleton by a variety of high resolution x-ray based scanning techniques and histology.
