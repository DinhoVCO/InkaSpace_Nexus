Flight Assignment/Project Notes: ISS

NOTE: Extended to 9/10/2018 per NSSC information (Ed., 10/10/17)

Task Description: This project seeks to use an unbiased genome-wide approach to define the changes in DNA methylation that occur in Arabidopsis during spaceflight, in order to better understand the spectrum of responses that allow adaptation to the spaceflight environment. This project is one of both discovery on a genome-wide scale and application to specific hypotheses. We propose replicated whole genome bisulfite sequencing in Arabidopsis roots and leaves to discover and quantify 5-methylCytosine (5mCyt) epigenetic modification, coupled with RNASeq examination of the comparable transcriptomes to both quantitatively and qualitatively assess spaceflight-associated DNA methylation on transcript profiles. We will then test the hypothesis that methylation is involved in spaceflight adaptation by similarly examining methylation mutants.

Epigenetic features play a role in the regulation of gene expression and the subsequent response of an organism to its environment. DNA methylation is a major epigenetic modification that is not directly coded into the genome sequence and yet can modify expression and be inherited for at least for one generation. Several groups, including ours, have shown that large numbers of plant genes are differentially expressed in response to spaceflight.

We now have extensive preliminary data that show significant genome-wide 5mCyt modification differences in spaceflight plants compared to ground controls.

The tactical objectives of this proposal are: 1) Bisulfite whole genome sequencing will be used to generate maps of spaceflight-induced changes in 5mCyt at single base-pair resolution for roots and leaves. 2) RNASeq will be used to digitally quantify and qualitatively assess transcriptome in the same samples, as differential methylation has been functionally linked to changes in both promoter regulation and structural changes in RNAs. These data will characterize functional impacts of spaceflight induced across the entire genome. 3) DNA methylation mutants will be used to directly test the hypothesis that epigenetic modification of DNA is a key aspect of spaceflight adaptation.

Elucidating the spaceflight methylomes of plants contributes to the fundamental understanding of how biology responds and adapts to the spaceflight environment, and also provides insight in the adaptive strategies plants use in the face of novel stimuli outside of their evolutionary experience. The long term goal of this line of inquiry is to understand how the methylome contributes to the adaptive strategies of plants to novel environments.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: This research contributes to a greater understanding of the role of epigenetic changes to the genome in response to novel or stressful environments.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2018 
Task Progress: In this final year of the grant, the returned plant samples were extracted and evaluated, and then ensuing molecular data analyzed. So far, these data have contributed to two additional manuscripts; one in press, and one in the final stages of preparation. A synopsis of the results and conclusions follow:

Harvests

Plants for SVT (Science Verification Test) and EVT (Experiment Verification Test) were grown in VEGGIE and were harvested at 11 days. Each plate was harvested to a single Falcon tube containing RNA later. Samples were then stowed at -80Deg Celsius until returned to the Principal Investigators (PIs). Harvest from station was monitored by the PI via video communication from their lab in Gainesville, FL.

Post-Harvest Analysis

Samples for both flight and ground control were transferred by the PI to the University of Florida for analysis of quality and quantity. Samples were warmed and separated into root and leaf tissue, hypocotyl tissue was removed and stored for future analysis. Plates were then separated into 4 replicates, each having two plates within a replicate. A portion of the plants (2-4) were removed from each plate for RNA extraction, and then depending on genotype, 4-20 plants that were left on the plate were used for DNA extraction.

Extraction of Nucleic Acids from Flight and Ground Control Samples

RNA extraction was completed using a Qiagen RNeasy Plant kit. DNA extraction was preformed using a method developed in-house for high levels of clean genomic DNA. All sample material provided enough nucleic acid material for downstream applications.

Imaging and plant growth: Images of the first plates in VEGGIE were taken on Day 4, Day 8, and Day 11 (harvest). Due to excellent germination and growth all plates appear to have consistent growth. Photography of plates was sufficient for post-harvest analysis to provide data fro growth rates and morphometric analyses.

Methylome Analyses: To examine the role of 5mCyt in spaceflight adaptation, the APEX04-EPEX experiment conducted onboard the ISS evaluated the spaceflight altered genome-wide methylation profiles of two methylation-regulating gene mutants (methyltransferase 1 (met1-7) and elongator complex subunit 2 (elp2-5)) along with a wild type Col-0 control. The methylome data revealed that both elp2-5 and met1-7 mutants had, on average, significantly higher methylation levels in flight than in the ground controls for both leaf and root tissues, whereas there were no differences in methylation levels between flight samples and ground controls in Col-0 roots and leaves. Overlapping the differential gene expression data with differentially methylated cytosines within protein-coding gene regions revealed the complex transcriptional regulatory processes of adapting to spaceflight. The methylation mutants provided new insights into organ-specific adaptation to spaceflight. The results show that elp2-5 has a role in regulating root adaptation to spaceflight, whereas met1-7 has a more prominent role in the physiological adaptation of leaves. Gene ontology enrichment showed that a significant proportion of Col-0 differentially-methylated and expressed genes were associated with pathogen response consistent with previous reports. Met1-7 and elp2-5 showed enrichments unique to each organ and genotype. The major points of discovery were:

- Spaceflight increases genome-wide DNA methylation levels in met1-7 and elp2-5 but not in the wild type Col-0 plants

Average genome wide methylation level profiles in spaceflight and on the ground.

- Spaceflight changes in DNA methylation levels were associated with protein-coding gene regions.

- Spaceflight alters the distribution and direction of differential methylated cytosines (DmCs) and differential methylated regions (DMRs) in elp2-5 and met1-7 leaves and roots.

- There is a positive correlation of differential DNA methylation and differential gene expression. - The patterns of differential DNA methylation and differential gene expression in response to spaceflight differs among the methylation mutants and the wild type.

General conclusions

A growing number of studies are showing evidence that DNA methylation plays a crucial role in regulating stress responses and adaptation in plants. Understanding how plant systems adapt to novel environments, such as spaceflight, is important for advancing research in plant abiotic stress tolerance, which has valuable implications for ensuring food security in adverse environments. Previous spaceflight transcriptomic and proteomic data showed prominent spaceflight changes in genes typically associated with abiotic stress response on Earth. Therefore, to understand how plants adapt to the novel environment of spaceflight, it is important to investigate the epigenetic regulation of plantsâ€™ genes in spaceflight and identify specific marks that contribute to known gene expression patterns.

In this study, DNA methylation dynamics in the leaves and roots of wild-type Col-0 and two methylation mutant (elp2 and met1) Arabidopsis lines grown in spaceflight and on the ground were assessed with a single-base resolution. The genome-wide analyses presented here show that the mechanisms that regulate spaceflight responses also involve changes in DNA methylation status in an organ-specific manner. Although spaceflight did not induce large scale genome-wide changes in wild type Col-0 leaves and roots, spaceflight did induce significant genome-wide methylation changes in the roots and leaves of both elp2-5 and met1-7.

Several observations reported here suggest that differential DNA methylation in response to spaceflight is highly organ-specific and affects a different set of genes, and the GO-enriched categories differed between leaves and roots in both the methylation mutants. Thus, the finding that global methylation patterns are drastically remodeled by spaceflight in an organ-specific manner could provide insight into how epigenetic marks influence plant genomes and transcriptional programs to respond and adapt to novel conditions.

However, before the flight experiment was launched, the biochemical protocols were first calibrated with non-spaceflight experiment samples:

The EVT (Experiment Verification Test) materials were used to calibrate the amount of material needed to provide the 200 ng of genomic DNA required per sample to conduct the methylome analyses. The results of these calibrations were published in December of 2016 (LeFrois et al., 2016). Briefly: Genomic DNA was isolated from the three genotypes comprising APEX04: Col-0, met1-7, and elp2-5. Molecular analysis was performed to assess both the quality and quantity of gDNA produced from these extractions. Gene-specific DNA methylation (rather than global, due to cost constraints) was evaluated at two sites: AT2G07698 (+253 to +507 from ATG) and AT2G07687 (+19 to +355 from ATG) from shoot DNA samples of Col-0, met1-7 and elp2-5.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2016 
Task Progress: Over the past year, we have completed successfully both a Science Verification Test (SVT) and Experimental Verification Test (EVT) and are prepared for flight. Further, ongoing ground studies of epigenetics have prepared us to process the samples upon their return from flight. Currently, techniques are being used for gene specific studies in our three experimental seed lines.

Report Summary

Experimental plants were planted and stayed dormant until experimental start. Plants grew well and as expected in the VEGGIE growth hardware. Harvest was completed by pseudonauts and material was stored to successfully preserve nucleic acid. RNA was successfully extracted and had both high quality and quantity for downstream applications. RNA was used to analyse gene expression levels by real-time PCR. DNA was successfully extracted in high quality and high quantity. DNA then underwent successful bisulfate conversion followed by sequencing. Gene expression (RNA) and cytosine differences in the methlyome (DNA) were then able to be compared in downstream applications.

Upcoming work

Gene Specific Differential Methylation in Mutants

In order to be able to detect differential methylation between Col-0 and two methylation mutants, we are utilizing TA Cloning of bisulfite converted DNA to allow for sequencing of single PCR product. Bisulfite conversion is a chemical treatment for DNA that converts non-methylated cytosines into thymines (C=>T) and leaves methylated cytosines. In this experiment we will be performing a bisulfite conversion on genomic DNA from Col-0, Met1-7, and Elp2-5 shoots, and generating PCR products from the exon regions of AT1G34170 and AT5G26080 â€“ two highly differentially methylated genes of interest from the APEX-04 EVT study. The PCR products will have a C in place of a methylated cytosine. Our current goal is to sequence the PCR products and be able to detect, with single-nucleotide resolution, sites of differential methylation. What we have now are very diverse PCR products in the same sample tube. Direct Sanger sequencing of the samples generated noisy data. This noise could likely be due to the fact that these genes are highly differentially methylated, producing PCR products with a variety of nucleotide sequences which will elevate the level of noise in the chromatograms produced.

TA cloning is the process by which PCR products are ligated into a vector; bacteria are then transformed with that vector, selected, and grown. The cloned plasmid vectors containing the PCR products can then be extracted and Sanger sequenced. By using TA cloning, we will generate clean data because each sample we submit will only contain one species of PCR product. We will also be able to quantify and generate percent of differential methylation by counting the number of Ts vs. Cs for each sample type (i.e., Col-0 AT1G34170).

Flight Readiness

With the findings in this report we feel confident in the ability for the spaceflight experiment to return high quality materials to be used for ground analysis. All â€œExperimental Success Criteriaâ€ fell within acceptable standards, with the majority of the criteria meeting the â€œexcellentâ€ standard.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2015 
Task Progress: Most of this year's progress is in terms of developing the tools and testing the protocols, but a Science Verification Test (SVT) is scheduled for August.

Aim 1 required a bisulfate whole genome sequencing protocol that will be used to identify the changes induced in spaceflight compared to ground and has been used for preliminary data generation. The protocol was shown to work successfully and was able to determine 5mCyt patterns across the entire genome of Arabidopsis.

Aim 2 required RNASeq to be used to understand the transcriptional status of the genomes. A pilot RNASeq project was prepared and executed to better clarify the protocol necessary for spaceflight samples. This project utilized small amounts of material for different sections of a root to examine gene expression. The execution of the pilot RNASeq project greatly expanded the familiarity of protocol and requirements for the upcoming experiment.

The pilot experiment showed that RNA-seq method was more powerful in identifying differentially expressed genes compared to arrays. RNA-seq identified novel genes that are not detected by arrays and gives additional information on splice variants.

Aim 3 required the two aims above to be applied to Arabidopsis mutants with disrupted or deficient key DNA methylation genes. The proposed experiment called for three different seed lines to be launched to the International Space Station (ISS) for 12 days growth before harvest for ground analysis. The seed lines are to be one wild-type and two gene knock out lines (methylation mutants). An aim of the proposal sought to perturb DNA methylation through mutation, to understand the effects of the altered DNA methylation directly on the methylome.

Two different genes were target for these experiments; ELP2 and MET1. Due to the significant role of the genes of interest, multiple lines of the knock-out were screened for uniform phenotypes, growth rates, and viability to the study.

Task Description: This project seeks to use an unbiased genome-wide approach to define the changes in DNA methylation that occur in Arabidopsis during spaceflight, in order to better understand the spectrum of responses that allow adaptation to the spaceflight environment. This project is one of both discovery on a genome-wide scale and application to specific hypotheses. We propose replicated whole genome bisulfite sequencing in Arabidopsis roots and leaves to discover and quantify 5-methylCytosine (5mCyt) epigenetic modification, coupled with RNASeq examination of the comparable transcriptomes to both quantitatively and qualitatively assess spaceflight-associated DNA methylation on transcript profiles. We will then test the hypothesis that methylation is involved in spaceflight adaptation by similarly examining methylation mutants.

Epigenetic features play a role in the regulation of gene expression and the subsequent response of an organism to its environment. DNA methylation is a major epigenetic modification that is not directly coded into the genome sequence and yet can modify expression and be inherited for at least for one generation.
