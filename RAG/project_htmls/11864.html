NEW TITLE--July 2018: Genomic and Functional Analysis of Desiccation Tolerance of International Space Station Isolated Enterococcus faecalis and Their Pathogenicity in Caenorhabditis elegans. Change in organism used in this project, approved by NASA Headquarters.

Enterococcus faecalis is an opportunistic human pathogen consistently isolated and detected by molecular analysis on board the International Space Station (ISS). The ability of this organism to survive outside of the human gut may have important implications for its spread to a new host environment. The goal of this study is to characterize the desiccation survival capacity of ISS isolated E. faecalis strains and to investigate their pathogenicity in the model organism Caenorhabditis elegans. First, we shall provide the first comparative genomic information of E. faecalis strains to characterize strain-specific sequence variants and the identification of traits that may confer increased resistance to desiccation and antibiotics. We will also examine the presence of the clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats-CRISPR-associated proteins (CRISPR-Cas) that confer bacterial resistance to foreign DNA and may provide an additional method of persistence to the transfer of mobile genetic elements. Phenotypic data will determine how desiccation tolerance varies, and how these variations relate to the clinical and commensal strain types. In vivo pathogenicity tests will culminate our investigation into the effect of E. faecalis biofilm morphotypes on the ability to establish infection in the model organism Caenorhabditis elegans. This work will help to determine genomic, phenotypic, and physiological basis for various desiccation and antibiotic resistance of ISS isolated E. faecalis, and provide insight to pathogenicity of spaceflight isolated microorganisms, where astronaut immunity is decreased and the availability of antibiotics is limited.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: This project will investigate the potential for opportunistic human pathogens, E. faecalis, to survive outside of the human gut. These isolates have not been genetically or phenotypically characterized and those are the first steps of this project.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2020 
Task Progress: Enterococcus faecalis is a multidrug resistant, opportunistic human pathogen and a leading cause of hospital acquired infections. Recently, isolates have been recovered from the air and surfaces onboard the International Space Station (ISS). Pangenomic and functional analyses were carried out to assess their potential impact on astronaut health. Genomes of each ISS isolate, and both clinical and commensal reference strains, were evaluated for their core and unique gene content, acquired antibiotic resistance genes, phage, plasmid content, and virulence traits. In order to determine their potential survival when outside of the human host, isolates were also challenged with three weeks of desiccation at 30% relative humidity. Finally, pathogenicity of the ISS strains was evaluated in the model organism Caenorhabditis elegans. At the culmination of this study, there were no defining signatures that separated known pathogenic strains from the more commensal phenotypes. As a result, the current reliance on database information alone must be shifted to experimentally evaluated characteristics of clinically relevant microorganisms. By harnessing the technology already onboard the ISS, the crew will then be able to achieve near-real time detection of isolates that may impact crew health.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2018 
Task Progress: While waiting on the arrival of the Enterococcus faecalis isolates recovered from the International Space Station (ISS), I began my fellowship research by expanding my skill set as a microbiologist. I was given the opportunity to analyze my first metagenomic data set of microbial communities collected from the soils of Vulcan Copahue, Chile. This introduction into bioinformatics would later prove to be a highly effective exercise for developing the skills needed to analyze the genomic data from the ISS strains. In addition, I spent this time learning the techniques necessary for library preparation and sequencing using the Nanopore MinION sequencer, the same technology recently used onboard the ISS. These skills, combined with my previous NASA flight operations experience, provided me with the opportunity to perform the first MinION DNA sequencing under simulated Mars and Europa gravity conditions while onboard a Zero-G parabolic flight ( https://www.media.mit.edu/projects/search-for-extra-terrestrial-genomes-setg/overview/ ). The data from this flight have recently been accepted by npj Microgravity (Carr et al., 2018), and a second manuscript is currently in progress.

Upon receiving the ISS strains the first objective was to characterize their genomic content, and to compare them to previously identified isolates. The assembly of each isolate’s genome was accomplished by combining Illumina (250 nt, paired end reads) with Nanopore sequencing technologies. Using the tools provided by the Center for Genomic Epidemiology (CGE) website ( http://www.genomicepidemiology.org ), I was able to identify the sequence type, acquired antibiotic resistance genes, virulence genes, phage elements, plasmids, and the potential for these strains to serve as human pathogens. With the known sequence type, I was able to find similar strains with fully assembled genomes and reconstruct a phylogenetic tree to determine the closest relatives to the ISS strains. Additionally, by performing a pan-genome analysis, I was able to identify which genes are unique to the ISS strains.

Briefly, ISS_1 (strain type (ST) 65) is most closely related to human commensal strains of E. faecalis, yet it also contains many previously documented virulence genes. Isolates ISS_2 and ISS_3 (ST30) were recovered on the same day at two different locations within the U.S. Lab. Analysis of the 2191 core genes shared by all E. faecalis isolates in this study revealed these isolates differ by only 2 single nucleotide polymorphisms. ISS_4 (ST40) is most closely related to isolate JH-1, and is a member of the clonal complex 40, a group of E. faecalis strains recovered from clinical settings. ISS_4 was also positive for all of the listed virulence traits for E. faecalis. All four strains tested positive (>80% likelihood) for the potential to serve as human pathogens, as predicted by the PathogenFinder feature of CGE.

The genomic data is currently guiding the development of the second objective of my fellowship, the phenotypic characterization of the isolates and their potential for pathogenicity. Following the efforts of Schiwon et al., 2013, I will be determining the minimum inhibitory concentrations of different classes of antibiotics for each isolate. I will also characterize each isolate’s ability to form a biofilm, a trait that may prove beneficial in a zero-gravity environment. Because each of the isolates were recovered from surface or air samples, I will investigate their ability to survive extended periods of desiccation. The ability of Enterococcus species to survive outside of the human gut may be a driving force behind hospital outbreaks, and equally important onboard the ISS. Each of these assays will be performed using E. faecalis OG1RF as a representative commensal-like strain, and E. faecalis MMH594 as a representative clinical isolate to provide a range of comparison for the ISS strains.

NOTE: This project is funded by NASA as a result of a Space Act Agreement between NASA and the Sloan Foundation, for Collaborative Research Proposal for Microbiology of the Built Environment (MoBE). The purpose of this Space Act Agreement is to enhance the ability of NASA and the Sloan Foundation to support scientific research and the dissemination of information about the effect of spaceflight on microbial ecosystems in spacecraft such as the International Space Station (ISS) through sponsorship of postdoctoral fellowships. NASA is providing the isolates for all studies.

Staphylococcus epidermidis is an opportunistic human pathogen consistently isolated and detected by molecular analysis on board the International Space Station (ISS). The formation of biofilms is a significant process for S. epidermidis to establish pathogenicity, and five distinct biofilm morphotypes have been recently identified. The goal of this study is to characterize the biofilm forming capacity of ISS isolated S. epidermidis strains and to investigate their pathogenicity in the model organism Caenorhabditis elegans. First, we shall provide the first comparative genomic information of S. epidermidis strains to characterize strain-specific sequence variants and the identification of mutations in biofilm-associated genes that may have been incurred during spaceflight. We will also examine the presence of the clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats-CRISPR-associated proteins (CRISPR-Cas) that confer bacterial resistance to foreign DNA and may provide an additional method of persistence to spaceflight conditions. Using quantitative microscopic analyses, confocal scanning laser microscopy will be used to characterize biofilm morphotypes, and each morphotype will be evaluated for antibiotic resistance. Transcriptional analysis and proteomic data will determine how biofilm-associated gene expression and protein production vary, and how these variations relate to the biofilm morphotype produced. In vivo pathogenicity tests will culminate our investigation into the effect of S. epidermidis biofilm morphotypes on the ability to establish infection in the model organism Caenorhabditis elegans. This work will help to determine genomic, phenotypic, and physiological basis for various biofilm morphotypes formed by ISS isolated S. epidermidis, and provide insight to pathogenicity of spaceflight isolated microorganisms, where astronaut immunity is decreased and the availability of antibiotics is limited.
