International Space Station – Microbial Observatory of Pathogenic Virus, Bacteria, and Fungi (ISS-MOP) project will generate the microbial censuses associated with various ISS modules using traditional culture-based methods and state-of-the art molecular techniques. The objectives of the ISS-MOP project are to measure presence of viral and select bacterial and fungal pathogens and correlate their presence on crew. The effects of the spaceflight environment on viral and microbial pathogen dynamics are largely unknown and represent both a significant gap in our knowledge and an important opportunity to study the presence of microbial populations and predict health risks during long-term space exploration.

The hypothesis of the ISS-MOP project is that certain short-living viruses associated with inert environmental spaces in the closed habitat of the ISS can have an adverse influence on crew health. To test this hypothesis the team will catalog viruses associated with surfaces and air of the ISS modules using the all-encompassing Lawrence Livermore Microbial Detection Array (LLMDA), which contains probes to detect not only viruses but also clinically important human bacterial and fungal pathogens. In addition, skin and saliva samples from crewmembers will be requested to test the hypothesis.

There are three aims proposed in this ISS-MOP project. While the cultivation-based assay (Aim 1) provides a valid estimation of microbial presence, it offers only a limited assessment of the phylogenetic/pathogenic diversity and physiological breadth of the microbial population present. Therefore, the team will use the novel and comprehensive LLMDA microarray, which is specifically designed to elucidate the pathogenic viral and microbial diversity profiles of complex samples to at least the species level (Aim 2). In addition, we will characterize the virulence and antibiotic resistance mechanisms associated with these pathogens from the ISS by developing a new Virulence and Antibiotic Resistance gene array and analyze the samples on this array. The final deliverable of the proposed project to NASA will be the ISS-MOP dataset, a compilation of all of the genomic sequences and genetic information of viruses and microbes encountered on and within the ISS habitat (Aim 3).

The objectives of the ISS-MOP project directly respond to the National Research Council Committee for the Decadal Survey on Biological and Physical Sciences in Space recommendation P1 where it states, “to establish a microbial observatory program on the ISS to conduct long-term, multigenerational studies of microbial population dynamics.” The ISS-MOP project will also provide answers to NASA Space Biology Guiding Questions: How do alterations in gravity affect in microbial cells (CMM1); Does the enhanced virulence or other genetic changes observed with microbial cultures flown in space occur in other species (CMM4); and What are the genetic changes underlying physiological responses to spaceflight demonstrated in biological systems (CMM5).

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: The effects of the spaceflight environment on viral and microbial pathogen dynamics are largely unknown and represent both a significant gap in our knowledge and an important opportunity to study the presence of microbial populations and predict health risks during long-term space exploration. International Space Station – Microbial Observatory of Pathogenic Virus, Bacteria, and Fungi (ISS-MOP) project will generate the microbial censuses associated with crewmembers and various ISS modules using both traditional culture-based methods and state-of-the art molecular techniques to a degree that has not previously been possible.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2022 
Task Progress: The International Space Station (ISS) is a uniquely enclosed environment that has been continuously occupied for the last two decades. Throughout its operation, protecting the health of the astronauts on board has been a high priority. The human microbiome plays a significant role in maintaining human health, and disruptions in the microbiome have been linked to various diseases. To evaluate the effects of spaceflight on the human microbiome, body swabs and saliva samples were collected from four ISS astronauts on consecutive expeditions. Astronaut samples were analyzed using shotgun metagenomic sequencing and microarrays to characterize the microbial biodiversity before, during, and after the astronauts’ time on board the ISS. Samples were evaluated at an individual and population level to identify changes in microbial diversity and abundance. No significant changes in the number or relative abundance of taxa were observed between collection time points when samples from all four astronauts were analyzed together. When the astronauts’ saliva samples were analyzed individually, the saliva samples of some astronauts showed significant changes in the relative abundance of taxa during and after spaceflight. The relative abundance of Prevotella in saliva samples increased during two astronauts’ time on board the ISS, while the relative abundance of other commensal taxa, such as Neisseria, Rothia and Haemophilus, decreased. The abundance of some antimicrobial resistance genes within the saliva samples also showed significant changes. Most notably, elfamycin resistance gene significantly increased in all four astronauts post-flight and a CfxA6 beta-lactam marker significantly increased during spaceflight but returned to normal levels post-flight. The combination of both shotgun metagenomic sequencing and microarrays showed the benefit of both technologies in monitoring microbes on board the ISS. There were some changes in each astronaut’s microbiome during spaceflight, but these changes were not universal for all four astronauts. Two antimicrobial resistance gene markers did show a significant change in abundance in the saliva samples of all four astronauts across their collection times. These results provide insight for future ISS microbial monitoring studies and targets for antimicrobial resistance screenings.

The microorganisms present on ISS surfaces originate from crew and cargo or from life support recirculation in an almost entirely closed system. The Microbial Tracking 1 (MT-1) project was the first ISS environmental surface study to report on the metagenome profiles without using whole genome amplification. The study surveyed the microbial communities from eight surfaces over a 14-month period. The Microbial Tracking 2 (MT-2) project aimed to continue the work of MT-1, sampling an additional four flights, over another year and a half. Eight surfaces across the ISS were sampled with sterile wipes and processed upon return to Earth. DNA, extracted from the processed samples (and controls) were treated with propidium monoazide to detect intact/viable cells, or left untreated, to detect the total DNA population (free DNA/compromised cells/intact cells/viable cells. Treated and untreated DNA were analyzed; metagenomics analysis was also conducted on the DNA samples. Culture and quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) analyses were also performed to supplement the metagenomics results. The overall number of microbial species detected at each time point did not significantly change, but species profiles did. Multi-dimensional scaling and differential abundance analysis showed significant changes in the microbial populations between sampling time points but not across surface locations. The observed antimicrobial resistance gene profiles for the ISS surface samples were more stable over time, both temporally and spatially, with 29 antimicrobial resistance genes detected across all samples. Metagenomic assembled genomes were able to be reconstructed from the dataset, resulting in 82 metagenomic assembled genomes, which provided further insight into the functional capabilities of the microbiome of this unique built environment. The populations of bacteria and fungi collected from the eight ISS surfaces remained similar between locations but changed over the course of a year and a half. The microbial communities were dominated by organisms associated with the human skin, indicating a large exchange between the astronaut crewmembers and the ISS surfaces. Despite the changes in the microbial community, the presence of antimicrobial resistance genes was stable, indicating the persistence of genetic redundancy.

Task Progress: [Ed. note--Progress as of November 2020]

The International Space Station (ISS) is a uniquely enclosed environment that has been continuously occupied for the last two decades. Throughout its operation, protecting the health of the astronauts on-board has been a high priority. The human microbiome plays a significant role in maintaining human health, and disruptions in the microbiome have been linked to various diseases. To evaluate the effects of spaceflight on the environmental and human microbiome, surface swabs were collected from five consecutive flights. Body swabs and saliva samples were collected from four ISS astronauts on consecutive expeditions. Samples were analyzed using shotgun metagenomic sequencing and microarrays to characterize the microbial biodiversity before, during, and after the astronauts’ time onboard the ISS. Samples were analyzed using metagenomic sequencing and DNA microarray and evaluated at an individual and population level to identify changes in microbial diversity and abundance. No significant changes in the number or relative abundance of taxa were observed between collection time points when samples from all four astronauts were analyzed together. When the astronauts’ saliva samples were analyzed individually, the saliva samples of some astronauts showed significant changes in the relative abundance of taxa during and after spaceflight. The relative abundance of Prevotella in saliva samples increased during two astronauts’ time onboard the ISS while the relative abundance of other commensal taxa such as Neisseria, Rothia, and Haemophilus decreased.

We have performed microbiological culture of environmental surface wipe and air samples from the five flights (F4-8). Overall, we observed bacterial and fungal growth from surface samples from all flights. The concentration ranged from 10^2 to 10^7 CFU/m2. There were no differences in the bacterial read counts between Flights 4-7, in both the propidium monoazide (PMA) and non-PMA treated samples. However, there were differences in fungal reads, with the highest fungal counts obtained from F6, in both the non-PMA and PMA treated samples.

We analyzed the microbial profiles from all eight surfaces across four different environmental flights F4-F7 using metagenomics sequencing. Most of the top 12 most abundant species such as Propionibacterium acnes, six Staphylococcus spp., Haemophilus parainfluenzae, Rothia mucilaginosa, Corynebacterium sp. GD7., and Streptococcus mitis are all part of the normal oral microbiome.

The combination of both shotgun metagenomic sequencing and microarrays showed the benefit of both technologies in monitoring microbes on-board the ISS. There were some changes in each astronaut’s microbiome during spaceflight, but these changes were not universal for all four astronauts. These results provide insight for future ISS microbial monitoring studies and targets for antimicrobial resistance screenings.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2019 
Task Progress: In this performing year, we performed metagenomic DNA sequencing of all four environmental samples (F4, F5, F6, and F7) and the crew 1 samples. We performed analysis on the abundance and diversity comparisons of environmental samples, and we compared crew 1 samples to surface samples to determine any commonality between crew and environmental microbiome. We also compared the crew 1 samples preflight, inflight, and postflight to determine any diversity changes during spaceflight.

Propionibacterium acnes and Staphylococcus epidermidis are the most prevalent in all eight surface locations. Propionibacterium was the most abundant bacterial genus in skin and ear. Streptococcus, Prevotella, and Actinomyces are the top three genera in mouth and saliva. In nostril, Corynebacterium, Propionibacterium, and Staphylococcus are the most abundant. Non-metric multidimensional scaling showed that of the eight crew body sites analyzed, skin samples are most similar in microbial diversity and abundance to the ISS surfaces. Saliva samples showed the most interesting trend of decreasing diversity in the three timepoints during flight, while the diversity increased in post-flight timepoints. Analysis of samples from additional crewmembers will help us better understand the fluctuation of the microbiome pre-, during, and post-flights from this crewmember.

Task Description: [Ed. note (January 2018): change in grant number and end date per NSSC information and NASA Ames Research Center; note grant 80NSSC18K0113 has period of performance of 10/20/2017-4/30/2019]

International Space Station – Microbial Observatory of Pathogenic Virus, Bacteria, and Fungi (ISS-MOP) project will generate the microbial censuses associated with various ISS modules using traditional culture-based methods and state-of-the art molecular techniques. The objectives of the ISS-MOP project are to measure presence of viral and select bacterial and fungal pathogens and correlate their presence on crew. The effects of the spaceflight environment on viral and microbial pathogen dynamics are largely unknown and represent both a significant gap in our knowledge and an important opportunity to study the presence of microbial populations and predict health risks during long-term space exploration.

The hypothesis of the ISS-MOP project is that certain short-living viruses associated with inert environmental spaces in the closed habitat of the ISS can have an adverse influence on crew health. To test this hypothesis the team will catalog viruses associated with surfaces and air of the ISS modules using the all-encompassing Lawrence Livermore Microbial Detection Array (LLMDA), which contains probes to detect not only viruses but also clinically important human bacterial and fungal pathogens. In addition, skin and saliva samples from crewmembers will be requested to test the hypothesis.

There are three aims proposed in this ISS-MOP project. While the cultivation-based assay (Aim 1) provides a valid estimation of microbial presence, it offers only a limited assessment of the phylogenetic/pathogenic diversity and physiological breadth of the microbial population present. Therefore, the team will use the novel and comprehensive LLMDA microarray, which is specifically designed to elucidate the pathogenic viral and microbial diversity profiles of complex samples to at least the species level (Aim 2). In addition, we will characterize the virulence and antibiotic resistance mechanisms associated with these pathogens from the ISS by developing a new Virulence and Antibiotic Resistance gene array and analyze the samples on this array. The final deliverable of the proposed project to NASA will be the ISS-MOP dataset, a compilation of all of the genomic sequences and genetic information of viruses and microbes encountered on and within the ISS habitat (Aim 3).

The objectives of the ISS-MOP project directly respond to the National Research Council Committee for the Decadal Survey on Biological and Physical Sciences in Space recommendation P1 where it states, “to establish a microbial observatory program on the ISS to conduct long-term, multigenerational studies of microbial population dynamics.” The ISS-MOP project will also provide answers to NASA Space Biology Guiding Questions: How do alterations in gravity affect in microbial cells (CMM1); Does the enhanced virulence or other genetic changes observed with microbial cultures flown in space occur in other species (CMM4); and What are the genetic changes underlying physiological responses to spaceflight demonstrated in biological systems (CMM5).

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: The effects of the spaceflight environment on viral and microbial pathogen dynamics are largely unknown and represent both a significant gap in our knowledge and an important opportunity to study the presence of microbial populations and predict health risks during long-term space exploration. International Space Station – Microbial Observatory of Pathogenic Virus, Bacteria, and Fungi (ISS-MOP) project will generate the microbial censuses associated with crewmembers and various ISS modules using both traditional culture-based methods and state-of-the art molecular techniques to a degree that has not previously been possible.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2018 
Task Progress: Summary of accomplishments:

Flight and sample status: 1). Successfully enrolled four crewmembers to carry out our studies to collect ISS environmental surface wipes, air samples, crew body swabs, and saliva samples for microbiological and viral analysis; 2). ISS environmental samples have been collected over the performing period. Crewmember body swab and saliva samples from preflight, infight, and postflight samples are complete for the first crewmember. Sample collection for crewmembers 2-4 is still ongoing.

Progress towards scientific goals

1. Cultivation-based assay to estimate microbial presence. We have performed microbiological culture of environmental surface wipe and air samples from the first three flights (F4, F5, F6). We started our first flight as F4 as a continuation of the flight name from Microbial Tracking-1 (MT-1, PI: Kasthuri Venkateswaran) project which collected samples from three flights (F1, F2, F3). The surface wipes were plated in three different agar plates, Blood Agar (BA), Reasoner's 2A Agar (R2A), and Potato Dextrose Agar (PDA). The bacterial (grown from BA and R2A) and fungal (grown from PDA) contents from the samples were evaluated using Colony Forming Units (CFU) /m2. Overall, we observed bacterial and fungal growth from surface samples from all three flights. The microbial growth concentration ranged from 10^2 to 10^6 CFU/m2. The culture of air samples did not yield significant growth as compared to negative control samples possibly due to the much lower biomass available from air filters.

2. Molecular analysis to characterize ISS environmental and crew microbial profiles. We performed the Lawrence Livermore Microbial Detection Array (LLMDA) analysis on F4 environmental surface wipes and all of crewmember #1 saliva and body swab samples collected pre-, in-, and post-flight. In total, ~100 samples and controls have been subjected to LLMDA analysis. We also performed metagenomic DNA sequencing of the first two environmental samples (F4 and F5) and the crew 1 samples. We performed bioinformatic analysis of F4, F5, and crew 1 samples to determine any commonality and differences between two flights, between crew and environmental microbiome, and between preflight, inflight, and postflight to determine any diversity changes during spaceflights. We observed that Propionibacterium, Staphylococcus, Rothia, Veillonella, Prevotella are among the top bacteria detected in the samples. The microbial diversity between the three datasets also showed some differences. The last sample collected for crewmember #1, R+180 days, was not included in these results due to time constraints. We are preparing manuscripts to report these findings.

3. ISS-MOP dataset, a compilation of all the genomic sequences and genetic information of viruses and microbes encountered on and within the ISS habitat. We will compile all data generated to date to make this into a dataset and provide to the NASA Data Archive.

Team interactions: 1). Team conducted monthly conference calls to discuss progress and results and operational matters during this entire performing period; 2). ISS-MOP investigators conducted an in-person meeting on June 18, 2018 at Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory (LLNL) to discuss work progress, lessons learned, and publication plans; 3). Team members attended the American Society for Gravitational and Space Research (ASGSR) meeting in Seattle in Oct 2017 to review work progress and present preliminary data.

The PI attended the Human Research Program (HRP) meeting in Houston, TX in January 2018 and presented a poster on the ISS-MOP project.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2017 
Task Progress: During this performing period, we made progress on science definition and science readiness for our flight experiments. We evaluated two air samplers: NIOSH Personal Bioaerosol Cyclone Sampler (PBCS) and Sartorious air sampler (MD8). We selected MD8 due to higher biomass collected and higher efficiency. Additionally, we evaluated the DNA-free gelatinous filter as the filter to collect air samples and verified these filters as DNA free and capable of collecting viable bacteria and fungi. We tested human swab collection procedures and DNA yield and modified collection procures to maximize sample yield. We evaluated the Lawrence Livermore Microbial Detection Array and shotgun metagenomic sequencing tools using previously collected ISS filter and dust samples. This resulted in a manuscript recently submitted to Microbiome. We evaluated the detection of antibiotic resistance genes and virulence genes from DNA sequencing data from previously collected ISS samples. A manuscript is in preparation. We evaluated analysis of viral samples on the Lawrence Livermore Microbial Detection Array using several human clinical and spiked samples. Several herpesviruses were detected.

We presented at Informed Consent Briefing meeting with the Inc 53/54 crew on Sept, 2016 and Inc 55/56 on Feb, 2017 to discuss the project goals and the samples to be collected preflight, inflight, and postflight. Our project enrolled three crew. The first crew’s preflight samples were collected on Nov 2016 and Jan 2017. We worked on IRB (Institutional Review Board) renewal and prepared a Crew Questionnaire and amended IRB on Jan 2017. Operationally, payload implementation was initiated at Ames Research Center (ARC), July 2016. The ERD and Mini-ED were signed in Nov 2016. Baseline Data Collection Plan for Inc 51/52 was signed on Dec 2016. Data Sharing Plan Document for Inc 51/52 was signed Jan 2017.

Regarding project meetings, we have held monthly MT-2 meetings with ARC Project Scientist to update the team on schedule and upcoming milestones, review SVT, and readiness. MT-1 and -2 teams met with the GeneLab team to discuss project data submission in April 2016. ISS-MOP investigators meeting was conducted Feb 2017 at Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) to discuss work progress and current and future research plans.
