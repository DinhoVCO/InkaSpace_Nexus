Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2020 
Task Progress: Final Report, June 16, 2020

SUMMARY

We studied the effects of simulated microgravity and ionizing radiation on the complex process of virus reactivation that is intimately associated with the way cells respond to external stress. Tools to evaluate the effects of radiation and simulated microgravity included: both viral and cellular gene expression, viral infectivity, and viral titer. In addition, this study aimed to increase our understanding of mechanisms of EBV reactivation and therefore had the potential to discover new therapeutic targets for diseases caused in the general population by EBV. Therefore this proposal appropriately addressed ‘Pathogen infectivity and host immunity’ using human blood cells (B lymphocyte cell line infected with EBV) as one of the targeted research topics by the Heart, Lung and Blood Institute (NHLBI) described on p-6 of PAR-09-120: Biomedical research on the International Space Station (Biomed –ISS) ( UH2). The reactivation of all herpesviruses is known to involve 2 components or stages: the initial initiating molecular events that cause reactivation of the latent genomes to progress to the production of reactivating virus, and then the propagation of these reactivating viruses to produce clinical disease. The second part of this process undoubtedly involved overcoming components of the acquired immune response if clinical disease is to result. In order to understand reactivation from a mechanistic point of view, these two processes were examined independently. In the proposed studies, we focused only on the initial events that microgravity and ionizing radiation initiate in latent cells to induce the first stage of (molecular) reactivation.

EBV reactivation may not be completely homologous to VZV and CMV reactivation. The important point here though is that by focusing on EBV, which has a well-established cell culture system of latency, we can study the mechanistic effects of microgravity and ionizing radiation in inducing EBV reactivation. This alone will provide critical and important information regarding the effects of these inducers on EBV reactivation in humans. Clearly, after these molecular events are established in EBV, possible parallel mechanisms in VZV and CMV can be investigated in future studies. Among the many stressors astronauts are exposed to during spaceflight, cosmic radiation may lead to various serious health effects. Specifically, space radiation may contribute to decreased immunity, which has been documented in astronauts during short and long-duration missions, as evidenced by several changes in cellular immunity and plasma cytokine levels. Reactivation of latent herpes viruses, either directly from radiation of latently infected cells and/or from perturbation of the immune system, may result in disease in astronauts. Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) is 1 of the 8 human herpes viruses known to infect more than 90% of human adults and persists for the life of the host without normally causing adverse effects. Reactivation of several latent viruses in astronauts is well-documented, although the mechanism of reactivation is not well understood.

We studied the effect of 4 different types of radiation, (1) 137Cs gamma rays, (2) 150-MeV protons, (3) 600 MeV/n carbon ions, and (4) 600 MeV/n iron ions on the activation of lytic gene transcription and of reactivation of EBV in a latently infected cell line (Akata) at doses of 0.1, 0.5, 1.0, and 2.0 Gy. The data showed that for all doses used in this study, lytic gene transcription was induced and median viral loads were significantly higher for all types of radiation than in corresponding control samples, with the increases detected as early as 4 days post-exposure and generally tapering off at later time points. The viability and size of EBV-infected Akata cells were highly variable and exhibited approximately the same trend in time for all radiation types at 0.1, 0.5, 1.0, and 2.0 Gy. This work shows that reactivation of viruses can occur due to the effect of different types of radiation on latently infected cells in the absence of changes or cytokines produced in the immune system. In general, gamma rays are more effective than protons, carbon ions, and iron ions in inducing latent virus reactivation, though these high energy particles did induce more sustained and later reactivation of EBV lytic gene transcription. These findings also challenge the common relative biologic effectiveness concept that is often used in radiobiology for other end points.

Accomplishments towards Aim 1

Cell Culture of Cells in T-Flasks: The Akata EBV Positive cells were aseptically cultured and gradually expanded in T-75 flasks and eventually T-175 flasks using media and supplements per recommendation of Jeffrey Cohen. Cells were incubated at 370C, 5% CO2, and the flasks were kept in an upright position. Cells were split and fed every four days, once the media had turned yellow, by removing 25 ml of cells and media and replacing it with 25 ml of fresh RPMI 1640 complete media. During early passage numbers of the cells, 1.0e7 cell aliquots were frozen down in case anything catastrophic happened to the cell line.

Optimizing Viewing Cells and Images: T-75 flasks and bioreactors were seeded with 1.0e6 cells/ml, 50 ml of media, while optimizing the experiment. A small aliquot of cells from each bioreactor were added to a corresponding well on a 24 Well Cell Culture Cluster (Costar, Corning, NY) so that the cells could be viewed and photographed using the Nikon TMS Inverted Microscope for each time point at 40X. Cells in T-75 flasks were able to be viewed and photographed by laying the T-75 flasks horizontally on the microscope. All images taken at 40X were saved and captioned.

Optimizing Cell Counts and Viability: For each flask and bioreactor an aliquot of cells were stained at a 1:1 ratio with acridine orange and propidium iodine (AO/PI) and then added to a disposable cell counting chamber (Nexcelom Bioscience, Lawrence, MA). Cell counts, viability, and cell size were evaluated using the Nexcelom designed EBV Assay using a Cellometer Auto 2000.

Aliquots of Cells for Other Labs: It had been predetermined that 1.0e6 cells would be used for testing the viral load of EBV at JSC and that 3.0e6 cells would each be sent to David Bloom’s lab and Raymond Stowe’s lab. From the Cellometer Auto 2000 results, the volumes needed for 1.0e6 cells and 3.0e6 cells were calculated. 3.0E6 cells were aliquoted for both Dr. Raymond Stowe and Dr. David Bloom’s labs. The cells were centrifuged for 15 minutes at 40C and 2000 rpm to obtain a pellet. The cell pellet for Bloom’s lab was resuspended in 300 ul of RNALater RNA Stabilization Reagent at 40C overnight and were then moved and stored in a -800C freezer (Qiagen, Germantown, MD). The cell pellet for Stowe’s lab was resuspended in 200-300 ul of supernatant and was then put in the Mr. Frosty freezing container in the -800C overnight. The next morning the frozen cell suspension was moved out of the freezing container and into a labeled box for Dr. Stowe in the -800C. The 1.0e6 aliquot was for DNA extraction at the JSC lab. After the necessary aliquots were made, additional aliquots were made to be stored in the -80c so that DNA extraction could be repeated if necessary.

Irradiation of Cells: For irradiation the cells were exposed to one of four different types of radiation: 137Cs gamma, proton, iron ion, or carbon ion on day 0. The cells were exposed to an acute dose of 0.1, 0.5, 1.0, or 2.0 Gy at room temperature. Proton, iron ion, and carbon ion irradiation was performed at the Brookhaven National Laboratory’s facilities. 137Cs gamma irradiation was performed at both NASA Johnson Space Center’s and Brookhaven National Laboratory’s facilities. The energies of gamma, proton, iron ion, and carbon ion beams were 150, 150, 600, and 600 MeV/u, respectively.

Shipment of Cells to Brookhaven National Laboratory: The Akata EBV positive cells underwent several trial runs to optimize the cell viability during shipment to and from Brookhaven National Laboratory (BNL). It was determined that the following methods yielded the best viability for the Akata EBV positive cells. On day 0 cell counts and viability were performed and aliquots were made for each lab as previously described. With fresh media, 70 ml of cells were added at a concentration of 1.0e6 cells/ml to a T-25 flask (Costar, Corning, NY). The flask lids were completely tightened and the bottles were sealed with parafilm before being placed back in the incubator at 370C, 5% CO2. On day 1 the cells were removed from the incubator and shipped at room temperature to Brookhaven National Laboratory in Upton, New York. The cells were received at BNL on day 2 and the cells were put in an incubator at 370C, 5% CO2 to recover from the shipping conditions. On day 3 the cells were taken out of the incubator to be irradiated at room temperature. After irradiation, the cells were moved back to an incubator at 370C, 5% CO2 until day 4 when the cells were shipped back to Johnson Space Center at room temperature. On day 5 the cells were received at JSC and were put in an incubator at 370C, 5% CO2 to recover from the shipping conditions. On day 6 the cells were split, aliquoted, fed, and then transferred to the correct cell culture vessel, either a T-75 flask or a 50 ml High Aspect Ratio Vessel (HARV) bioreactor before being returned to the incubator. For graphing purposes, all BNL time points from day 6 to day 22 were adjusted by two days to align with the time points from the JSC portion of the experiments.

DNA Extraction: For DNA Extraction, the ArchivePure DNA Cell/Tissue kits was used and their DNA Purification from 1-2 million cultures cells protocol was modified and optimized (5Prime, Gaithersburg, MD.) The cell pellet and supernatant were separated via centrifugation at 14,000 RPM for 20 seconds. 200 ul of supernatant was added to a separately labeled microcentrifuge tube and 20 ul of residual liquid was used to resuspend the cell pellet in the original microcentrifuge tube. Both the resuspended cell pellet and the supernatant underwent the DNA extraction protocol. The modifications for this protocol include cooling the sample to room temperature (RT) by placing it in ice for 15 minutes rather than 1 minute at the beginning of the protein precipitation steps, centrifuging for 5 minutes at 14,000 RPM for both of the centrifugation steps for DNA precipitation rather than 1 minute, and incubating for 30 minutes at 650C for rapid DNA hydration rather than for 5 minutes.

Nanodrop and QPCR: To determine the concentration of DNA present in each sample, nanodrop was run on each sample the day of the initial quantitative PCR (qPCR) reaction. If for some reason the samples were run again after the samples had been stored at 40C for several weeks then nanodrop was redone on the samples. For determining viral load, qPCR was done using the Fast Reaction plates on the TaqMan 7900HT Fast Real-Time PCR System (Applied Biosystems, Grand Island, NY). A fluorogenic probe, 5’/6-FAM-TGTACACGCACGAGAAATGCGCC-TAMRA/3’, was synthesized from IDT DNA Technologies. The primers, 5’CGGAAGCCCTCTCTGGACTTC-3’ and 5’CCCTGTTTATCCGATGGAATG-3’, from the BALF5 gene were also synthesized from IDT DNA Technologies. The optimized qPCR reaction mix contained 10% of DNA, 1X TaqMan Master Mix, 200 µM each primer, 150 µM probe, and 43.5% H20. The threshold and baseline number for cycles was set to automatic on the program, and each sample was run in duplicate. A standard curve was obtained using BALF5 standards that were serially diluted, and was used to calculate the viral copy number for each reaction well that was run. Normalizing Data from QPCR: To normalize the data, the mean viral copy number from the sample duplicates was calculated. The mean value from each sample was then divided by the concentration determined by Nanodrop for the same sample. After all of the results were expressed as copies of EBV per ng of DNA the values were then converted to represent the percent change from day 0, thus meaning that all values (day 4, day 8, day 12, day 16, and day 20) from a sample were compared to the day 0 value. From the percent changes, the mean from all of the repeats was calculated and then graphed.

Accomplishments towards Aim 2:

Akata EBV Positive cells were seeded at a concentration of 1.0e6 cells/ml in 50 ml of media to a 50 ml high-aspect ratio vessel (HARV) bioreactor (Synthecon, Inc. Houston, TX). The bioreactors were attached to a RCCS base rotator in a 370C, 5% CO2 incubator, and the microgravity environment was modeled by rotating the bioreactors at 10 rotations per minute. There were two different vessel conditions for control 1 x g conditions: an upright T-75 flask with a vented cap (Corning Inc. Corning, NY) and a 50 ml HARV bioreactor in a static horizontal position (Synthecon, Inc. Houston, TX). For the control conditions, the cells were seeded at the same concentration and volume of media, and were grown in normal conditions. Cells were aseptically split and harvested every four days by removing 25 ml of cells and media and replacing it with 25 ml of fresh RPMI 1640 complete media until day 16.

Akata EBV positive cells were evaluated for any changes in cell viability and cell size by an AO/PI stain with the Nexcelom Cellometer. The Akata EBV positive cells did not show any significant change in cell viability when comparing the microgravity conditions to the control conditions. Additionally, the Akata EBV positive cells did not show any significant changes in either total cell size (microns) or live cell size (microns) when comparing the microgravity condition to the control conditions. The Akata EBV positive cell pellet showed a statistically significant decrease in the mean percent change of EBV viral load from day 0 on day 4, day 8, day 12, and day 16 in the modeled microgravity condition; whereas, there was no significant change for the control conditions.

Accomplishments towards Aim 3:

Akata EBV positive cells were irradiated with 137Cs gamma irradiation at both the NASA Johnson Space Center’s and Brookhaven National Laboratory’s facilities. Task Description: We will study the effects of simulated microgravity and ionizing radiation on the complex process of virus reactivation that is intimately associated with the way cells respond to external stress. Tools to evaluate the effects of radiation and simulated microgravity will include: both viral and cellular gene expression, viral infectivity and viral titer, and DNA methylation. In addition, this proposal aims to increase our understanding of mechanisms of Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV) reactivation and therefore, has the potential to discover new therapeutic targets for diseases caused in the general population by EBV. Therefore this proposal appropriately addresses: 1. Ground-based Research Designed to Lead to Space Flight; 2. Cell Biology and Microbiology: This section of the NRA solicits NOVEL ground-based experiments that use cell cultures, tissue cultures, microbiological organisms, or small invertebrate organisms to elucidate the basic mechanisms living systems use to respond to changes in gravity, i.e., how are changes sensed and transduced to alter gene expression and the structure and function of the musculoskeletal system. The reactivation of all herpes viruses is known to involve 2 components or stages: the initial initiating molecular events that cause reactivation of the latent genomes to progress to the production of reactivating virus, and then the propagation of these reactivating viruses to produce clinical disease. The second part of this process undoubtedly involves overcoming components of the acquired immune response if clinical disease is to result. In order to understand reactivation from a mechanistic point of view, these two processes need to be examined independently. In the proposed studies, we will be focusing only on the initial events that microgravity and ionizing radiation initiate in latent cells to induce the first stage of (molecular) reactivation.

EBV reactivation may not be completely homologous to Varicella zoster virus (VZV) and Cytomegalovirus (CMV) reactivation. The important point here though is that by focusing on EBV, which has a well-established cell culture system of latency, we can study the mechanistic effects of microgravity and ionizing radiation in inducing EBV reactivation. This alone will provide critical and important information regarding the effects of these inducers on EBV reactivation in humans. Clearly, after these molecular events are established in EBV, possible parallel mechanisms in VZV and CMV can be investigated in future studies.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: This proposal aims to increase our understanding of mechanisms of Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV) reactivation and therefore, has the potential to discover new therapeutic targets for diseases caused in the general population by EBV.

PROJECT SUMMARY

Unique stressors associated with spaceflight reduce astronaut immunity resulting in the reactivation of latent herpes viruses including Epstein-Barr virus (EBV). EBV is being used as a model for at least 3 other human herpes viruses that reactivation in astronauts during a space mission. Stress induced release of stress hormones such as cortisol, catecholamines, and other stress factors resulting in reduced immunity. Physical stressors, microgravity, and radiation play a major role in the reactivation of EBV. To study the effect of radiations, human EBV infected B-lymphocytes will be exposed to various dosages radiations (Protons) at Brookhaven National Lab, New York, to mimic space radiation exposure to crewmembers. Assays will be performed to study the phenotypic changes and EBV load before and after the radiation exposure.

REPORT OF DELIVERABLES

I) Materials and Methods

Cell Culture of Cells in T-Flasks. The Akata EBV Positive cells and Akata EBV Negative cells were aseptically cultured and gradually expanded in T-75 flasks. Cells were incubated at 37 degrees C, 5% CO2 and the flasks were kept in a vertical position. Cells were split and fed every four days, once the media had turned yellow, by removing 25 ml of cells and media and replacing it with 25 ml of fresh RPMI 1640 complete media. During early passage numbers of the cells, 1.0e7 cell aliquots were cryopreserved.

Cell Culture of Cells in Bioreactors. Akata EBV Positive cells and Akata EBV Negative wells were centrifuged at 100 x g to concentrate the cells so that the cells could be seeded at a concentration of 1.0e6 cells/ml in 50 ml of media to a 50 ml bioreactor (Synthecon, Inc. Houston, TX). Akata EBV Negative cells, which would serve as a negative control during experiments, were treated to identical conditions as the Akata EBV Positive cells. The bioreactors were attached to a RCCS base rotator in a 37 degrees C, 5% CO2 incubator, and the microgravity environment was modeled by rotating the bioreactors at 10 revolutions per minute. Cells were aseptically split every four days by removing 25 ml of cells and media and replacing it with 25 ml of fresh RPMI 1640 complete media.

Optimizing Viewing Cells and Images. T-75 flasks and bioreactors were seeded with 1.0e6 cells/ml, 50 ml of media, while optimizing the experiment. A small aliquot of cells from each bioreactor were added to a corresponding well on a 24 Well Cell Culture Cluster (Costar, Corning, NY) so that the cells could be viewed and photographed using the Nikon TMS Inverted Microscope for each time point at 10X and 40X. Cells in T-75 flasks were able to be viewed and photographed by laying the T-75 flasks horizontally on the microscope. All images taken at 40X were saved and captioned.

Optimizing Cell Counts and Viability. For each flask or bioreactor 20 ul of cells were mixed with 20 ul of AO/PI staining solution (Nexcelom Bioscience, Lawrence, MA) and added to a disposable cell counting chamber. Nexcelom specifically designed an assay, named EBV Assay, for the Akata cell line and this experiment. Using the Cellometer Auto 2000 and the EBV Assay cell counts, cell concentrations, cell sizes, and viability were obtained. All data from the Cellometer Auto 2000 was recorded and saved.

DNA Extraction and PCR Amplification. For DNA Extraction, the ArchivePure DNA Cell/Tissue kits were used. DNA Purification from 1-2 million cultures cells protocol was modified and optimized (5Prime, Gaithersburg, MD.) The cell pellet and supernatant were separated via centrifugation at 14,000 RPM for 20 seconds. 200 ul of supernatant was added to a separately labeled microtube and 20 ul of residual liquid was used to resuspend the cell pellet. Both the resuspended cell pellet the supernatant underwent the DNA extraction protocol. The modifications for this protocol include cooling the sample to RT by placing it in ice for 15 minutes rather than 1 minute at the beginning of the protein precipitation steps, centrifuging for 5 minutes at 14,000 RPM for both of the centrifugation steps for DNA precipitation rather than 1 minute, and incubating for 30 minutes at 65 degrees C for rapid DNA hydration rather than for 5 minutes.

To determine the concentration of DNA present in each sample, nanodrop was run on each sample the day of the initial qPCR reaction. If for some reason the samples were run again after the samples had been stored at 4 degrees C for several weeks then nanodrop was repeated on the samples. For determining viral load, qPCR was performed using the Fast Reaction plates on the TaqMan 7900. A fluorogenic probe 5’/6-FAM-TGTACACGCACGAGAAATGCGCC-TAMRA/3’ was synthesized from IDT DNA Technologies. The primers, 5’CGGAAGCCCTCTCTGGACTTC-3’ and 5’CCCTGTTTATCCGATGGAATG-33, from the BALF5 gene were also synthesized from IDT DNA Technologies. The optimized qPCR reaction mix contained 10% of DNA, 1X TaqMan Master Mix, 200 uM each primer, 150 uM probe and 43.5% H2O. The threshold and baseline number for cycles was set to automatic on the program. A standard curve, obtained by serially diluting the BALF5 standards, was used to calculate the viral copy number for each reaction.

II) RESULTS

Overall, the data show that radiation exposure disrupts EBV from its latent state (i.e., reactivation), and supports the concept that radiation exposure during spaceflight may pose an additional hazard to crewmembers during long-duration missions.

EBV reactivation may not be completely homologous to Varicella zoster virus (VZV) and Cytomegalovirus (CMV) reactivation. The important point here though is that by focusing on EBV, which has a well-established cell culture system of latency, we can study the mechanistic effects of microgravity and ionizing radiation in inducing EBV reactivation. This alone will provide critical and important information regarding the effects of these inducers on EBV reactivation in humans. Clearly, after these molecular events are established in EBV, possible parallel mechanisms in VZV and CMV can be investigated in future studies.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: This proposal aims to increase our understanding of mechanisms of Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV) reactivation and therefore, has the potential to discover new therapeutic targets for diseases caused in the general population by EBV.
