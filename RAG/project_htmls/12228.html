Task Description: A major impediment to long-duration space travel is loss of muscle mass and strength during spaceflight. Surprisingly, the small worm Caenorhabditis elegans is a good, cost-effective model for studying the effects of spaceflight upon muscle. Using this device, on Earth, we have shown that defects in muscle attachment complexes lead to decline in worm muscle strength. Motivated by these past flight data and ground results, we propose to (a) demonstrate the flight-readiness of our miniaturized device for quantitative characterization of muscle strength of C. elegans, (b) test our hypothesis, based on past flight data, that muscle strength decreases in flight as a result of altered muscle gene expression in the worm, and (c) quantify muscle strength loss in spaceflight across multiple worm generations. Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2023 
Task Progress: A major impediment to long-duration space travel is loss of muscle mass and strength during spaceflight. Surprisingly, the microscopic nematode Caenorhabditis elegans (C.elegans) is a good, cost-effective model for studying the effects of spaceflight upon muscle. Our past experiments on Earth and the International Space Station (ISS) have shown that the expression of key muscle genes that encode members of a muscle attachment complex is reduced in C.elegans during spaceflight. A key open question is if and how these changes in gene expression result in reduced muscle strength in response to spaceflight. While reduced force production is documented for astronauts, it is yet to be demonstrated for C.elegans.

To address this critical knowledge gap, we developed a miniaturized device dubbed "NemaFlex" that enables continuous measurement of worm muscle forces based on deflection of soft micropillars. We integrated the NemaFlex device into a worm loading apparatus, so that gravid adult animals of a specific size can be sorted from mixed cultures and evaluated for muscle strength in individual chambers. The spaceflight hardware and worm culture bags were flown as the Micro-16 experiment to the ISS on February 20, 2021, as part of the Northrop Grumman Cygnus NG-15 cargo mission. Experiments were successfully conducted with the crew recording videos of worms deflecting the micropillars in the NemaFlex device. The key results from the spaceflight investigation are: (1) The body diameter of wild-type worms decreased compared to ground controls, potentially due to altered metabolism; (2) The muscle strength of space-adapted worms was lower than that of corresponding worms cultured on ground. Thus, the Micro-16 experiment showed that muscle strength decline occurs in a physiologically relevant animal model, similar to that found in astronauts.

The scientific impact of this study is significant as it opens avenues for future investigations to identify drugs that could alleviate the muscle strength decline in space. This spaceflight project contributed to training of more than a dozen personnel, including students and postdocs, on C. elegans, microfluidics and space biology. The funded work resulted in 16 peer-reviewed publications, several student awards, outreach to K-12 students and commercialization of new technologies.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2020 
Task Progress: Progress report duration: 05/19/2019 – 04//2020

During this project period, the Principal Investigator's (PI’s) team made progress in the following areas: (1) Pre-Experiment Verification Test (EVT) progress, (2) EVT trial, (3) Post-EVT science testing.

Pre-EVT progress

Worm culture: We have optimized the conditions for worm culture in the fluorinated ethylene propylene (FEP) bags with culture volumes of 20 mL. We found that an inoculum concentration of 1000 larvae/mL is sufficient for sub-cultures and for animals to grow within the temperature range of 16C – 20C. These culture conditions allow a maximum launch delay of 7 days.

NemaFlex device: We have designed and optimized the NemaFlex chamber design to enable size-based sorting of animals from the unsynchronized cultures present in the FEP bags. We have also developed a protocol for loading worm cultures into this device that now is capable of providing strength data from atleast 30 gravid adults.

We have worked with BioServe to build the worm loading apparatus (WLA) that integrates the NemaFlex device. The basic version of the apparatus was tested in the PI’s laboratory and recommendations have been provided to BioServe for improvements to be made. The improved WLA was tested in the EVT trial.

Multigenerational studies: The PI team has conducted multigenerational studies in their laboratory. Under these culture conditions we measured strength of wild-type and dys-1 animals. Our results show that the strength of wild-type and dys-1 animals do not change over the 7 generations studied.

EVT trial at BioServe

Multigenerational growth studies were conducted at BioServe with the goal of measuring muscle strength of wild-type and dys-1 animals.

Post EVT science testing

Testing with new C. elegans Maintenance Medium (CeMM). We started testing with new CeMM to assess culture health and muscle strength. The culture density of the animals was between 1000 – 1350 adults/mL, which meets the ERD success criterion. The muscle strength of animals at timepoints of weeks 1, 3, 5, and 7 also did not show any difference for wildtype animals. Multigenerational studies for dys-1 are pending.

[Ed. note October 2020: Compiled from PI progress report submitted to NASA Ames]

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2018 
Task Progress: Following our suggested ground testing timeline, we have made significant progress on many aspects of our project, including culturing of selected nematode strains in the axenic media, making strength measurements of wild-type and mutant nematodes cultured in the axenic media, and culturing of nematodes in axenic media in fluorinated ethylene propylene (FEP) bags. We also pursued various spin-off projects related to our original proposal during the lag in hardware acquisition from BioServe.

Previously we described successful culturing in the axenic media with the wild-type animal as well as the unc-112 and unc-52 muscular mutants suggested for use in spaceflight. Based on recent experiments, however, we believe the dys-1 mutant strain is also of interest. The dys-1 gene of C. elegans has been studied previously during spaceflight. Expression of dys-1 significantly increases during spaceflight, and a more recent study has shown that gene expression is not affected as much in dys-1 mutants as in wild-type worms during spaceflight. The previous research with dys-1 in space as well as new findings from our own lab led us to also start culturing dys-1 in the axenic media as well.

Using an optimized NemaFlex platform for measuring the strength of C. elegans, we previously obtained strength measurements for age-synchronized wild-type animals at various time points. During the flight, animals will not be age-synchronized but rather in a mixed population of worms of all different ages. Thus, we need to be able to select for worms of a desired approximate age. The criteria for selecting nematodes of the appropriate developmental stage (young adult) were tested on wild-type and unc-52 animals in these devices. The young adult animals were selected based on the fact that they contain oocytes, making them distinct from larval animals, and also that their diameters have not reached near the maximum limit of growth yet as seen in older adult animals. To test the robustness of our selection criteria, we compared young adult animals selected from the mixed population inside the device with animals of the predicted same age from an age-synchronized population of young adults. The strength values for wild-type age-synchronized animals and mixed population animals agreed well, with no significant difference between the two, indicating that the selection criteria work well for wild-type animals. However, there was some disparity between the unc-52 animals that were age-synchronized and mixed, and the mixed-age unc-52 mutants were not discernibly different from wild-type animals. This indicates that unc-52 strength may be difficult to discern from wild-type animals since the mutation does not severely affect the muscle function. Unc-52 animals of the age-synchronized population are also skinnier on average than the mixed population, meaning that the animals we selected to analyze in the mixed population may have been slightly too old; this can be fixed by setting a diameter cutoff to exclude animals that are too fat. We expect that unc-112 or dys-1 will be better mutants to study in this context because they are much weaker than both wild-type and unc-52 in our E. coli-based studies.

Because we had not received BioCells from BioServe during 2017, we decided to explore alternate culturing vessels. We utilized the Origen PermaLife Cell Culture Bags-PL07 (12mL maximum volume). We injected 2500 larval stage 1 animals into the culture bag, and within 7 days (the approximate time to reach adulthood), the population size had grown to 15,000, which was comparable to animals grown in a petri dish. This indicates that there was proper oxygen exchange and favorable conditions for animals to grow. Culture bags have been utilized in the past for spaceflight studies with C. elegans, so this type of FEP bag is a good option for a culture vessel during spaceflight. We expect that animals will also culture well in the VueLife bags that we recently acquired from BioServe.

During the downtime in which we were not sent the hardware we needed to continue our ground studies for the spaceflight experiment, we pursued three main side projects: 1) investigating dystrophin mutants for strength defects and screening drugs to improve strength, 2) looking at effects on muscle strength from starvation or diet restriction (axenic media) when transferring worms away from E. coli at the beginning of adulthood, and 3) analyzing fatty acid composition using FTIR (Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy) in worms fed axenically.

The dystrophin protein is important for proper muscle function in both humans and C. elegans. In humans, mutations in the dystrophin gene can lead to severe muscle disorders including Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy. Additionally, the dys-1 gene in C. elegans plays an important role in muscle function and is differentially regulated during spaceflight. We hypothesized that dys-1 mutants of C. elegans would be detectably weaker in our NemaFlex devices, which we did observe in the BZ33 strain of dys-1 for adults older than 1 day. We also tested whether drugs known to improve certain aspects of muscle health could alleviate the strength loss in dys-1 mutants. Both prednisone, the standard compound for treating muscular dystrophy symptoms in humans, and melatonin improved muscle strength in C. elegans dys-1 mutants. This indicates dys-1 as an interesting mutant for studying loss of muscle strength related to diseases and spaceflight, and it also validates NemaFlex as a useful platform for testing the efficacy of compounds in treating the loss of muscle strength. This platform may be useful in future studies for testing drugs that could help to prevent loss of muscle strength in astronauts.

To better understand how muscle structure and function changes with different diets, we investigated how animal strength changes when transferred from an E. coli diet at the end of development (Day 1 Adult) to either a starvation condition of buffer or to axenic media. Animals kept on the E. coli diet through Day 2 of adulthood continued to grow in strength, while those transferred to buffer or axenic media lost strength. This further validates that axenic media is distinct from a starvation condition and may better resemble a dietary restriction condition. This also demonstrates that muscle strength is strongly affected by the animal’s diet.

Finally, we explored using Fourier Transform Infrared (FTIR) imaging to detect changes in C. elegans’ diet. We utilized the axenic media with and without a supplemented fatty acid called Eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA). The use of axenic media helped to prevent confounding the data by the fatty acids present in E. coli. We showed that distinct peaks representing fatty acids were present in wild-type animals but absent or reduced in fat-3 and tub-1 mutants that are not able to synthesize certain fatty acids. Supplementing the diets of fat-3 and tub-1 animals with EPA changes the FTIR spectra to indicate changes in chemical composition of the animal.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2017 
Task Progress: Following our suggested ground testing timeline, we have made significant progress on many aspects of our project, including acquisition of axenic media, culturing of selected nematode strains in the new media, characterization of nematode growth, strength measurements of nematodes, and development of analysis software.

After reaching out to a number of companies for quotes on the custom media, C. elegans maintenance media (CeMM), we found a company, Cell Guidance Systems, able to make the media following our custom formulation at a very affordable rate relative to the cost of this custom media purchased by individuals in the past. Once NASA released funding to us, we were able to place the order and obtain the media in about 6 weeks, as is a customary turnaround time for custom media. We also kept some special order chemicals on hand with Cell Guidance Systems for any future orders of CeMM that may be necessary. Having made this connection with a reliable media manufacturing company, we have established the groundwork essential for current and future experiments that rely on use of this special media.

Introducing C. elegans into this axenic media is a process met with difficulties, especially for first time users. Given that the media is extremely nutrient dense, the inadvertent addition of any bacteria or foreign microorganisms will lead to a very severe contamination that is obvious to the naked eye within a few days. The nematode must initially be transferred from its typical culture of agar and E. coli to CeMM, so cross contamination can be difficult to avoid. After some initial contamination issues, we successfully started cultures in the axenic media with the wild-type animal as well as the unc-112 and unc-52 muscular mutants suggested for use in spaceflight. The cultures are currently being maintained in 60 mm petri dishes with a media volume of 2.5 mL to allow sufficient oxygenation and minimize usage of media until BioCells become available for culture maintenance. Approximately every 2-3 weeks, a 100 µL aliquot of a crowded culture is introduced to a fresh 2.5 mL of CeMM to prevent overcrowding and starvation of a culture. As per the literature suggestions, streptomycin and kanamycin are used at concentrations of 200 µg/mL and 20 µg/mL, respectively.

Wild-type animals were characterized for their growth in the media to ensure that they were developing in a manner consistent with that reported in the published literature. Literature on CeMM-grown worms reports growth data from worms on the front end of the population (i.e., those growing the most), while our data represents the average animal in the culture at the time point. Considering this slight difference, growth data and trends are consistent with those previously reported in the literature. Some animals in the population reach maturity beginning on Day 7, with the last animals becoming mature on Day 11. This wide variation among individual animals is also consistent with CeMM-cultured nematodes from previously published studies.

The animals grown in CeMM are much thinner in their diameters compared to animals cultured in the standard bacterial environment. Because our NemaFlex platform is developed for worms of specific size ranges, a new device was designed and fabricated for use specifically with young adult nematodes cultured in CeMM. While the NemaFlex device used for young adult animals cultured in E. coli features a pillar edge-to-edge spacing of around 60 µm, the newly designed device has an edge-to-edge spacing of 40 µm. This tighter spacing ensures that the skinnier CeMM-cultured worms are adequately challenged by the pillars in the microfluidic environment. The closer contact of the worm with the pillar environment ensures that the animals are able to deflect the pillars rather than just moving past them with minimal contact.

After development of this optimized NemaFlex platform, we obtained strength measurements for wild-type animals at various time points. These strength measurements were done on an age-synchronized population beginning at a time point where approximately half of the worms had reach maturity (Day 9 of life) through the time when all worms reached adulthood (Day 11 of life). Animals across this time period were not significantly different from one another in their strength. This indicates that strength values for worms of slightly different ages (Day 9-11) can be grouped together as a population. This becomes important in future work when only the approximate, not exact age, of a worm is known when selecting for worms from a population of mixed ages.

For comparison purposes, we looked at the strength values of CeMM-cultured nematodes alongside nematodes cultured in bacteria that were at approximately the same developmental stage. Because the worms are drastically different in size, and we have previously found a cubic dependence on the diameter of the worm, we scaled the strength of the worms by d^3. Accounting for the different sizes of the worms may be necessary if worms cultured in space are smaller on average than worms cultured on Earth. Even after accounting for the size of the worm, animals cultured in CeMM are significantly weaker than their E. coli-cultured counterparts (p<0.05 for all time points). This could potentially be a result of minimal surface tension in the liquid axenic media, a very different environment than the solid agar with E. coli., or simply from the different nutritional source. The data has potentially interesting implications in muscle development research.

The other area where major improvements were made was the in-house built image processing software. Our software is designed to track the deflections of the pillars that the nematode comes into contact with. Some fundamental changes were made to the algorithm that led to a >2.5x increase in processing speed of the image sequences, and some changes were made to the user interface as well to increase user friendliness. The main change to the algorithm involved the implementation of a two-step identification process of the pillars for tracking. For this, a circular Hough transform followed by grid verification improved the speed and accuracy of pillar tracking. The improvements to the user interface include easier visualization and real-time tracking of pillars being deflect by the worm. Pillars that the worm comes into contact with at some point during the duration of the image sequence are shown in blue, while pillars that are currently being deflected by the worm are circled in red. The user can therefore visualize the pillar number and location as the worm is deflecting them. This makes validation of the data during the processing step much more user friendly.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2016 
Task Progress: To measure the muscle strength of C. elegans, we developed a microfluidic platform called NemaFlex. The goal of this project is to use NemaFlex on ISS to test the hypothesis that muscles are weaker in space-grown worms. During the flight definition meeting, the tasks were grouped into those that are hardware independent and those that are dependent on hardware provided by BioServe – the implementation partner for our project. The summary of our Year 1’s progress is that we have basically wrapped up hardware-independent tasks. The hardware-dependent tasks could not be completed due to lack of funding support to BioServe and uncertainty in availability of flight opportunity. The next step is to determine if a flight opportunity is available for our project, and according to this timeline initiate hardware-dependent studies. Below we list the hardware-dependent tasks for Year 1 and the progress made.

1) Liquid CeMM Procurement: The first steps of our groundwork in validating the nematode for flight studies involve culturing C. elegans in an axenic medium called C. elegans Maintenance Medium (CeMM). In order to begin any ground experiments, the media must therefore first be acquired. CeMM is a complicated and expensive media that has to be custom made. Due to a delay in receiving NASA funding for purchasing supplies (received January 20, 2016), there was a subsequent delay in obtaining the media. Once funds were obtained, the order was placed for the custom medium, which takes about 2 months to manufacture, test, and be received. The media was obtained on April 6, 2016, which came after the deadline for the first annual report. Therefore significant progress was not possible during the time frame of Year 1 progress report. However, we have made progress in using CeMM media in culturing nematodes as part of Year 2 effort and strength measurements are currently ongoing with worms cultured in CeMM media.

2) Light microscopy module (LMM) compatibility: We visited the Glenn Research Center and learned about the capabilities of the LMM module. LMM hardware uses an upright microscope which is not conducive for our experiments. BioServe has an inverted microscope, which appears to be suitable, although we have not done any testing with our specimens and devices.

3) Software development: We have made considerable progress in speeding up the code for analyzing pillar deflections in the NemaFlex device. Strength analysis on a population of 20+ animals now takes only 1 hour. The software development necessary for this project has been done.

4) Influence of strength on worm diameter: The ability to account for the size of the animal becomes important in a few different aspects of the spaceflight project. The key factor to consider is that animals grown in space may be skinnier than their control grown on Earth that experience normal gravity conditions. We will want to ensure that we are measuring strength changes that result from changes at the genetic level affecting muscle functionality rather than measuring changes in strength due to a change in worm size. Some other applications for this scaling relationship include differences in diameter between wild-type animals and some mutants that may be sent to space, as well as being able to account for the size of the animal in a population of worms of different ages.

For worms of varying sizes but the same age, we expected to see a relationship with the output force that was measured. To investigate this, we examined both the effects of nematode length and diameter on the strength detected. To first look at the effect of animal length, we utilized a mutant nematode called lon-2, which has a similar diameter to the wild-type animal but reaches a length of approximately 1.4x that of the wild-type animal at the young adult stage. For worms of the same diameter but different lengths, we found that there was no significant difference in the strengths of the animals, therefore allowing us to determine that animal length does not play a significant role in the strength of the animal. We next looked at how the strength changed across worms of different diameters from both the wild-type and lon-2 populations. We found that strength varies as a cubic function of body diameter. We then looked at wild-type animals at different ages during their development and were able to confirm this same cubic dependence. Thus to remove the effects of diameter, strength should be normalized by the diameter cubed. This aspect will be incorporated into our software.

Motivated by these past flight data and ground results, we propose to (a) demonstrate the flight-readiness of our miniaturized device for quantitative characterization of muscle strength of C. elegans, (b) test our hypothesis, based on past flight data, that muscle strength decreases in flight as a result of altered muscle gene expression in the worm, and (c) quantify muscle strength loss in spaceflight across multiple worm generations. Successful measurements of muscle strength in flight will make C. elegans a much more complete and comprehensive genomic model for muscle physiology, enabling meaningful translation of discoveries made in C. elegans to astronauts. Our proposed studies have important spin out implications for sarcopenia (an urgent public health problem on Earth) and may produce a terrestrial model for testing potential therapeutic interventions for muscle strength decline in flight.
