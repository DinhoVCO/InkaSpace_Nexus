Flight Assignment/Project Notes: Ground-based spaceflight analogue

NOTE: Extended to 12/31/2023 per NSSC information (Ed., 3/17/24)

NOTE: Extended to 12/31/2022 per F. Hernandez/ARC (Ed., 7/27/21)

NOTE: Extended to 12/31/2021 per NSSC information (Ed., 3/8/21)

NOTE: Extended to 12/31/2020 per NSSC information (Ed., 7/20/2020)

NOTE: Extended to 4/30/2020 per NSSC information (Ed., 6/26/18)

NOTE: Extended to 5/31/2019 per F. Hernandez/ARC (Ed., 5/8/18)

Task Description: While unexpected responses in microorganisms are well documented for short duration culture, the impact of long duration, multigenerational growth of microorganisms in the chronic stress of microgravity has not been investigated. The implications of this gap in knowledge are tremendous, as the response to short duration growth in microgravity alters gene expression, pathogenesis-related stress responses (ex., biofilm formation and resistance to antibiotics), and virulence. Moreover, it is known that stress responses (including chronic stress) can increase mutagenesis in bacteria leading to genomic plasticity, which may provide novel mechanistic insight into genomic instability underlying resistance to antibiotics, pathogenicity, and virulence. We propose that long-term growth in spaceflight culture will induce heritable genomic and epigenetic changes in microorganisms with adverse phenotypic outcomes that could have a profound impact on the risk to crew health and vehicle system performance. Our previous studies aboard the Space Shuttle investigated the response of the model enteric pathogen, Salmonella enterica serovar Typhimurium (S. Typhimurium) to short duration spaceflight culture and demonstrated a significant increase in virulence in a murine model of infection, in addition to biofilm formation, as compared to identically grown cultures on the ground. In addition, global microarray and proteomic analyses revealed that 167 transcripts (including small noncoding RNAs/ncRNAs) and 73 proteins changed expression, with the evolutionarily conserved protein Hfq identified as a likely global regulator involved in the response to this environment. Hfq binds to small ncRNAs thereby facilitating their association with mRNAs, the outcome of which plays a diverse role in regulating gene expression, virulence, stress responses, antibiotic resistance, DNA methylation, and genome stability. We hypothesize that long-term multigenerational culture of S. Typhimurium in the spaceflight environment will result in stress-induced mutations and genomic instability as reflected in changes in the genomic, epigenetic, transcriptomic, and select phenotypic characteristics (including virulence) that will reflect a central role for Hfq as a global regulator of these heritable responses. To address this hypothesis, we propose to characterize the impact of long term spaceflight culture (at least 100 generations) of an S. Typhimurium wild type and hfq mutant on the genomic, epigenetic, transcriptomic, virulence, and pathogenesis-related characteristics of these strains. This proposal addresses Research Emphasis 2 - ISS (International Space Station) Microbial Observatory Studies from section B.3 of this announcement, as it is designed to “take advantage of the technological maturity, low cost, and speed of genomic analyses and the rapid generation time of microbes to monitor the evolution of microbial genomic changes in response to the selective pressures present in the spaceflight environment.” In addition, by employing genomic, transcriptomic, and epigenomic analysis, this proposal also addresses the Research Emphasis 1 – Spaceflight Omics Studies in section B.2. In its design, this proposal will address the NASA Space Biology Plan, Overarching Questions CMM-1a, CMM-1d, and CMM5. This proposal also directly addresses the highest priority recommendations from the Decadal Survey for the establishment of an ISS Microbial Observatory conducting long-term, multigenerational studies of microbial population dynamics (Priority P1). In addition, this study analyzes “microbial growth and physiological responses to the multiple stimuli encountered in spaceflight environments,” as described by the panel in Priority P2.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: This research will enrich life on Earth through the use of space technology and the application of biomedical knowledge. Specifically, this study will utilize the microgravity spaceflight platform to 1) to broaden our knowledge of the host-pathogen interaction that leads to infectious disease, and 2) for the development of new therapeutic strategies to combat infectious disease for the general public.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2024 
Task Progress: • Intriguing biofilm formation was observed over time for both the wild type and hfq mutant cultures grown under both long-term low shear modeled microgravity (LSMMG) and control conditions.

• Differences in pathogenesis-related phenotypes in both the wild type and hfq mutant were observed over time under both LSMMG and control conditions.

• We are currently in the process of finishing up data analysis for the -omics, stress responses, imaging, and virulence data, and anticipate submitting a manuscript for publication in the near future.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2020 
Task Progress: To accommodate a switch from automated hardware operation to crew manual performance of our experiment on orbit, we successfully completed an SVT (science verification test) to confirm that the Principal Investigator (PI) team can grow and manually transfer Salmonella (wild type/WT and hfq mutant/MT) in the same bags for use in the Exploring Virulence and Omics during Long-term Viability Experiments in Spaceflight (EVOLVES) flight hardware in a fully enclosed container for up to 12 bags (per strain).

• Successfully performed experiment verfication test (EVT) EVT-3, which was done to confirm that the PI team can grow and manually transfer Salmonella (WT and MT) in bags housed within sealed Techshot cassettes for up to 24 bags (per strain).

• Successful post-EVT optimization to finalize growth times/bag transfer times and nucleic acid yield/integrity for WT and MT.

• Excellent RNA and DNA yield and integrity, viable cultures recovered from glycerol – successfully passed all aspects of manual EVT-3.

• At NASA’s request to return to automated hardware due to crew time limitations, we performed two engineering tests to optimize updated hardware. Our second test was conducted after COVID-19 limitations on access to laboratory research, which preluded our ability to shake the cultures after bag to bag transfer – however, we successfully met the EVT requirement for the number of bacterial inoculum transfers of the WT strain under static conditions – with excellent turbidity in all bags in one cassette.

• Currently preparing for Engineering Test 3 and EVT-3, which will be run simultaneously.

• Projected to launch on NG-14 (Northrop Grumman CRS Flight 14), Oct 30, 2020.

Task Description: While unexpected responses in microorganisms are well documented for short duration culture, the impact of long duration, multigenerational growth of microorganisms in the chronic stress of microgravity has not been investigated. The implications of this gap in knowledge are tremendous, as the response to short duration growth in microgravity alters gene expression, pathogenesis-related stress responses (ex., biofilm formation and resistance to antibiotics), and virulence. Moreover, it is known that stress responses (including chronic stress) can increase mutagenesis in bacteria leading to genomic plasticity, which may provide novel mechanistic insight into genomic instability underlying resistance to antibiotics, pathogenicity, and virulence. We propose that long-term growth in spaceflight culture will induce heritable genomic and epigenetic changes in microorganisms with adverse phenotypic outcomes that could have a profound impact on the risk to crew health and vehicle system performance. Our previous studies aboard the Space Shuttle investigated the response of the model enteric pathogen, Salmonella enterica serovar Typhimurium (S. Typhimurium) to short duration spaceflight culture and demonstrated a significant increase in virulence in a murine model of infection, in addition to biofilm formation, as compared to identically grown cultures on the ground. In addition, global microarray and proteomic analyses revealed that 167 transcripts (including small noncoding RNAs/ncRNAs) and 73 proteins changed expression, with the evolutionarily conserved protein Hfq identified as a likely global regulator involved in the response to this environment. Hfq binds to small ncRNAs thereby facilitating their association with mRNAs, the outcome of which plays a diverse role in regulating gene expression, virulence, stress responses, antibiotic resistance, DNA methylation, and genome stability. We hypothesize that long-term multigenerational culture of S. Typhimurium in the spaceflight environment will result in stress-induced mutations and genomic instability as reflected in changes in the genomic, epigenetic, transcriptomic, and select phenotypic characteristics (including virulence) that will reflect a central role for Hfq as a global regulator of these heritable responses. To address this hypothesis, we propose to characterize the impact of long term spaceflight culture (at least 100 generations) of an S. Typhimurium wild type and hfq mutant on the genomic, epigenetic, transcriptomic, virulence, and pathogenesis-related characteristics of these strains. This proposal addresses Research Emphasis 2 - ISS (International Space Station) Microbial Observatory Studies from section B.3 of this announcement, as it is designed to “take advantage of the technological maturity, low cost, and speed of genomic analyses and the rapid generation time of microbes to monitor the evolution of microbial genomic changes in response to the selective pressures present in the spaceflight environment.” In addition, by employing genomic, transcriptomic, and epigenomic analysis, this proposal also addresses the Research Emphasis 1 – Spaceflight Omics Studies in section B.2. In its design, this proposal will address the NASA Space Biology Plan, Overarching Questions CMM-1a, CMM-1d, and CMM5. This proposal also directly addresses the highest priority recommendations from the Decadal Survey for the establishment of an ISS Microbial Observatory conducting long-term, multigenerational studies of microbial population dynamics (Priority P1). In addition, this study analyzes “microbial growth and physiological responses to the multiple stimuli encountered in spaceflight environments,” as described by the panel in Priority P2.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: This research will enrich life on Earth through the use of space technology and the application of biomedical knowledge. Specifically, this study will utilize the microgravity spaceflight platform to 1) to broaden our knowledge of the host-pathogen interaction that leads to infectious disease, and 2) for the development of new therapeutic strategies to combat infectious disease for the general public.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2019 
Task Progress: · Performed a series of science verification tests (SVTs) before experiment verfication test (EVT)-1, with both the wild type and mutant Salmonella strains to confirm successful culture transfers of the targeted volume of bacterial inocula from bag-to-bag. The hardware continued to be updated, optimized, and tested as needed. Worked closely with our Techshot hardware team to confirm fidelity and biocompatibility of the hardware updates, including those involved with the pumps (pump function before and after autoclaving, flow rate and duration to ensure full transfer of the desired bacterial inocula into each bag), biocompatibility of new tubing, and established degassing procedures to minimize the potential for bubble formation in the culture medium during and after loading into flight bags.

· Performed EVT-1. Successfully validated science procedures for pre-flight preparation of bacterial inocula, culture media, and fixatives. Successfully loaded bacterial culture media into flight hardware and had success with the first few bags within each cassette. Encountered challenges during EVT-1 with bacterial transfers from bag-to-bag with each transfer. The mutant strain faced the biggest challenge.

· Performed a series of post-EVT-1 tests, including live imaging of bacterial growth and inocula mixing tests. These tests confirmed that lack of mixing (and not low oxygen) was the issue with bag-to-bag bacteria transfer, where less and less bacteria were being transferred each time. This was particularly challenging for the mutant as it has low motility. To address this challenge, we successfully established a high fidelity mixing protocol to be performed just prior to transfer of culture to a new bag.

· EVT-2 to start in July 2019.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2018 
Task Progress: Bacterial growth curves performed under lower aeration (closed capped tubes) since discovering that the oxygen concentrations would dip in the flight hardware. Confirmed that neither the wt or hfq mutant reach death phase under lower oxygen conditions (closed capped tubes) through six days.

Mixing tests in FEP bags (similar to flight hardware) from Techshot – found that mixing would not be a major issue using the hfq mutant (low motility) as a worst-case scenario.

Assessed the potential for bubble formation by Salmonella under microaerobic and anaerobic oxygen conditions.

We engineered our own Tupperware prototype of the testbed to initiate bacterial growth curves under low oxygen conditions anticipated in the flight hardware. We used this prototype until Techshot was able to deliver their testbed to our lab.

Hosted Techshot in our research lab at Arizona State University (ASU) to install preliminary Exploring Virulence and Omics during Long-term Viability Experiments in Spaceflight (EVOLVES) test bed.

Reviewed the Interface Design Description (IDD) and had extensive conversations regarding hardware requirements for the experiment. We decided on a volume transfer error between bags.

We worked with the test bed on basic procedures -- loading, cleaning, etc.

We completed growth curve testing to assess the impact of variable volume transfers of bacterial cultures between bags on the growth of S. Typhimurium (wt and mutant). Based on these results, we provided Techshot with a volume transfer error wherein no major alterations to the growth curve were observed in this range. Based on these growth curves, we estimate that we will transfer cultures at 48 hours for all bags. This should result in a 48 day experiment from start to finish per strain.

Participation in TechShot’s internal design peer review in December, 2017.

Launch scrub scenario testing: Tested stability and efficacy of antibiotic in the growth media. Based on our testing, the antibiotic is stable in the media throughout the duration of the experiment and well past. Determined timing to thaw bacterial glycerol stocks, ~30 mins (will be doubled for on orbit thawing). ERD (experiment requirements document) completed and submitted to NASA.

The implications of this gap in knowledge are tremendous, as the response to short duration growth in microgravity alters gene expression, pathogenesis-related stress responses (ex, biofilm formation and resistance to antibiotics), and virulence. Moreover, it is known that stress responses (including chronic stress) can increase mutagenesis in bacteria leading to genomic plasticity, which may provide novel mechanistic insight into genomic instability underlying resistance to antibiotics, pathogenicity, and virulence. We propose that long-term growth in spaceflight culture will induce heritable genomic and epigenetic changes in microorganisms with adverse phenotypic outcomes that could have a profound impact on the risk to crew health and vehicle system performance. Our previous studies aboard the Space Shuttle investigated the response of the model enteric pathogen, Salmonella enterica serovar Typhimurium (S. Typhimurium) to short duration spaceflight culture and demonstrated a significant increase in virulence in a murine model of infection, in addition to biofilm formation, as compared to identically grown cultures on the ground. In addition, global microarray and proteomic analyses revealed that 167 transcripts (including small noncoding RNAs/ncRNAs) and 73 proteins changed expression, with the evolutionarily conserved protein Hfq identified as a likely global regulator involved in the response to this environment. Hfq binds to small ncRNAs thereby facilitating their association with mRNAs, the outcome of which plays a diverse role in regulating gene expression, virulence, stress responses, antibiotic resistance, DNA methylation, and genome stability. We hypothesize that long-term multigenerational culture of S. Typhimurium in the spaceflight environment will result in stress-induced mutations and genomic instability as reflected in changes in the genomic, epigenetic, transcriptomic, and select phenotypic characteristics (including virulence) that will reflect a central role for Hfq as a global regulator of these heritable responses. To address this hypothesis, we propose to characterize the impact of long term spaceflight culture (at least 100 generations) of an S. Typhimurium wild type and hfq mutant on the genomic, epigenetic, transcriptomic, virulence, and pathogenesis-related characteristics of these strains. This proposal addresses Research Emphasis 2 - ISS (International Space Station) Microbial Observatory Studies from section B.3 of this announcement, as it is designed to “take advantage of the technological maturity, low cost, and speed of genomic analyses and the rapid generation time of microbes to monitor the evolution of microbial genomic changes in response to the selective pressures present in the spaceflight environment.” In addition, by employing genomic, transcriptomic, and epigenomic analysis, this proposal also addresses the Research Emphasis 1 – Spaceflight Omics Studies in section B.2. In its design, this proposal will address the NASA Space Biology Plan, Overarching Questions CMM-1a, CMM-1d, and CMM5. This proposal also directly addresses the highest priority recommendations from the Decadal Survey for the establishment of an ISS Microbial Observatory conducting long-term, multigenerational studies of microbial population dynamics (Priority P1). In addition, this study analyzes “microbial growth and physiological responses to the multiple stimuli encountered in spaceflight environments,” as described by the panel in Priority P2.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: This research will enrich life on Earth through the use of space technology and the application of biomedical knowledge. Specifically, this study will utilize the microgravity spaceflight platform to 1) to broaden our knowledge of the host-pathogen interaction that leads to infectious disease, and 2) for the development of new therapeutic strategies to combat infectious disease for the general public.
