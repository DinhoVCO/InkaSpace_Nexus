Task Description: Astronauts exposed to extended periods of unloading experience significant bone loss. As NASA undertakes an exploration initiative involving long-duration spaceflight and exploration of asteroids and Mars, astronauts will encounter prolonged periods of unloading followed by subsequent extra-vehicular activities in a gravity environment. Loss of bone mass and strength will complicate these activities and impair mission efficiency. For instance, considering the magnitude of bone loss occurring during spaceflight (0.5-1.5% loss of bone mineral density (BMD)/month), a fall on an already weakened skeletal system could result in fracture. Therefore, development of countermeasures for microgravity-induced bone loss is critical. We previously demonstrated that osteoblast- and osteocyte-specific deficiency in the gap junctional protein connexin 43 (Cx43) is protective against bone loss induced by unloading via hindlimb suspension (HLS). However, the mechanism by which this occurs is unknown. Osteocytes are well situated in bone to detect and communicate mechanical signals to effector cells and therefore play a pivotal role in bone's ability to sense and respond to unloading from exposure to microgravity. Therefore, we will examine the hypothesis that Cx43 deficiency attenuates the response of osteocytes to unloading resulting from simulated microgravity. Additionally, we will examine whether the response of osteocytes exposed to simulated microgravity in vitro can predict bone response to simulated microgravity in vivo. Using an in vivo and in vitro model, we will complete the following two aims.

Aim 1: Examine the response of wild-type and Cx43-deficient (via CRISPR gene editing) murine osteocytes (OCY454) to simulated microgravity in vitro using a High Aspect Ratio Vessel (HARV).

Aim 2: Examine the effect of simulated microgravity in vivo on bones from osteocyte-specific Cx43-deficient mice. Cx43 will be selectively deleted from mice using a Cre recombinase approach similar to what we previously used to delete Cx43 from osteoblasts and osteocytes.

Using well-established experimental paradigms like the HARV apparatus and HLS greatly increases the probability of successfully completing our aims and attaining important, novel findings. Collectively, our infrastructure and expertise, in combination with multiple unique reagents, place us in an unparalleled position to rapidly and significantly advance knowledge pertaining to the mechanisms underlying microgravity-induced bone loss. These aims and the experiments designed therein represent a substantive departure from our current understanding and will reveal novel targets for countermeasures.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: The proposed research will establish the mechanism underlying the protective effect of Cx43 against microgravity-induced bone loss. This will help fill gaps in our understanding of gravity-sensing mechanisms. We will also gain insight as to whether in vitro models of microgravity, such as culturing cells in a HARV apparatus, induce similar changes in osteocytic gene expression as do in vivo models such as HLS. From the perspective of microgravity countermeasures, this information will guide the choice of potential therapeutics.

On the ground, concomitant bone loss is frequently experienced by individuals confined to bed for long durations, whether as a result of neurological injury, trauma, or neuromuscular disease (e.g., cerebral palsy and muscular dystrophy). In these patients, and in healthy patients subjected to extended bed rest, a significant loss of bone occurs. In the case of recoverable disease or when ambulation is still possible, this leaves patients at risk for falls and catastrophic fracture. Understanding the mechanisms involved in bone loss during spaceflight, and the development of countermeasure strategies, will provide a framework for the development of preventative treatments for astronauts and the clinical population.

The proposed research is innovative because it provides the first simultaneous examination of effects of simulated microgravity on genetically modified osteocytes in vivo and in vitro. A few studies have examined the effects of simulated microgravity on bone in vivo and in vitro,but no study has compared the effects of simulated microgravity on osteocytes or genetically modified mice in vivo and in vitro in the same study. Osteocytes play a key role in bone’s mechanosensitivity and adaptation to changes in mechanical loading. Furthermore, we are investigating the novel concept that Cx43 deficiency protects against microgravity-induced bone loss through a non-canonical mechanism involving an interaction between Cx43 and the WNT/ß-catenin pathway. Finally, we will use innovative transcriptomic approaches to compare the response of primary osteocytes exposed to simulated microgravity in vivo to osteocytic OCY454 cells exposed to simulated microgravity in vitro. In this proposal we are leveraging our experience and expertise in bone biology (Donahue) and gene expression (Buck). Our team brings forward a collaborative and unique skill set in order to ensure a complete and comprehensive investigation of the response of osteocytic cells to simulated microgravity.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2022 
Task Progress: Progress on Aim 1. As detailed in previous annual reports, we made considerable progress on aim 1. In addressing aim 1, which focused on the response of wild-type (WT) and Cx43-deficient (via CRISPR gene editing) murine osteocytes (OCY454) to simulated microgravity in vitro, WT Ocy454 osteocytic cells and Cx43 deficient (Cx43 KO) cells generated by CRISPR were seeded onto Alvetex 12 well scaffold inserts (500,000 cells/insert; Reprocell) and cultured for 10 days at 37°C in aMEM (10% FBS; 2% Pen/Strep). At 10 days, scaffolds were placed in rotary cell culture vessels (RCCS; Synthecon) to simulate three conditions: 1) microgravity (Sim (MG)); 2) rotary ground (Rot Grd); or 3) static ground (Stat Grd) conditions, as previously documented, for 72 hours. Relative expression of Sim MG and Rot Grd genes (compared to Stat Grd) for osteocytic apoptosis (DDIT3) and resorption (RANKL,OPG) were assessed by the micro-CT method. Soluble sclerostin expression in the media was assessed by ELISA (R&D Systems) and normalized to scaffold cell number by PicoGreen DNA Assay (Invitrogen). Data (n = 4-8 RCCS vessels per group) were analyzed by 2-way ANOVA with Tukey’s post-hoc test (p<0.05).

Seventy-two hours of Sim MG significantly increased relative DDIT3 expression in WT but not Cx43 KO osteocytes. RANKL/OPG expression was not affected by Sim MG or Cx43 deficiency. Sclerostin protein secretion was significantly upregulated by Sim MG in WT osteocytes but not Cx43 KO osteocytes. These results partially support our hypothesis and suggest that Cx43 deficiency protects against unloading induced osteocyte apoptosis and sclerostin secretion, a potent WNT/B-catenin inhibitor. The RANKL/OPG expression ratio was unaffected by Sim MG and Cx43 KO despite previous reports. This may be due to experimental differences between the studies and our own, such as osteocyte cell type used, MLO-Y4 versus Ocy454, or differences in unloading conditions. Furthermore, despite increased apoptotic signaling in WT osteocytes undergoing SIM MG versus Cx43 KO, cell counts were not statistically different at seventy-two hours between groups. This suggests that increased cell death due to apoptotic signaling may not be apparent in WT osteocytes till later timepoints or be compensated by enhanced proliferation.

These results suggest that Cx43 deficiency reduces apoptotic gene expression and sclerostin expression in response to simulated microgravity. Thus, the protective effects of osteoblast and osteocyte Cx43 deficiency on unloading induced bone loss may be to preserve osteocyte viability and boost WNT/B-catenin signaling.

Progress on Aim 2. For aim 2, we were able to examine mice with constitutive deletion of Cx43. Mice with constitutive deletion of Cx43 in osteocytes only (Dmp1 Cre) display only a mild bone loss phenotype making them a good choice for our experiments. Additionally, we were able to expand the study and examine muscle changes in these mice. DMP1 Lineage Cx43 Deficient mice underwent hind limb unloading (HLU) to simulate microgravity or normal weight-bearing activity (controls) for 3 weeks. Data was analyzed for effect of treatment (HLU, control) and genotype (WT, Cx43 cKO) by 2-WAY repeated measures ANOVA with Tukey’s multiple comparisons test (*p< 0.05). HLU and Cx43 deficiency synergistically decreased normalized gastrocnemius but not soleus mass (only affected by HLU) (p<0.05). HLU overall decreased tetanic torque following plantar flexion compared to controls by -20% (p<0.05). In contrast, Cx43 deficiency independently increased tetanic torque by +26% compared to controls, abrogating much of the disuse effect (p<0.05). In uninjured diaphyseal femurs, HLU and Cx43 deficiency synergistically decreased cortical and metaphyseal bone volume (p<0.05; ~2-3 fold relative to controls). Although fracture calluses trended toward decreased callus volume with HLU (-22% vs. controls) and reduced mineralization with Cx43 (-16% vs WT) independently, these effects didn’t reach significance. Our results suggest that Cx43 deficiency in DMP1 lineage cells may protect against impairments in muscle function with HLU but not changes in bone mass or fracture healing, at least in female mice.

We also had the ability to expand the study and examine the effect of HLU on fracture healing. Mice with constitutive deletion of Cx43 in osteocytes only (Dmp1 Cre) underwent HLU for 3 weeks following skeletal maturity (26 weeks) to simulate extended microgravity exposure. Then, mice had their right femur fractured by open surgical dissection (stabilized with 24-gauge pin) and were allowed to heal for 14 days. Mice given normal cage activity throughout the experiment served as weight-bearing controls (GC). Mouse injured and uninjured contralateral hindlimb bone and muscle properties were analyzed for the effects of treatment (HLU, control) and genotype (WT, Cx43 cKO) by 2-WAY ANOVA with Tukey’s multiple comparisons test (*p< 0.05). Cx43 deficient bones demonstrated significantly reduced cortical area fraction at baseline compared to WT mice. With HLU, both WT and CX43 deficient mice showed significant diaphyseal bone loss and gastrocnemius muscle mass (p <0.05); however, this effect wasn’t dependent on genotype. Looking at fractured femurs, there were trends of differential effects between treatment and genotype. For example, WT mice with HLU showed decreased callus bone volume and bone volume fraction compared to ground control with CX43 deficient calluses showing opposite effects. In addition, Cx43 deficient mice under ground control conditions showed decreased callus formation compared to WT mice. These results suggest that Cx43 deficiency in DMP1 lineage cells may protect against impairments in fracture healing with HLU, and therefore microgravity. We have continued with development of a tamoxifen inducible osteocyte specific Cx43 deficient mouse model using DMP1 Cre-ERT2 construct. Importantly, DMP1 Cre-ERT2 shows only a small degree of leakiness (~10-20% of osteocytes) and minimal muscle targeting. This novel model will allow us to more accurately determine the role of postnatal Cx43 expression on osteocyte apoptosis and senescence in the context of disuse without a confounding basal phenotype. We plan to generate inducible, osteocyte specific Cx43 deficient mice. Inducible, osteocyte specific Cx43 deficient mice will be generated by crossing the 10kb-Dmp1-Cre-ERT2 transgenic line D77 (DMP1 Cre-ERT2; Jax #029594) with Cx43 floxed (Cx43fl/fl; Jax Stock #008039) mice. Furthermore, the Cre reporter Ai14 allele (Rosa-CAG-LSL-tdTomato Jax Stock #007914) will be introduced into our experimental mouse line to visualize and quantify DMP1 Cre-ERT2 activated cells. Mice will be given tamoxifen for 3 consecutive days (3X; 75mg/kg; i.p.) 1 week before unloading to induce recombination. DMP1 Cre-ERT2 Cx43fl/fl Ai14 mice given tamoxifen will serve as osteocyte Cx43 deficient mice whereas Cx43fl/fl Ai14 littermate mice given tamoxifen will serve as controls. Thus far, we have successfully bred the Cx43 and Ai14 alleles to homozygosity in a C57BL/6J congenic mouse strain and crossed to the DMP1 Cre-ERT2 line. Experimental mice are aging for experiments starting in June 2023.

Task Description: Astronauts exposed to extended periods of unloading experience significant bone loss. As NASA undertakes an exploration initiative involving long-duration spaceflight and exploration of asteroids and Mars, astronauts will encounter prolonged periods of unloading followed by subsequent extra-vehicular activities in a gravity environment. Loss of bone mass and strength will complicate these activities and impair mission efficiency. For instance, considering the magnitude of bone loss occurring during spaceflight (0.5-1.5% loss of bone mineral density (BMD)/month), a fall on an already weakened skeletal system could result in fracture. Therefore, development of countermeasures for microgravity-induced bone loss is critical. We previously demonstrated that osteoblast- and osteocyte-specific deficiency in the gap junctional protein connexin 43 (Cx43) is protective against bone loss induced by unloading via hindlimb suspension (HLS). However, the mechanism by which this occurs is unknown. Osteocytes are well situated in bone to detect and communicate mechanical signals to effector cells and therefore play a pivotal role in bone's ability to sense and respond to unloading from exposure to microgravity. Therefore, we will examine the hypothesis that Cx43 deficiency attenuates the response of osteocytes to unloading resulting from simulated microgravity. Additionally, we will examine whether the response of osteocytes exposed to simulated microgravity in vitro can predict bone response to simulated microgravity in vivo. Using an in vivo and in vitro model, we will complete the following two aims.

Aim 1: Examine the response of wild-type and Cx43-deficient (via CRISPR gene editing) murine osteocytes (OCY454) to simulated microgravity in vitro using a High Aspect Ratio Vessel (HARV).

Aim 2: Examine the effect of simulated microgravity in vivo on bones from osteocyte-specific Cx43-deficient mice. Cx43 will be selectively deleted from mice using a Cre recombinase approach similar to what we previously used to delete Cx43 from osteoblasts and osteocytes.

Using well-established experimental paradigms like the HARV apparatus and HLS greatly increases the probability of successfully completing our aims and attaining important, novel findings. Collectively, our infrastructure and expertise, in combination with multiple unique reagents, place us in an unparalleled position to rapidly and significantly advance knowledge pertaining to the mechanisms underlying microgravity-induced bone loss. These aims and the experiments designed therein represent a substantive departure from our current understanding and will reveal novel targets for countermeasures.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: The proposed research will establish the mechanism underlying the protective effect of Cx43 against microgravity-induced bone loss. This will help fill gaps in our understanding of gravity-sensing mechanisms. We will also gain insight as to whether in vitro models of microgravity, such as culturing cells in a HARV apparatus, induce similar changes in osteocytic gene expression as do in vivo models such as HLS. From the perspective of microgravity countermeasures, this information will guide the choice of potential therapeutics.

On the ground, concomitant bone loss is frequently experienced by individuals confined to bed for long durations, whether as a result of neurological injury, trauma, or neuromuscular disease (e.g., cerebral palsy and muscular dystrophy). In these patients, and in healthy patients subjected to extended bed rest, a significant loss of bone occurs. In the case of recoverable disease or when ambulation is still possible, this leaves patients at risk for falls and catastrophic fracture. Understanding the mechanisms involved in bone loss during spaceflight, and the development of countermeasure strategies, will provide a framework for the development of preventative treatments for astronauts and the clinical population.

The proposed research is innovative because it provides the first simultaneous examination of effects of simulated microgravity on genetically modified osteocytes in vivo and in vitro. A few studies have examined the effects of simulated microgravity on bone in vivo and in vitro,but no study has compared the effects of simulated microgravity on osteocytes or genetically modified mice in vivo and in vitro in the same study. Osteocytes play a key role in bone’s mechanosensitivity and adaptation to changes in mechanical loading. Furthermore, we are investigating the novel concept that Cx43 deficiency protects against microgravity-induced bone loss through a non-canonical mechanism involving an interaction between Cx43 and the WNT/ß-catenin pathway. Finally, we will use innovative transcriptomic approaches to compare the response of primary osteocytes exposed to simulated microgravity in vivo to osteocytic OCY454 cells exposed to simulated microgravity in vitro. In this proposal we are leveraging our experience and expertise in bone biology (Donahue) and gene expression (Buck). Our team brings forward a collaborative and unique skill set in order to ensure a complete and comprehensive investigation of the response of osteocytic cells to simulated microgravity.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2019 
Task Progress: Aim 1: Examine the response of wild-type (WT) and Cx43-deficient (via CRISPR gene editing) murine osteocytes (OCY454) to simulated microgravity in vitro using a high aspect ratio vessel (HARV).

We have made considerable progress in vitro experiments for aim 1. In this aim we hypothesized that Ocy454 wildtype (WT) cells, which display characteristics of mature osteocytes, exposed to simulated µg display decreased expression of bone anabolic genes and increased expression of bone catabolic genes. Furthermore, this effect would be attenuated in Ocy454 Cx43 deficient (Cx43 KO) cells.

To test our hypotheses, Ocy454 WT and Cx43 KO (generated by CRISPR) cells were first cultured at 37°C (5% CO2) for 12 days with media changes (aMEM;20% FBS; Gibco) every 3 days. Next, Ocy454 cells were trypsinized and cultured on collagen 1 coated microcarrier beads (Cytodex3™; GE Healthcare) at 3 x 106 cells per 50 mg of beads for 2 days. A small portion of cells were also collected in RIPA buffer for western blot protein analysis with anti-mouse Cx43 antibody (Sigma C6219; 1:8000). Finally, microcarrier beads with Ocy454 cells attached were loaded onto disks (n = 3 per group/genotype) and placed in a rotary cell culture system (RCCS; Synthecon). Disks were rotated counterclockwise at 15-17 rpm for 24 hrs to simulate µg by average gravity vector cancellation [4]. Non-rotating (static) disks served as controls. Immediately following the experiment, Ocy454 cells were lysed directly off the beads and mRNA was collected and purified using a RNeasy mini kit (Qiagen). SYBR green based qPCR was run with the average cycle number (Cq) of ß-actin and GAPDH genes used for delta CT normalization.

Cx43 protein (43 kDa) was present in cellular protein fractions from Ocy454 WT cells but not Cx43 KO cells. This confirmed that Ocy454 cells express Cx43, which has not been reported in the literature. Furthermore, this confirmed Cx43 deficiency in our Cx43 KO cells. Following 24 hrs of exposure to simulated µg, one disk per group/genotype showed evidence of contamination and wasn’t included in the analysis. Remaining WT Ocy454 cells showed a nearly 8 fold increase in Ptgs2, encodes Cyclooxygenase-2, and a 3 fold increase in Tnfsf11, encodes RANKL, expression versus static controls. Upregulation of these same genes were attenuated in Cx43 KO Ocy454 cells exposed to µg versus static controls. Furthermore, Tnfrsf11B, encodes OPG, an inhibitor of osteoclast formation, was upregulated in Ocy454 KO cells exposed to µg versus static controls. All cells regardless of genotype, when exposed to ug, showed increased Pdpn expression, encodes Podoplanin, a marker of dendrite formation, versus static controls. These results support our hypothesis that osteocytic Cx43’s protective effects during unloading may be due to a reduction in genes related to bone resorption. This is in line with in vivo studies that show that osteoblast/osteocyte-specific Cx43 deficiency attenuates key mechanoresponsive genes involved in bone resorption (RANKL/OPG).

Conclusions: Osteocytic RANKL/OPG signaling attenuation due to Cx43 deficiency may be one osteoprotective mechanism during unloading. Future experimental studies should confirm these gene expression results and also investigate the role of µg and Cx43 deficiency in altering osteocytic WNT/ß-catenin signaling.

Aim 2: Examine the effect of simulated microgravity in vivo on bones from osteocyte-specific Cx43-deficient mice.

In Aim 2 we hypothesized that the loss of Cx43 in osteocytes would be protective against microgravity-induced bone loss in vivo. We used a mouse model with Cx43 deleted only in osteocytes and subjected mice to unloading via hindlimb suspension (HLS) to investigate the role of osteocytic Cx43 in bone’s response to unloading. Cx43-deficient mice were bred using a DMP1-Cre-Lox system on a C57Bl/6J background. At 6 months old, male wild-type and osteocyte specific Cx43-deficient mice were randomly assigned to control (normal ambulation) or HLS groups for 3 weeks (n=5-8 per group). Bone geometry of the left femur was assessed by micro-CT. Mechanical properties were assessed by 3-point bending to failure. Left quadriceps and gastrocnemius muscles were weighed to assess muscle atrophy. Two-Way ANOVA with Tukey’s tests were used to test for significant differences between genotype and treatment (p<0.05).

Cx43 deficiency in osteocytes led to decreased cortical area fraction (CtAr/TAr) and increased marrow area, periosteal perimeter, and endosteal perimeter. Differences in CtAr/TAr did not remain after HLS as more bone was lost from wild-type than Cx43-deficient mice. Differences in bone parameters and response to unloading were not seen in metaphyseal trabecular bone. Cx43-deficient bones exposed to HLS had greater ultimate load than wild-type HLS, but there were no differences in ultimate stress. No differences were found in muscle mass of the left quadriceps or gastrocnemius muscles of HLS mice relative to control.

Conclusions: These results are similar to what was seen in mice with Cx43 deleted in osteoblasts and osteocytes. This suggests that the role of Cx43 in bone’s mechanosensing mechanism is mainly found in osteocytes. Cx43-deficient bones display early development of osteopenia and are less susceptible to the negative effects of unloading. However, Cx43 deficiency did not protect against HLS-induced muscle loss. Future work will evaluate the responses of other genes involved in sensing and transducing mechanical loading in Cx43-deficient mice.
