Task Description: POSTDOCTORAL FELLOWSHIP

NOTE: This project is funded by NASA as a result of a Space Act Agreement between NASA and the Sloan Foundation, for Collaborative Research Proposal for Microbiology of the Built Environment (MoBE). The purpose of this Space Act Agreement is to enhance the ability of NASA and the Sloan Foundation to support scientific research and the dissemination of information about the effect of spaceflight on microbial ecosystems in spacecraft such as the International Space Station (ISS) through sponsorship of postdoctoral fellowships. NASA is providing the isolates for all studies.

OVERVIEW

The proposed work will offer genetics-based avenues to gain insight into all 3 of the guiding questions as listed in this solicitation, but will directly address: “Does long-duration spaceflight alter normal rates of evolutionary change?”

We propose to perform whole-genome sequencing of 50 isolates from the International Space Station (ISS) focused within the genera Staphylococcus and Ralstonia. These ISS-isolate genomes will be analyzed within the context of both an exhaustive, Earth-based reference genomic library constructed for each genus, and ISS astronaut microbiome metagenomic data. Co-Investigator Alexander Voorhies and collaborator Hernan Lorenzi are currently working with ‘before,’ ‘during,’ and ‘after’ microbiome metagenome from 9 ISS astronauts that were onboard the space station for various lengths of time spanning 2013–2016. Our unique position of being able to integrate microbiome sequencing data from another NASA effort with newly sequenced genomes of ISS isolates offers an unprecedented opportunity to explore many Space Biology evolutionary questions at the molecular level.

OBJECTIVES/METHODS

Genera Staphylococcus and Ralstonia were selected based on several criteria including ubiquity within human microbiota, potential pathogenicity, and their abundant representation within Earth-based reference databases, the ISS archive of available isolates, and our currently in-hand astronaut microbiome metagenomic data. We will investigate many metrics of molecular evolution aboard the ISS by directly addressing the following questions:

1) Are any particular genetic elements (i.e., genes/gene cassettes/operons) disproportionately represented in ISS isolates as compared to their Earth-based, nearest phylogenetic relatives?

2) Are there different propensities for specific individual genome mutations (i.e., single-point mutations such as substitutions/insertions/deletions, non-synonymous to synonymous substitution ratios, transition to transversion ratios, and genetic rearrangements) for organisms in the space-based built environment?

3) Which specific genes (and codons within these genes) are under positive, negative, or neutral selection aboard the ISS, but not within terrestrial references?

4) Can we track microbial transmission from an individual astronaut to the built environment, and/or the ISS serving as a vector and contributing to the microbiome of resident astronauts?

5) As assessed via comparison to metagenome-assembled genomes reconstructed from ISS-astronaut microbiomes, how representative are the isolate genomes recovered from the ISS of the actual microbiota of ISS astronauts?

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: SIGNIFICANCE

Microorganisms are not only integral to human health, but are also inexorable and necessary components of any self-contained elemental cycling, food production, or water system, and longer-duration human space exploration will require all of these systems. To design safe, built-environment microbial ecosystems to support these life-support systems requires not only understanding short-term, “plastic” microbial responses to the space environment, but also longer-term propensities for microbial evolution and adaptation. This work will leverage newly developed genetics-based computational tools, technologies, and approaches to generate the first comprehensive, whole-genomic database of microorganisms isolated from the space-based built environment while addressing fundamental questions about evolution aboard the ISS.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2021 
Task Progress: Update: The primary work from this time period is described in our publication “Reference-guided metagenomics reveals genome-level evidence of potential microbial transmission from the ISS environment to an astronaut’s microbiome” (Lee et al. 2021). Using assembled genomes of our ISS-derived microbial isolates as references and recruiting metagenomic reads from an astronaut’s nasal microbiome revealed no recruitment to a Staphylococcus aureus isolate from samples before launch, yet systematic recruitment across the genome when sampled after 3 months aboard the ISS, with a median percent identity of 100%. This suggests that either a highly similar S. aureus population colonized the astronaut’s nasal microbiome while the astronaut was aboard the ISS or that it may have been below detection before spaceflight, instead supporting a shift in community composition. This work highlights the value in generating genomic libraries of microbes from built-environments such as the ISS and demonstrates one way such data can be integrated with metagenomics to facilitate the tracking and monitoring of astronaut microbiomes and health.

Lee et al. (2021). Reference-guided metagenomics reveals genome-level evidence of potential microbial transmission from the ISS environment to an astronaut’s microbiome. iScience. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.isci.2021.102114 (see also Bibliography section below)

We are still working on a publication focusing on the genomics and physiology of Staphylococcus isolates, similar to the publication focused on Burkholderia, previously reported in FY2020 report (O'Rourke A et al. "Genomic and phenotypic characterization of Burkholderia isolates from the potable water system of the International Space Station." PLoS One. 2020 Feb 20;15(2):e0227152. https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0227152

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2020 
Task Progress: This work employs a multitude of physiological and bioinformatic approaches to investigate microbial isolates recovered from the International Space Station (ISS). We do not currently have the capabilities to design and implement a decades-long evolutionary experiment aboard the ISS with proper controls, but the archive of microbes that have been isolated from the ISS over the course of 2006–2015 provides an invaluable collection that can be explored after the fact. We are utilizing this isolate collection to ask many questions about these microbes in contrast to their nearest relatives that have not been aboard the ISS (e.g., gene-enrichment/depletion, antibiotic resistance profiles and genetic complements, plasmid composition, rates of single-nucleotide and single-amino acid variability as a function of core phylogenetic distance). We additionally are combining this dataset with another invaluable dataset, metagenomic data from the Astronaut Microbiome Project, to try to track if there is any evidence of microbial transmission from the ISS microbiome to the microbiome of resident astronauts.

We have successfully received, cultured, sequenced, and assembled the genomes of 114 isolates recovered from the ISS – 24 of genus Burkholderia, 37 Ralstonia, and 53 Staphylococcus. All 3 of these genera are common human microbiome members and potential pathogens. Work thus far has given us some idea of the variability of these organisms and their plasmid content, how they relate to their nearest Earth-bound relatives phylogenetically, gene-content-wise, and in terms of their susceptibility to antibiotics. For an example in terms of variability, we recovered 24 isolates from 2 species of Burkholderia, B. cepacia, and B. contaminans that were isolated over a 5-year period. Within species, these all form monophyletic clades based on phylogenomic trees with all available references, and all demonstrate greater than 99% average nucleotide identity based on successful genome alignments of greater than 95%. This reveals all members of each of these 2 species are of a highly similar genomic lineage and may have all stemmed from the same or very similar founding populations, rather than being a mixed assemblage. A pangenomic analysis (considering the entire genetic complement of some defined group of organisms) incorporating just the 19 ISS-derived B. cepacia, for instance, demonstrated that they all shared greater than 90% of the same genes. And the identification of putative plasmids revealed that this small proportion of genetic variability that exists appears to be harbored on a highly variable population of plasmids, carried alongside a highly conserved and stable chromosomal population. When incorporating these 19 ISS-derived B. cepacia into a pangenomic analysis with 136 available B. cepacia reference genomes allowed us to identify 265 functions significantly enriched in our ISS isolates as compared to the references (several annotated in 19/19 of our isolates and 0/136 of the references), and 98 functions significantly depleted in our isolates. It is important to keep in mind however that because these differences co-vary with genome phylogeny, it is not yet knowable if these particular enriched traits are a consequence of time spent on the ISS, or just a result of a more general evolutionary divergence through time. This work has now been published ( https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0227152 ), and we are in the process of writing up and submitting the results of similar approaches on our 53 Staphylococcus ISS-derived isolates. Likely due to being recovered from various exposed surfaces of the ISS, in contrast with just coming from the water-system as was the case with Burkholderia and Ralsonia, there seems to be much greater variability in the Staphylococcus representatives.

In an effort to find evidence of microbial transmission from the ISS microbiome to the microbiome of an astronaut, we have also integrated these newly sequenced ISS-isolate genomes with data from the Astronaut Microbiome Project. This includes astronaut microbiome metagenomic data from multiple timepoints before, during, and after a mission on the ISS. Through recruiting the short reads from the astronaut microbiome metagenomic data to our ISS-isolate genomes, we have identified at least genome so far where there was no recruitment at the four timepoints pre-flight from an astronaut’s nasal microbiome, strong recruitment across the entire genome when sampled on days 90 and 180 onboard the ISS, and then near-zero recruitment after returning to Earth. We see two possible explanations for this. One is that something similar enough to recruit to this isolate colonized the astronaut’s nasal microbiome when they were aboard the ISS, and then began being outcompeted when the astronaut returned to Earth. The second is that something similar enough to recruit to this isolate genome was already present in the astronaut’s nasal microbiome, but was at too low of a relative abundance to be captured before their time aboard the ISS. Then the ISS environment was conducive to it becoming more abundant, which let us capture it with this approach, and after returning to Earth it may have returned back down to its lower abundance. Some astronauts have noted prolonged congestion while aboard the ISS, and studies have shown a relative increase in Staphylococcus bacteria to be associated with such symptoms. This work is about to be submitted for publication.

Through recruiting the short reads from the astronaut microbiome metagenomic data to our ISS-isolate genomes, we have identified at least genome so far where there was no recruitment at the four timepoints pre-flight from an astronaut’s nasal microbiome, strong recruitment across the entire genome when sampled on days 90 and 180 onboard the ISS, and then near-zero recruitment after returning to Earth. We see two possible explanations for this. One is that something similar enough to recruit to this isolate colonized the astronaut’s nasal microbiome when they were aboard the ISS, and then began being outcompeted when the astronaut returned to Earth. The second is that something similar enough to recruit to this isolate genome was already present in the astronaut’s nasal microbiome, but was at too low of a relative abundance to be captured before their time aboard the ISS. Then the ISS environment was conducive to it becoming more abundant, which let us capture it with this approach, and after returning to Earth it may have returned back down to its lower abundance. We think it is the former, transmission, but we cannot at this point rule out the possibility that it is the latter. Either way something interesting is going on. We are in the process of writing up this work.

Task Description: POSTDOCTORAL FELLOWSHIP

NOTE: This project is funded by NASA as a result of a Space Act Agreement between NASA and the Sloan Foundation, for Collaborative Research Proposal for Microbiology of the Built Environment (MoBE). The purpose of this Space Act Agreement is to enhance the ability of NASA and the Sloan Foundation to support scientific research and the dissemination of information about the effect of spaceflight on microbial ecosystems in spacecraft such as the International Space Station (ISS) through sponsorship of postdoctoral fellowships. NASA is providing the isolates for all studies.

OVERVIEW

The proposed work will offer genetics-based avenues to gain insight into all 3 of the guiding questions as listed in this solicitation, but will directly address: “Does long-duration spaceflight alter normal rates of evolutionary change?”

We propose to perform whole-genome sequencing of 50 isolates from the International Space Station (ISS) focused within the genera Staphylococcus and Ralstonia. These ISS-isolate genomes will be analyzed within the context of both an exhaustive, Earth-based reference genomic library constructed for each genus, and ISS astronaut microbiome metagenomic data. Co-Investigator Alexander Voorhies and collaborator Hernan Lorenzi are currently working with ‘before,’ ‘during,’ and ‘after’ microbiome metagenome from 9 ISS astronauts that were onboard the space station for various lengths of time spanning 2013–2016. Our unique position of being able to integrate microbiome sequencing data from another NASA effort with newly sequenced genomes of ISS isolates offers an unprecedented opportunity to explore many Space Biology evolutionary questions at the molecular level.

OBJECTIVES/METHODS

Genera Staphylococcus and Ralstonia were selected based on several criteria including ubiquity within human microbiota, potential pathogenicity, and their abundant representation within Earth-based reference databases, the ISS archive of available isolates, and our currently in-hand astronaut microbiome metagenomic data. We will investigate many metrics of molecular evolution aboard the ISS by directly addressing the following questions:

1) Are any particular genetic elements (i.e., genes/gene cassettes/operons) disproportionately represented in ISS isolates as compared to their Earth-based, nearest phylogenetic relatives?

2) Are there different propensities for specific individual genome mutations (i.e., single-point mutations such as substitutions/insertions/deletions, non-synonymous to synonymous substitution ratios, transition to transversion ratios, and genetic rearrangements) for organisms in the space-based built environment?

3) Which specific genes (and codons within these genes) are under positive, negative, or neutral selection aboard the ISS, but not within terrestrial references?

4) Can we track microbial transmission from an individual astronaut to the built environment, and/or the ISS serving as a vector and contributing to the microbiome of resident astronauts?

5) As assessed via comparison to metagenome-assembled genomes reconstructed from ISS-astronaut microbiomes, how representative are the isolate genomes recovered from the ISS of the actual microbiota of ISS astronauts?

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: SIGNIFICANCE

Microorganisms are not only integral to human health, but are also inexorable and necessary components of any self-contained elemental cycling, food production, or water system, and longer-duration human space exploration will require all of these systems. To design safe, built-environment microbial ecosystems to support these life-support systems requires not only understanding short-term, “plastic” microbial responses to the space environment, but also longer-term propensities for microbial evolution and adaptation. This work will leverage newly developed genetics-based computational tools, technologies, and approaches to generate the first comprehensive, whole-genomic database of microorganisms isolated from the space-based built environment while addressing fundamental questions about evolution aboard the ISS.
