Task Description: Health of the musculoskeletal system depends on exposure to mechanical loading. Removal or reduction of mechanical loading precipitates muscle atrophy and bone loss. Thus, despite rigorous exercise protocols and other countermeasures, muscle atrophy and bone loss are still virtually inevitable consequences of spaceflight. One approach for ameliorating the development of these untoward consequences of space travel is by the induction of artificial gravity during spaceflight via centrifugation. The strength of the artificial gravity depends both on the speed of rotation and diameter of the ship. While it is possible to achieve centrifugal force equivalent 1.0 G, it is also possible that lower levels of force may also be effective for preventing muscle atrophy and irreversible bone loss. In this study, we propose to evaluate the potential effectiveness of artificial gravity for the prevention of muscle atrophy and weakness and bone loss by studying rats using an innovative system for partial unloading that has been developed previously for mice by members of our team. We will study the musculoskeletal response of adult male rats (n=14 / group) following exposure to different degrees of unloading representing 0.2 G, 0.4 G, and 0.7 G as well as fully loaded animals for 1, 2, and 4 weeks. We will study the impact of this partial gravity analog environment on muscle and bone structure and function, including performing histological analyses, in situ force measurement of muscle contraction, as well as measurement of bone mass, microarchitecture, and strength. In addition, we will perform both serological and tissue molecular analyses to better understand the mechanisms and time course of development of these changes at different degrees of unloading. This work will also allow us to more effectively understand the relationship between the development of muscle atrophy and weakness and bone deterioration at varying levels of artificial gravity. At the conclusion of this study, we will have defined the potential impact of partial artificial gravity for prevention of musculoskeletal dysfunction and deterioration. This work will inform the future application of artificial gravity in human research and space exploration.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: We believe that this research will positively impact in life on Earth since it will inform us about the effects of reduced muscle and bone use that occur normally in our daily lives and as a consequence of disease or injury. For example, using a cane may reduce the normal weightbearing of a single limb. While this obviously may be necessary from an assistive standpoint, this could lead to additional atrophy of the limb muscles and osteopenia of the bone.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2020 
Task Progress: Research accomplishments during this final period: During this final budget period, we completed our first studies using the partial weightbearing system in female animals with good success and recently had a manuscript accepted for publication in Frontiers of Physiology. We also completed our extension of the original grant, in which we sought to evaluate questions of stress associated with our new model, blood flow concerns with use of the pelvic harness for unloading, and evaluation of relative forces placed on the fore and hind limbs of the animals. We are also completing and submitting bone-related manuscripts.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2019 
Task Progress: During the past year we completed the bulk of our originally planned work studying the musculoskeletal effects of partial unloading at different levels. We also have completed most of the analyses of bone and muscle and have submitted several manuscripts describing this work and are currently completing several of the of the RNAseq analyses. We have identified dose-depending effects of partial weigthbearing (also termed fractional gravity) on both muscle and bone health. These have included reductions in bone mineral density and reduced muscle mass and force production. We have also demonstrated a dose-dependent increase in relative number of fast-twitch fibers in the muscle that also follows a dose-dependent pattern.

There remain several manuscripts in preparation from this work that we hope to have submitted in the coming months. In addition to this work, we extended our model to studies of resveratrol in preserving muscle function as well as looking at varying loading levels. These efforts also led to augmentation funding to study several other aspects of the model that require additional refinement, including evaluating stress levels in the rats, evaluating blood flow in the hind limbs, and assessing how equally or unequally the four limbs are unloaded.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2018 
Task Progress: We have been making excellent progress on all 3 aims of the proposal. Over the past year, we have steadily studied multiple groups of rats exposed to different levels of partial weightbearing for periods of time from 1 to 4 weeks. Assessments have included electrophysiological and functional studies of muscle as well pQCT and microCT of hind limb muscles. We are in the process of completing extensive laboratory investigations as well. We effectively resolved a number of our early challenges to ensure that the animals tolerated the harness system well and were not able to disconnect themselves during the unloading. Our data shows that the analog is effective, inducing a dose-dependency to the degree of unloading; animals at progressively lower relative gravitational loads demonstrate decreasing muscle weight, reduced force generation with electrical stimulation and reduced grip strength, as well as lower cortical bone density. We anticipate completing most of the remaining animal data collection within the next 3-4 weeks, at which point our focus will turn mainly to tissue processing and molecular analyses.

Flight Assignment/Project Notes: NOTE: Extended to 9/30/2018 per F. Hernandez/ARC (Ed., 12/6/17)

Task Description: Health of the musculoskeletal system depends on exposure to mechanical loading. Removal or reduction of mechanical loading precipitates muscle atrophy and bone loss. Thus, despite rigorous exercise protocols and other countermeasures, muscle atrophy and bone loss are still virtually inevitable consequences of spaceflight. One approach for ameliorating the development of these untoward consequences of space travel is by the induction of artificial gravity during spaceflight via centrifugation. The strength of the artificial gravity depends both on the speed of rotation and diameter of the ship. While it is possible to achieve centrifugal force equivalent 1.0 G, it is also possible that lower levels of force may also be effective for preventing muscle atrophy and irreversible bone loss. In this study, we propose to evaluate the potential effectiveness of artificial gravity for the prevention of muscle atrophy and weakness and bone loss by studying rats using an innovative system for partial unloading that has been developed previously for mice by members of our team. We will study the musculoskeletal response of adult male rats (n=14 / group) following exposure to different degrees of unloading representing 0.2 G, 0.4 G, and 0.7 G as well as fully loaded animals for 1, 2, and 4 weeks. We will study the impact of this partial gravity analog environment on muscle and bone structure and function, including performing histological analyses, in situ force measurement of muscle contraction, as well as measurement of bone mass, microarchitecture, and strength. In addition, we will perform both serological and tissue molecular analyses to better understand the mechanisms and time course of development of these changes at different degrees of unloading. This work will also allow us to more effectively understand the relationship between the development of muscle atrophy and weakness and bone deterioration at varying levels of artificial gravity. At the conclusion of this study, we will have defined the potential impact of partial artificial gravity for prevention of musculoskeletal dysfunction and deterioration. This work will inform the future application of artificial gravity in human research and space exploration.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: We believe that this research will positively impact in life on Earth since it will inform us about the effects of reduced muscle and bone use that occur normally in our daily lives and as a consequence of disease or injury. For example, using a cane may reduce the normal weightbearing of a single limb. While this obviously may be necessary from an assistive standpoint, this could lead to additional atrophy of the limb muscles and osteopenia of the bone.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2017 
Task Progress: Research accomplishments during the past year: During the past year, we have focused on setting up the laboratory to begin the proposed studies. This effort has included hiring appropriate personnel and purchasing, assembling, moving, and testing a number of pieces of equipment (e.g., Aurora force measuring system for rats, Rotarod, pQCT, EMG, EIM), and the optimization of cage design and a weighing system for the suspended rats. In addition, a major effort has been made to design a functional and effective suspension system for the rats. The basic approach was that used previously by co-investigator Mary Bouxsein, PhD in mice. However, the considerably greater weight of the rat demanded a number of major modifications that required considerable effort to achieve. This required two major modifications that were devised by the post-doctoral associate overseeing this work, Marie Mortreux, PhD. Specifically, a vest-like device is placed around the chest and a pelvic suspension system (i.e., a padded wire secured beneath the hips) is used to support the hind limbs. These two pieces are then connected to a bar that is then affixed to a chain, which hangs from a rod affixed near the top of the cage. This suspension system has allowed the animals to move around the cage comfortably with reduced weight on their limbs, with full access to food and water and without any restriction regarding their normal behavior (grooming, exploring, resting). It has also limited any bipedal standing or climbing to only very short periods. Overnight observations, during the animals’ active phase, confirm that the animals are consistently unloaded for about 95% of the observed period on the first night alone at 0.2 G. This unloading is likely to improve over time as the animal becomes more acclimated to the set-up. There have been no untoward effects, with animals maintaining a stable weight and with little to no signs of stress.

As part of this work, we have also completed a variety of more minor yet important tasks, including ensuring our ability to obtain reproducible measurements with our various outcome assessment tools and improving our speed in performing all assessments.

Finally, we have spent a great deal of time designing and improving our cages to ensure that the rats have sufficient space to move comfortably, have full access to food and water, and cannot get tangled in the suspension apparatus. Developing, prototyping, and testing these cages have been completed. At the time of this writing, we are in the process of assembling all the cages and beginning our first full set of experiments.
