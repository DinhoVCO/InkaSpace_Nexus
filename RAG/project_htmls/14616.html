Flight Assignment/Project Notes: NOTE: End date changed to 12/16/2021 per NSSC information (Ed., 12/30/20)

Task Description: The GeneLab data repository is a rapidly expanding and remarkably powerful resource with which to dissect organismal responses to spaceflight at levels ranging from the metagenome to the transcriptome and proteome. The research in this GeneLab Data System study sought to extend the bioinformatics tool development for GeneLab data to a cross-kingdom architecture, making inter-organismal comparisons possible. This approach facilitates mining across the wealth of datasets within the GeneLab system and drives insights into the fundamental responses to spaceflight shared by biological systems as a whole. This work has approached some of GeneLab's core questions as to how implementation of large-scale dataset analyses can help produce new insights and hypotheses about the effects of spaceflight on biology. .

An interactive data visualization and mining environment has been developed based on the Qlik engine, which covers all the biology data deposited within GeneLab. This approach allows users to make interactive comparisons and filters between datasets to mine for both common and unique features of the transcriptomes of the GeneLab data. Current work is focused on maintaining and expanding this data environment and moving towards a highly intuitive user interface. This tool will allow users to rapidly move from data exploration to identifying spaceflight-response genes and from there to testable hypotheses.

Specific aims:

(1) Import the diversity of the datasets from the GeneLab Data repository into the Qlik database and develop Orthologous Matrix- and Gene Ontology-driven approaches to translate between them in a kingdom-transparent manner.

(2) Explore using common phenotyping tools and approaches for the research community to facilitate the testing of potential roles of these genes in spaceflight-related factors. This effort will focus on elements of spaceflight such as microgravity and hypoxic stress response that are applicable to a wide range of organisms.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: This research has been to develop an in silico analysis environment to explore GeneLab’s rich set of spaceflight and spaceflight-related data. This approach should help uncover not only patterns of ‘omic-level responses to spaceflight but also provide insight into how biology reacts to the range of stimuli related to spaceflight such as to gravity, radiation, and mechanical forces on Earth. The database allows comparisons across Kingdoms, providing a new tool for biologists to also ask how universal the molecular responses they are characterizing are across the diversity of life on Earth.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2022 
Task Progress: I. Completion of the TOAST database: The Test Of Arabidopsis Space Transcriptome (TOAST) database is a web-based data visualization and analysis environment for all publicly available plant science spaceflight data. The system imports data from GeneLab, from the original publications on each study and from datasets made available by the primary researchers. These data have then been manually curated and linked in a relational database that allows researchers to rapidly compare transcriptome patterns across studies of interest. This tool has been through extensive beta testing and optimization and the fully functional version is publicly available at: https://astrobiology.botany.wisc.edu/astrobotany-toast. The paper describing the TOAST data visualization environment was published in 2020 in Frontiers in Plant Science.

II. Ortholog mapping using the Cross-Species Explorer: A major goal for this research program has been to extend the data visualizations outlined above for TOAST to all the data available in the GeneLab data repository, including animal, plant, and microbial datasets. To this end, equivalent genes (orthologs) have been mapped across species. All the datasets deposited in GeneLab have then been imported and visualization and cross-referencing tools developed to make these comparisons transparent to the user. The web app to access this data engine (called the X-species Explorer) is publicly available at: https://astrobiology.botany.wisc.edu/x-species-astrobiology-genelab.

III. The Rad-Bio-App: In a parallel effort to help space biologists explore the effects of radiation across the available spaceflight omics-level data, a data visualization environment called the Rad-Bio-App has been developed. All the available radiation dosage data from the spaceflight experimentation deposited in GeneLab, as well as that from ground-based studies that have used a variety of sources and accelerators, have been imported and curated alongside associated metadata ranging from the source and type of radiation to species analyzed and hardware used to perform the experiment. All these data are then linked in a database to the associated Gene Lab data accessions, along with available data from the lunar and Martian environments. The App then allows users to explore the GeneLab datasets for their associated radiation environments and filter for similar and different experiment parameters. RadBioApp has 2 goals: (1) providing a useful tool for space radiation biologists, and (2) making analysis of radiation effects more accessible to the non-specialist researcher. The tool is available at: https://astrobiology.botany.wisc.edu/astro-rad-bio-app.

IV. Metadata analyses -The Matrix: GeneLab has developed standardized pipelines for analysis of microarray and RNAseq to help make data comparisons between studies more robust. In collaboration with the GeneLab Plant Analysis Working Group (Plant AWG), a curated metadata matrix (The Matrix) that cross-references to these GeneLab analyses has been developed. The combination of The Matrix and the standardized analyses allows researchers to ask what variables affect the spaceflight data most significantly. This tool is available at: https://astrobiology.botany.wisc.edu/astrobotany-toast/matrix-of-genelab-metadata.

V. Presentations, Outreach, and Education: During the course of this funding, the data visualizations made possible by this research have allowed presentations to be made at the annual meetings of the American Society for Gravitational and Space Research, the Space Generation Advisory Council in Taiwan, The International Space Station Research and Development (ISS R&D) Conference, the annual meeting of the American Society of Plant Biologists, the Midwest Plant Cell Dynamics Meeting, NASA Kennedy Space Center, the Academia Sinica in Taiwan, and at Carthage College. This space research has also formed the basis for outreach efforts at events ranging from University of Wisconsin sponsored outreach days (e.g., University of Wisconsin’s Science Expeditions), to work with groups such as the University of Wisconsin’s PEOPLE program (that engages under-represented groups in science), to Madison’s BioHouse (that engages with high-achieving undergraduates), to middle school and K-12 teachers (the BioPharmaceutical Technology Center Institute training program), to the Kids in Data program designed to introduce young students to concepts of data analysis, to space camp for 5th grades at the Deke Slayton Bike and Space Museum in Sparta, WI.

15 independent study students have worked in the Gilroy lab most semesters on various plant molecular and engineering projects related to space biology, with a team of ~4 contributing to user interface design for the GeneLab X-Species Explorer. Four undergraduates and one high school student have also been engaged in bioinformatics-driven projects related to GeneLab data analysis and visualization over this period.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2021 
Task Progress: The Test Of Arabidopsis Space Transcriptome (TOAST) database is a web-based data visualization and analysis environment for all publicly available plant science spaceflight data. The system imports data from GeneLab, from the original publications on each study, and from datasets made available by the primary researchers. These data have then been manually curated and linked in a relational database that allows researchers to rapidly compare transcriptome patterns across studies of interest. This tool has been through beta testing and optimization and the fully functional version is publicly available at: https://astrobiology.botany.wisc.edu/astrobotany-toast

TOAST imports the published primary omics analysis from the original researchers for each project and then uses these data to perform comparisons. This approach has the advantage of capitalizing on the insights these researchers have about their own experiments and how they have approached their omics work. The drawback is that this aggregates data from different researchers who have used multiple different analytical pipelines to generate, e.g., their lists of differentially expressed genes. In a complementary approach, GeneLab has developed standardized pipelines for analysis of microarray and RNAseq data and is running and depositing these results for each dataset in the GeneLab data repository. This approach should make comparisons between studies more robust. Therefore, to help understand these standardized comparisons and especially how they relate to those of the original researchers, a curated metadata matrix has been developed and cross-referenced to the relevant GeneLab analyses. The combination of this matrix and the standardized analyses has then allowed the asking of which variables affect the spaceflight data most significantly, so called Batch Effects.

A major goal for this work is to extend the data visualizations described above to all the data available in the GeneLab data repository, including animal, plant, and microbial datasets. This has required a method to map equivalent genes (orthologs) across species. Working with the Orthologous Matrix consortium the ortholog comparison tables for the organisms within GeneLab datasets have been imported and visualization and cross-referencing tools generated to make these comparisons between the diversity of GeneLab datasets transparent to the user. The web app to access this data engine (the X-species Explorer) is undergoing optimization of its user interface.

In a parallel effort to help space biologists explore the effects of radiation across the available spaceflight omics-level data, a data visualization environment called the Rad-Bio-App has also been developed. In collaboration with GeneLab, all the available radiation dosage data from the spaceflight experimentation deposited in GeneLab, as well as that from ground-based studies that have used a variety of sources and accelerators have been aggregated and manually curated. All this data is then linked in a database to the associated GeneLab data accessions. The App then allows users to explore the GeneLab datasets for their associated radiation environments and filter for similar and different experiment parameters.

The interface is designed to be accessible to a user base with a diverse level of knowledge about transcriptomics, proteomics, and spaceflight.

This current work is centered on plant biology but offers a strong starting point to extend the reach of these kinds of analyses to beyond the plant kingdom. Creating this increased envelope of investigation possible with the GeneLab Data System by facilitating comparisons between the breadth of data deposited within GeneLab is the opportunity we propose to explore in the research program outlined below.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2020 
Task Progress: Progress I; Initial Database Deployment: The initial implementation of the Test Of Arabidopsis Space Transcriptome (TOAST) database architecture has been focused on a system allowing the comprehensive analysis of plant science spaceflight data. This approach has provided a tractable set of data to develop visualization technologies. This database development has allowed exploration of user interface and analysis approaches that have then undergone extensive beta-testing. This has in turn allowed optimization of the utility of the data visualization tools and optimization of the system’s architecture to end-user needs. The final release of the plant specific TOAST database (version 4.5) has now been implemented and is being publicly released as a web-based portal.

Progress II; Extension of database to all GeneLab datasets: The major goal for this project is then to extend the data visualizations developed for the plant database to all the data available in the GeneLab data repository, including animal, plant, and microbial datasets. The critical advance over the plant TOAST database will be then to allow comparisons between all datasets, e.g., mining expression patterns in mice in spaceflight and being able to ask how closely these match to those in humans, microbes, and plants. This has required a method to map equivalent genes (orthologs) across species. The Orthologous Matrix consortium uses a set of robust ortholog mining algorithms to generate ortholog comparison tables for 2100 species, including all those within the GeneLab datasets. Importing these ortholog comparisons has allowed initial building of a database architecture where choosing genes in one dataset creates a filter across all datasets that maps the orthologous genes in any species to the initially chosen set. GeneLab datasets are being imported into this ‘Kingdoms’ environment, concentrating on those preprocessed and released by GeneLab. This allows a focus on results from a uniform, GeneLab community-vetted analytical pipeline. Accessing a dashboard within the Kingdoms app provides data visualizations that allow interactive filtering of pairwise comparisons within a dataset. These filters are interactively linked to a range of other data analyses, such the Gene Ontology SLIM and Reactome databases.

Current goals: The current orthology data network links the human, mice, rat, fly, worm, and yeast data, while another links Arabidposis, Brasica rapa, Tomato, Brachypodium, Rice, and E. coli. Current work is aimed at unifying these networks and extending them to other species within the GeneLab datasets.

Meta-data curation and Meta-data app: The GeneLab datasets are accompanied by an extensive set of meta-data. However, to be useful for the community to explore which datasets are available, and especially to use such exploration as a way of filtering and focusing on datasets for analysis within the Kingdoms environment, the meta-data needs to be visualized in an interactive manner. A comprehensive meta-data app has therefore been developed that allows exploration of all the GeneLab associated meta-data across all deposited data sets. To make this tool robust, the GeneLab meta-data has been imported and then manually curated to ensure it is both correctly annotated and consistent across the wealth of datasets available. This meta-data app is being deployed alongside the Kingdoms environment for testing as detailed in the following section.

Deployment for beta-testing: The Kingdoms backend database, user interface, and Meta-data app have now been developed and alpha tested in-house. A focused external alpha testing by the community of the GeneLab Analytical Working Groups is also being initiated. The Kingdoms data analysis environment will also be presented at the 2019 annual meeting of the American Society for Gravitational and Space Research, where a public beta testing to this broader community is planned. Feedback from the testing will be used to improve the user interface and also incorporate additional analytical pipelines and functionality requested by the alpha and beta testers.

Education and outreach: During 2018-2019, the data visualizations made possible by the GeneLab Kingdoms approach has been presented at venues ranging from the 2019 MidWest Plant Cell Dynamics Meeting and the annual meeting of the American Society for Gravitational and Space Research to presentations at Kennedy Space Center and the Academia Sinica in Taiwan. This space-related research is also a base for outreach efforts at events ranging from University of Wisconsin sponsored outreach days (e.g., University of Wisconsin’s Science Expeditions) to presentations for high school students and undergraduates (e.g., The University of Wisconsin’s BioHouse) through to middle school and K-12 teachers (Biotechnology Institute summer training program).

Over 2018-2019 approximately 15 independent study students have been working in the lab on various plant molecular and engineering projects related to space biology, with a team of ~4 contributing to user interface design for the GeneLab Kingdoms system, to the development of statistical analyses approaches for these datasets and to the alpha testing of the interface and analysis tools.

We have developed an interactive data visualization and mining environment based on the Qlik engine called the Test Of Arabidopsis Space Transcriptome (TOAST) database that covers all the plant biology data deposited within GeneLab. This approach allows users to make interactive comparisons and filters between datasets to mine for both common and unique features of the transcriptomes of the GeneLab plant data. The reach of this analysis has been further extended by incorporating both the published datasets from other space agencies and unpublished data sets generously provided by the plant science spaceflight community. Current work is focused on maintaining and expanding this plant-driven data environment and incorporating analytical pipelines for gene families, ecotypes, and species to move towards a highly intuitive analysis environment. This tool will allow users to rapidly move from data exploration to identifying spaceflight-response genes and from there to testable hypotheses. The interface is designed to be accessible to a user base with a diverse level of knowledge about transcriptomics, proteomics, and spaceflight.

This current work is centered on plant biology but offers a strong starting point to extend the reach of these kinds of analyses to beyond the plant kingdom. Creating this increased envelope of investigation possible with the GeneLab Data System by facilitating comparisons between the breadth of data deposited within GeneLab is the opportunity we propose to explore in the research program outlined below.

Specific aims:

(1) Import the diversity of the datasets from the GeneLab Data repository into the TOAST database and develop Orthologous Matrix- and Gene Ontology-driven approaches to translate between them in a kingdom-transparent manner.

(2) Develop common phenotyping tools and approaches for the research community to facilitate the testing of potential roles of these genes in spaceflight-related factors. This effort will focus on elements of spaceflight such as microgravity and hypoxic stress response that are applicable to a wide range of organisms.

In addition to using publications and presentations at meetings and the range of Open-Science data analysis sharing tools, results will be distributed to a broader general audience through a range of educational and outreach efforts. This research program will be incorporated into formal undergraduate classwork and also into outreach efforts to high schools and to the general public.
