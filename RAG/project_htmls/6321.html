Flight Assignment/Project Notes: NCE to 12/31/2006, per A. Chu 7/2006.

Task Description: Ionizing radiation induces oxidative damage, which affects all cellular components. However, the most significant form of damage induced by IR, in terms of cell death, mutagenesis, carcinogenesis, and effects on the cell cycle, is clustered DNA damage, often resulting in the formation of DNA double strand breaks. The DNA damage induced by high mass, high energy (HZE) space radiation is though to be more complex than that induced by gamma radiation, as HZE particles generate a denser trail of ionization events in their wake. We proposed to determine whether the same DNA repair pathways required for resistance to gamma radiation are also required for resistance to HZE particles. Because of the importance of double strand break (DSB) repair in prevention of carcinogenic “loss of heterozygosity” events, we have focused on DSB repair pathways. However, we are also interested in signal transduction pathways involved in the detection of IR induced damage- these pathways are important initiators of responses to IR, including cell cycle arrest, induction of repair, and induction of cell death. Thus we are investigating the effects of mutations in the nonhomologous end joining genes DNA ligase IV (LigIV), and Ku80, the endonuclease XPF, and the damage response genes ATM, ATR, and SOG. All of these genes are known to affect gamma resistance in mammals, fungi, and plants. Because knockouts in these genes are lethal in mammals (and, in some cases, in fungi), we are using the plant Arabidopsis as a model system. We are using the NSRL facility at Brookhaven to compare the sensitivity of these lines to gamma vs. 1GeV Fe particles.

We plan to determine which DNA double strand break genes, previously identified as involved in resistance to gamma radiation, are also required for resistance to HZE radiation.

The HZE component of cosmic radiation is recalcitrant to shielding and may result in a significant increase in risk of cancer in individuals undergoing long-term (months to years) exposure. The identification of genes involved in resistance to cosmic radiation may enable us to determine which individuals are especially susceptible to the carcinogenic effects of HZE and gamma radiation. 

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: Identification of genes involved in resistance to various types of ionizing radiation has direct applications to the identification of cancer-susceptible individuals and the determination of appropriate treatment regimens for particular tumors,

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2007 
Task Progress: Effects of gamma vs HZE on plant growth: We irradiated Arabidopsis seeds of various genotypes and wild-type backgrounds in doses ranging from 25 to 200 Gy. Approximately 40 seeds were then sown on agar plates and the plates digitally imaged 8 days later. We used the application NIH Image to trace the roots and enter the length into Excel for statistical analyses.

In no case did irradiation prohibit germination- at these doses Arabidopsis embryos do not die. Germination is driven by cell expansion, not cell division, but elongation of the root to lengths greater than approx. 1.5 mm requires cell division. At the highest doses of Fe ions (200 Gy), cell expansion as well as cell division was inhibited. Approximately twice this dose of gamma would be required to induce this effect on cell elongation.

In order to contrast sensitivity in a quantitative fashion, we interpolated our dose-response curves to estimate the dose required to inhibit root growth at 8 days by 50%. As shown in Table I below, most mutants that are sensitive to gamma radiation are also sensitive to HZE. In almost all cases, the biological effectiveness of HZE was about 3-fold that of gamma radiation, suggesting the all of the mutations tested play similarly important roles in resistance to the effects of both types of radiation on root growth. The RBE numbers, however, need additional statistical analysis to determine their significance. The checkpoint genes ATR and ATM differed widely in their effects on resistance to HZE. Plants defective in ATR were relatively resistant to HZE vs. gamma (RBE = 2.7, rather than 3.7), while ATM knockouts were relatively sensitive (RBE = 4.5). Correct interpretation of this result would depend on whether growth inhibition in these mutants is due to the prolonged imposition of a checkpoint, or to the failure of a checkpoint and resulting cell death. The LOH data presented below in Figure 2 suggests that the relatively HZE-resistant growth of atr vs. atm may be due to the loss of the ATR-imposed checkpoint, resulting in enhanced loss of heterozygosity in the prematurely dividing cells.

We also find that the sog mutation, a defect in a transcription factor required for checkpoint response to gamma radiation in the apical meristem, has little effect on root meristem response to either gamma or HZE.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2006 
Task Progress: Introduction. Ionizing radiation induces oxidative damage, which affects all cellular components. However, the most significant form of damage induced by IR, in terms of cell death, mutagenesis, carcinogenesis, and effects on the cell cycle, is clustered DNA damage, often resulting in the formation of DNA double strand breaks. The DNA damage induced by high mass, high energy (HZE) space radiation is thought to be more complex than that induced by gamma radiation, as HZE particles generate a denser trail of ionization events in their wake. We proposed to determine whether the same DNA repair pathways required for resistance to gamma radiation are also required for resistance to HZE particles. Because of the importance of double strand break (DSB) repair in prevention of carcinogenic “loss of heterozygosity” events, we have focused on DSB repair pathways. However, we are also interested in signal transduction pathways involved in the detection of IR induced damage- these pathways are important initiators of responses to IR, including cell cycle arrest, induction of repair, and induction of cell death. Thus we are investigating the effects of mutations in the nonhomologous end joining genes DNA ligase IV (LigIV), and Ku80, the endonuclease XPF, and the damage response genes ATM, ATR, and SOG. These genes are known to affect gamma resistance in mammals, fungi, and plants. Because knockouts in these genes are lethal in mammals (and, in some cases, in fungi), we are using the plant Arabidopsis as a model system. We are using the NSRL facility at Brookhaven to compare the sensitivity of these lines to gamma vs. 1GeV Fe particles. This is our second year of support; we are on no cost extension.

So far, we have performed irradiations of seeds in the NSRL5, NSRL6, NSRL7 and the June 2006 runs. Our first two runs enabled us to determine the approximate doses and protocols that would be most useful for quantification of sensitivity. Our third run enabled us to compare the effects of HZE vs. gamma radiation in a variety of repair- or response-defective lines, though germination was poor in a few of the lines, making interpretation difficult in one matched set of lines. We also obtained preliminary data for our assays of loss of heterozygosity. When we applied for a fourth run, we were informed that, as we were on no-cost extension, we were not eligible to use the BNL facility, and our application was rejected. Fortunately, the Director was kind enough to make an exception for us, enabling us to irradiate a fourth time. Because we still had funding available, we added a new experiment to our list: a comparison of the transcriptome, over a 1-24 hr time course, in wild-type vs. atm- lines, with gamma vs. HZE. These RNAs from irradiated plants are currently being prepared.

Effects of gamma vs HZE on plant growth: We can report on our NSRL 7 results here; our June 2006 results are still being processed. We irradiated Arabidopsis seeds of various genotypes in doses ranging from 25 to 200 Gy. Approximately 40 seeds were then sown on agar plates and the plates digitally imaged 8 days later. We used the application NIH Image to trace the roots and enter the length into Excel for statistical analyses.

In no case did irradiation prohibit germination- at these doses Arabidopsis embryos do not die. Germination is driven by cell expansion, not cell division. Elongation of the root to lengths greater than approx. 1.5 mm requires cell division, and substantial inhibition of root growth was observed with irradiation. At the highest doses of Fe ions (200 Gy), cell expansion as well as cell division was inhibited. Approximately twice this dose of gamma would be required to induce this effect on cell elongation.

In order to contrast sensitivity in a quantitative fashion, we plan interpolated our dose-response curves to find the dose required to inhibit root growth at 8 days by 50%. Most mutants that are sensitive to gamma radiation are also sensitive to HZE. However, the XPF gene product, which is probably required for the repair of relatively rare crosslinks induced by ionizing radiation, has little effect on sensitivity to HZE. Interestingly, the sog mutation, a checkpoint gene that suppresses the growth-inhibitory effects of gamma radiation in gamma-irradiated xpf seeds, may slightly enhance, rather than suppress, HZE sensitivity. However, the seeds for the wild-type control in this xpf arm of the experiment had poor germination and irregular growth, and we need to verify this result with our results from our June 2006 run.

Loss of heterozygosity (LOH). Here we report on our results from June, 2006. The growth assay described above reflects the combined effects of ionizing radiation on both cell survival and on the induction of cell cycle arrest (at these doses, no impact on cell size was observed). Here we employ a sectoring assay to determine the effects of each of these genotype/treatments on genetic stability. Each line carries, heterozygously, an albino allele near the tip of chromosome II. Many events can produce a sector, including loss of the chromosome, loss of the distal end of the chromosome, point-like mutations at the wild-type locus, or somatic recombination with the homolog. While the growth assay measures decreases in production of new cells (a combination of the effects of cell death and cell arrest) this assay measures the production of mutant sectors (requiring both a loss of heterozygosity and continued cell growth). Thus, not surprisingly, while all repair defective lines showed some increased susceptibility to IR induced LOH, the checkpoint defective mutants were far more sensitive to this effect than the repair defective mutants. Interestingly, the atm and atr mutants responded differently to each type of radiation. We would like to repeat this irradiation to confirm this result, and plan to use the NSRL facility again on a “pay as you go basis”, using funding from our DOE grant on checkpoint mechanisms in Arabidopsis.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2005 
Task Progress: Ionizing radiation induces oxidative damage, which affects all cellular components. However, the most significant form of damage induced by IR, in terms of cell death, mutagenesis, carcinogenesis, and effects on the cell cycle, is clustered DNA damage, often resulting in the formation of DNA double strand breaks. The DNA damage induced by high mass, high energy (HZE) space radiation is though to be more complex than that induced by gamma radiation, as HZE particles generate a denser trail of ionization events in their wake. We proposed to determine whether the same DNA repair pathways required for resistance to gamma radiation are also required for resistance to HZE particles. Because of the importance of double strand break (DSB) repair in prevention of carcinogenic “loss of heterozygosity” events, we have focused on DSB repair pathways. However, we are also interested in signal transduction pathways involved in the detection of IR induced damage- these pathways are important initiators of responses to IR, including cell cycle arrest, induction of repair, and induction of cell death. Thus we are investigating the effects of mutations in the nonhomologous end joining genes DNA ligase IV (LigIV), and Ku80, the endonuclease XPF, and the damage response genes ATM, ATR, and SOG. These genes are known to affect gamma resistance in mammals, fungi, and plants. Because knockouts in these genes are lethal in mammals (and, in some cases, in fungi), we are using the plant Arabidopsis as a model system. We are using the NSRL facility at Brookhaven to compare the sensitivity of these lines to gamma vs. 1GeV Fe particles. This is our first year of support.

So far, we have performed irradiations of seeds in the NSRL5 and NSRL6 runs. Our first run enabled us to determine the approximate doses that would be most useful for quantification of sensitivity. Our second run was performed just a few days ago and we are still processing the resulting data. However, we can report, qualitatively, on our results.

We irradiated Arabidopsis seeds of various genotypes and wild-type backgrounds in doses ranging from 25 to 200 Gy. Approximately 15 seeds were then sown on agar plates and root length scored 6 days later. In no case did irradiation inhibit germination- at these doses Arabidopsis embryos do not die, but cell division is inhibited, probably through a programmed checkpoint response. Germination is driven by cell expansion, not cell division. Elongation of the root to lengths greater than approx. 2mm also requires cell division. At the highest doses of Fe ions, cell expansion as well as cell division was inhibited. Approximate twice this dose (400 Gy) of gamma would be required to induce this effect.

In order to contrast sensitivity in a quantitative fashion. we plan to determine the dose required to inhibit root growth at day 6 by 50%. This dose will be largely reflective of effects of radiation on cell division. We will also score true leaf production (by counting post-embryonic leaves) at day 12, as the production of postembryonic organs is strictly dependent on cell division, also. Thus this will serve as an independent measure, in a different tissue type, of the same effect.

Our rough estimation of root growth in has produced four preliminary observations:

a) The biological effectiveness of Fe ions, in terms of their effect on both cell division and cell expansion, is about 2.5 fold that of gamma.

b) Every DNA repair mutant tested for sensitivity to Fe (Ku80, Lig4, Xpf) has indeed been sensitive to Fe, as well as gamma. Thus all of these repair genes play a role in the repair of HZE-induced damage.

c) We have observed no significant variation in the sensitivities to Fe or gamma of our three wild-type accessions: Co, Ler, and Ws. Thus among these commonly used ecotypes there is no significant natural variation in IR sensitivity.

d) Both atr and atm knockouts are sensitive to both gamma and Fe, suggesting, again, that these checkpoint/signal transduction proteins both have important roles in resistance to HZE as well as gamma radiation.

We plan to perform another run later this year, repeating most of the doses performed this round and adding a couple of additional, lower doses that should provide a more accurate estimate of the dose required for 50% inhibition of growth. We will continue our work quantifying Fe vs. gamma-induced H2AX foci, and we will obtain an estimate of the doses required to produce loss of heterozygosity in various Arabidopsis lines.
