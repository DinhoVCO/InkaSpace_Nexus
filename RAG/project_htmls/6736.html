Flight Assignment/Project Notes: NOTE: Received NCE through 9/30/2007, per A. Chu (9/06)

Task Description: We demonstrated that the orientation of lateral organs in plants (roots, branches and leaves) is determined by what is called the gravitropic set-point angle (GSA). That is, the specific orientation of lateral organs is genetically determined but controlled by the gravitropic responses of the lateral organs. The goal of this project was to use a combination of genetic, molecular, and physiological approaches to better understand the role of gravity in guiding whole plant development. Specifically, we used Arabidopsis as a model system for identifying genes required for controlling the GSA of later roots, rosette leaves and inflorescence branches. We have isolated a number of mutants that display altered GSA responses in lateral organs. The mutants have been designated mgsa mutants for modified gravitropic set-point angles. The molecular and physiological analyses of the mgsa mutants will provide new insight into the basis of GSA regulation and, thus, a better understanding of the physiological and molecular mechanisms by which plants use gravity to control their architecture.

To discover and characterize genes that are required for controlling the orientaion of lateral organs during plant development and to understand the mechanisms by which plants sense and respond to gravity.

The molecular and physiological analyses of the gsa mutants will provide new insight into the basis of GSA regulation and, thus, a better understanding of the physiological and molecular mechanisms by which plants use gravity to control their architecture. Since the bulk of a plant consists of lateral organs, our studies will contribute to a better understanding of the fundamental developmental biology of plant form and may eventually lead to the development of improved plant varieties for growth on future long-term space missions or for agriculture.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: Since the bulk of a plant consists of lateral organs, our studies will contribute to our understanding of the fundamental developmental biology of plant form and may eventually lead to the development of improved plant varieties for high-density agricultural. Unfortunately, since the project was terminated prematurely by NASA, the impact of this work will not be realized until another funding agency supports the project.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2007 
Task Progress: We identified several genes that, when mutated, affect the orientation of lateral organs in Arabidopsis. A few of the genes specifically affect lateral roots and others affect the orientation of leaves and branches. We were able to map one of the genes, MGSA4 to a small region on chromosome 4. Plants with a mutant version of mgsa4 display vertically oriented leaves and inflorescence branches. Identification of the MGSA4 gene could potentially allow genetic engineering of plants with altered architectures for agronomic and ornamental uses.

We characterized the gravitropic behavior of lateral roots and shoots in Arabidopsis and a few other plant species. In all cases, we found that lateral organs orient relative to the gravity vector using the same basic gravitropic response system used buy the primary roots and shoots. However, unlike the primary root and shoot, which typically maintain vertical orientation, lateral organs position themselves more horizontally. Moreover, they change their orientation in a developmentally dependent manner. For example, a young Arabidopsis branch elongates at near 90 degrees from vertical for a few days but then repositions to angles of about 60 degrees from vertical. Our studies showed that these developmental changes in branch orientation result from a fundamental change in the gravitropic set point (GSA) of the organ. Our results provide the most complete analysis of the GSA and demonstrate how plant architecture is regulated by its alteration during normal plant development.

We identified several genes that when mutated alter the orientation of lateral organs. We identified four genes that specifically affect orientation of lateral roots and another three that specifically affect leaf and branch orientation. We were able to identify one of the root genes to be an allele of RHD3, which had been previously shown to affect root hair development. We initiated mapping of the other root-specific mutants but the phenotypes are difficult to score and progress was slow. The phenotype of the shoot mutants was more robust so we focused on one of those mutants, mgsa4, for detailed mapping and eventually gene identification.

We characterized the phenotype of the mgsa4 mutant in detail and fund that the altered gravitropic response is restricted to the lateral organs of the above ground part of the plant. Specifically, the main shoot displays normal vertical orientation and gravitropic responses during all stages of development. The leaves and lateral branches also display normal gravitropism in response to changes in orientation. That is, if a branch is reoriented, it reorients back to the original position just as robustly as in wild type plants. However, the lateral organs display a basal orientation that is as much as 40 degrees more vertical than wild type. Based on our physiological analyses, we were able to conclude that the mgsa4 gene specifically affects the overall orientation of the lateral organs without affecting the basal gravitropic machinery. Our results represent a novel demonstration of a fundamental aspect of plant gravity responses that has not been described before.

Unfortunately, because this project was terminated prematurely, we were not able to identify the gene at the molecular level. Project personnel left the lab and manuscripts that were in early stages of preparation could not be completed.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2006 
Task Progress: We have continued to identify Arabidopsis mutants that have altered lateral root orientation while maintaining normal primary root orientation. Likewise, we have identified mutants that have altered leaf and/or branch orientation. All of these mutants retain normal gravitropic responses in the primary roots and primary shoot. Moreover, the lateral organs also display a robust gravitropic response. The mutations identified appear to specifically affect the GSA of the lateral organs, causing them to orient differently than in wild-type Arabidopsis plants. We have designated the mutants as mgsa mutants for modified gravitropic set-point angles.

We have made significant progress towards the molecular identification of the MGSA4 gene. Plants mutant for mgsa4, orient their leaves and inflorescence branches in a much more vertical position than wild-type plants. As a result the mutant has a significantly different architecture in the above-ground parts of the plant. Through fine-scale genetic mapping of mgsa4, we have narrowed the mutant locus to a region on chromosome 4. Transformation of mgsa4 mutant plants with wild-type DNA from BACS that are located in the region of mgsa4 have yielded plants that have wild-type leaf and branch orientations indicating the plants have been transformed with a wild-type copy of the MGSA4 gene. Although we have not yet identified the specific gene responsible for rescuing the mutant, we have been able to narrow the list of candidates to about 10 genes. We have also mapped the mgsa3 mutation to a region on chromosome 1. The lateral roots of the mgsa3 grow more horizontally than in wild-type plants.

As part of our analysis of the GSA response of lateral organs, we have carried out a detailed analysis of the GSA response in wild-type Arabidopsis plants and several other plant species using time-lapse methods. Our results demonstrate that the orientation of lateral organs in diverse species is primarily determined by the gravity responses of the lateral organs. Our results also demonstrate that the GSA response changes with the developmental stage of the lateral organs. Based on these physiological studies, we conclude that the overall architecture of a plant results from the orchestrated developmental changes in the GSA of the lateral organs. Moreover, the GSA can be modified by environmental stimuli such as the quality (red/far-red ratio) and quantity of light. A paper describing the effect of light on leaf orientation is in press and a more detailed manuscript describing the relationship between lateral organ orientation and plant architecture is in preparation.

Task Description: Using Arabidopsis as a model, we have found that this change in orientation is not simply due to the onset of gravitropic competence, as non-vertical lateral roots are capable of both positive and negative gravitropism. Thus, the horizontal growth of the new lateral roots is determined by what is called the gravitropic set-point angle (GSA). In Arabidopsis shoots, rosette leaves and inflorescence branches also display GSA-dependent developmental changes in their orientation. This developmental control of GSA in lateral organs of Arabidopsis provides a useful system for investigating components involved in regulating the directionality of tropistic responses. The goal of our proposal is to use a combination of genetic, molecular, and physiological approaches to better understand the role of gravity in guiding whole plant development. We propose to identify Arabidopsis mutants that have altered lateral root orientation while maintaining normal primary root orientation. Likewise, we will identify mutants that have altered leaf and/or branch orientation. We have already identified several such gsa mutants representing four loci. We propose to continue screening for new gsa mutants, clone and characterize the mutant and wild-type alleles of the genes already identified, and determinee the function of the gene products. The molecular and physiological analyses of the gsa mutants will provide new insight into the basis of GSA regulation and, thus, a better understanding of the physiological and molecular mechanisms by which plants use gravity to control their architecture. Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2005 
Task Progress: We have identified three mutations that specifically affect the gravity-dependent orientation of lateral roots that represent different genes. In addition, we have found 2 other likely mutants that are currently undergoing further analysis to determine if they represent novel loci. We have also confirmed the identification of three genes that specifically affect the gravity-dependent orientation of lateral organs in Arabidopsis shoots. Mutations in these latter 3 genes lead to plants with more vertical leaves and lateral branches. Several additional mutant plants with vertical leaf angles have been isolated and are currently being tested to determine if they represent different genes. We have genetically mapped one of the shoot mutants, mgsa4, to a small region of chromosome 4 that spans 2 BAC clones. These BACs have been subcloned as 10 to 15 KB cosmids and are being transformed into the mgsa4 mutant in an attempt to rescue the mutant phenotype. Once transformed plants are identified that have wild-type leaf orientation, we should be able to narrow the mutant gene to several candidates. These candidate genes will be sequenced to determine which gene represents MGSA4. We have also been making progress mapping the root orientation mutant, mgsa3. However, the map interval is still too large to proceed with rescue attempts.

We have also been making good progress with physiological characterization of the GSA responses of the mutant and wild-type plants. Papers describing these studies are being prepared for submission. An important discovery made during these studies has come through studies of how the gravity response of lateral roots is under apical control of the primary root. Our experiments have shown that a transmissible signal coming from the primary root apex alters the growth of lateral roots upon gravistimulation of the plant. Our results indicate that while auxin is involved, another unidentified signal is involved. We have also found that the auxin gradient that develops upon reorientation of primary roots, also develops transiently during reorientation of lateral roots. However, when a lateral root is growing at its set point angle, no auxin gradient can be detected. Thus, the auxin gradient develops only when an organs orientation is changed from its set point and a homeostatic uniform distribution is maintained at the set point.
