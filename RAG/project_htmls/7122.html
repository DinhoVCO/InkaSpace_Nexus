Task Description: The overall goal of the proposed research is to identify the structural and functional changes that occur in the utricle of the vestibular labyrinth as the result of transitions from one gravitational level into another. This information is critical for advancing our understanding of neurovestibular adaptation and its sequelae. Our strategy is to use centrifugation as a tool for temporarily altering the gravitational stimulus. We are particularly interested in determining the afferent firing rate and response characteristics and the morphological changes in the hair cell-afferent complexes found in the utricle that are modified when the organism moves from normal gravity (1G) to hyper-gravity (hyper-G) and in the opposite sense.

In the utricular otolith organ it was found that the afferent response sensitivity to head acceleration (13, fish) as well as the number of synaptic bodies in hair cells (56, rats) increase as the result of exposure to microgravity (µG). A preliminary result showed that exposure to hyper-G by centrifugation decreases the number of synaptic bodies (56, rats). The period of afferent re-adaptation to normal gravity in fish (13) was found to be close to the period of behavioral adaptation in humans (55). Re-adaptation is defined here as the process of adaptation to normal gravity. These results suggest the possibility that the process of adaptation to µG might be mimicked by the process of re-adaptation from a hyper-G level. However, the experiments that would combine the results of µG and hyper-G experiments for the same species are absent. Further, the correlations of the synaptic re-arrangement with afferent sensitivity following exposure to altered gravity for the same species remain undefined. The purpose of the present proposal is to fill these gaps.

Our model is the adult toadfish, Opsanus tau. Fish provide an excellent model system for gravitational research (7). We have everything in place to successfully complete the proposed set of experiments and modeling. Of prime importance is the fact that in the same species a comprehensive evaluation of the neural response to µG (13) and hyper-G (present proposal) exposure will be made. There are four specific aims:

Aim 1. Response characteristics of utricular afferents to Centrifugation. Fish will be placed in groups and exposed to different magnitudes (1.5 and 3 g) and duration (1-16 days) of centrifugation. On Center Controls and ground controls are included. Using electrophysiological techniques, individual utricular afferents will be physiologically characterized based on gravitoinertial acceleration testing. Afferent response properties to efferent vestibular activation will be specified. The duration of afferent recording session is tempered by the need to freeze the tissue in time to permit the measurements of structure-function correlations. To match the conditions under which post-flight experiments were performed (13) changes in afferent sensitivity caused by hyper-G will be studied from 1-24 hrs after centrifugation, and re-adaptation after 24-96 hrs in 1G .

Aim 2. Morphology of hair cell-afferent synaptic complexes. Combining the techniques of labeling of individual afferents with light and transmission electron microscopy a complete characterization of the afferent is possible: its physiology, its dendritic morphology in the macula, and the extent and quantification of synapses formed from the innervated hair cells. Visualization of complex 3-D microcircuitry will also reveal precise connectivity within the macula that is essential for understanding neurovestibular adaptation. Synaptic body counts will be performed to address the fundamental question of the correlation of afferent response sensitivity with hair cell synapse number.

Aim 3. Merging the structure and function of otolith afferents and their relationships to hyper-G. The specific times and thresholds of the afferent response adaptation and re-adaptation and the structural changes associated with the hair cell-afferent synapse will be determined by varying the magnitude and duration of centrifugation.

Aim 4. Modeling. Based on known methodologies and the present experimental results, research will be directed towards the establishment of phenomenological models describing the adaptive changes in utricular afferents in altered gravity conditions. We will examine the relationship between the afferent response and the number of synapses in normal and in hyper-G conditions. A model of toadfish otolith mechanics in these conditions will be also developed.

The four specific aims are directed to achieve the main goals: 1) to test the correlations of change in the gravitational level and the change in afferent response characteristics caused by head acceleration and the potential underlying changes in the morphology of the associated hair cell-afferent synaptic complexes, 2) to examine the role of efferent vestibular system on these correlations, 3) to test (propose) the possible mechanisms responsible for these correlations; and 4) to test if the transition from hyper-G to normal gravity can be used to mimic the effects of neural adaptation to µG.

We expect that convergence of findings in µG and hyper-G will provide a model of neurovestibular plasticity, and will lead to the development of novel test protocols for both ground- and space-based research. As we learn about the basic cellular processes in vestibular neurons, countermeasures will eventually be developed to reduce the symptoms associated with Space Motion Sickness or even modify synaptic sensitivity in preparation of entry into a higher gravity level.

The overarching hypotheses to be evaluated are: One, the gravitoinertial sensitivity of otolith nerve afferents is altered as a consequence of the animal's exposure to hyper-G conditions as well as µG. It is reasoned that the transition from 1G to hyper-G will decrease otolith afferent sensitivity (output in impulses per sec/input in G), and the transition from hyper-G back to 1G will increase it (and back to normal levels). Thus, the transition from hyper-G to normal gravity may serve as an imitator of neurovestibular sequelae to µG. Two, hair cell-afferent synaptic complexes are modified by the altered gravity condition: the up- or down regulation of synaptic bodies is specific to the direction of transition from 1G. The change in number of synapses is directly correlated with afferent response sensitivity. Three, afferent adaptation mechanisms may involve either self-learning processes confined to the hair cell or responses of the hair cell-afferent system to feedback signal(s) that originate from a misbalance of information coming from otolith and extra-otolith contributor(s), or a combination of both. (These mechanisms will be considered by modeling based on the experimental findings.) Four, efferent vestibular actions are not uniform on hair cells and afferents, and it is proposed that the changes brought on in otolith afferents by the transitions from one gravity state to another will be most prevalent and coincident in afferents strongly affected by efferent activation. If this proves to be the case, then the next step is to directly query the efferent neurons themselves.

The overall goal of the proposed research is to identify the structural and functional changes that occur in the utricle of the vertebrate vestibular labyrinth as the result of transitions from one gravitational level into another. This information is critical for advancing our understanding of neurovestibular adaptation and its sequelae, and for the effective design and validation of countermeasures to benefit astronaut health and performance. Our strategy is to use centrifugation as a tool for temporarily altering the gravitational stimulus. We are particularly interested in determining the afferent firing rate and response characteristics and the morphological changes in the hair cell-afferent complexes found in the utricle that are modified when the organism moves from normal gravity (1G) to hyper-gravity (hyper-G) and in the opposite sense. This study is a natural analog to our previous studies aboard STS-90 and 95, and will provide a comprehensive evaluation of the neural response to µG and hyper-G in the same species.

We expect that convergence of findings in µG and hyper-G will provide a solid model of neurovestibular plasticity, and will lead to the development of novel test protocols for both ground- and space-based research. As we learn about the basic cellular processes in vestibular neurons, countermeasures will eventually be developed to reduce the symptoms associated with Space Motion Sickness, benefit sensorimotor coordination and performance, or even down regulate synaptic sensitivity in preparation of entry into a higher gravity level.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: The functional importance of head stabilization is seen by our ability to change gaze orientation and maintain binocular fixation of objects of interest during not only standing but also during locomotor activities, and our ability to detect self-motion and adaptively react to applied perturbations of the head and body. In the space environment the vestibular system is profoundly affected by the loss of he gravity vector. However, conditions on Earth can seriously affect the individual's ability to functional normally. In addition aging places a serious challenge to the vestibular and neural centers responsible for posture and movement control. Disorders of the vestibular system affect head, gaze and posture stabilization, and can dramatically alter the patients willingness to induce self-motion and can lead to trepidation of normal activities. These studies will also provide important insights into vestibular function, and may help in the diagnosis and treatment of vestibular patients seen in the clinic.

Task Progress:

We have made significant progress towards reaching our stated goals. Two separate and successful centrifugation trials were conducted to examine the response of utricular nerve afferents to 3g and 1.5g hyper-gravity stimulation and the time course of re-adaptation to Earth's 1g following centrifugation. The centrifugation stimulus and configuration have worked perfectly and without incident. Further, we have been able to successfully record the electrophysiological responses of more that 60-70 separate afferents in each experiment, twice what we had anticipated. This increase in sample has provided a very reliable measure to evaluate response between experiments. In addition, we designed a separate rate table to conduct the On-Center Control experiments. This design worked perfectly, allowing the subject to be rotated at 38 rpm in a control aquatic system with stabile water temperature of 12°C.

As of October 2008, four (4) fish were studied following 3G exposure at 1, 2, 3, and 8 days; five (5) fish at 4 and 16 days; and three (3) fish at 5, 24, and 32 days (total 32 fish). The 24- and 32-Day groups were added to our protocol based on our results at 16-Day exposure to 3G. For the Re-adaptation experiments, three (3) fish were studied following 4-days of 3G exposure and re-adaptation periods of 1, 2, 4, 6 and 8 days (total 15 fish). In the next group of Re-adaptation experiments, three (3) fish were studied following 16-days of 3G exposure and re-adaptation periods of 2, 6 and 8 days, and four (4) at 4-days of re-adaptation to 1G (total 13 fish). We successfully completed four (4) fish exposed to 1.5G at 4-days and 16-days (total 8 fish). We successfully completed On-Center Control experiments of 38 rpm for 4- and 16-Day with 4 fish in each group.

Results and conclusions based on the analyses conducted to date on the effect of altered gravity levels on utricular afferent response. Data were collected from 2 orbital shuttle missions: STS-90 and STS-95. Within the first day after landing, the magnitude of response to an applied acceleration was significantly greater than for controls. The number of synaptic bodies in type II hair cells in rodent is labile, increasing following exposure to microgravity, suggesting an increase in synaptic density was responsible for the upregulation of sensitivity. 3-g Centrifugation data. The data must be viewed still as preliminary, because not all data obtained have been analyzed. The mean maximum sensitivity (Smax) of control afferents to a standard translation is 2333 ± 1460 (SD) imp/s/g . The Smax for each group based on days of 3-g exposure is given. A significant elevation at 4-Day and a significant decrease at 16-Day (p<0.0001) of Smax were observed. This is an important finding: the response of the utricle to hypergravity is modifiable and bi-modal. With 4 days of recovery (Re-adaptation) following 3-g exposure for 4- and 16-Days the gain did not return (fall or rise) to control levels. Percent plot shows the wide separation in Smax between 4-Day (red circles) and control fish, and the response of ~80% of afferents in16-Day group (blue diamonds) diverged from control afferents.

Length of time needed in the 1-g environment following 3-g exposure of 4 and 16 days for afferents to return to baseline levels. Only partial analysis is complete and recovery times of 1, 2, and 6 days have not yet been analyzed. The general conclusion is: the hypersensitivity within the first days of 3-g exposure and the hyposensitivity observed after longer durations of 3-g exposure require at least 4 or more days of return to 1-g to complete the readaptation process. It should be stated again that horizontal canal afferents responded in a normal fashion within each of the experimental groups following 3-g exposure with or without 1-g recovery days and on-center control tests, suggesting that a general labyrinthine mechanism independent of gravity state is unlikely. Quantification of individual synaptic bodies in utricular hair cells in each group is a work in progress.

Functional Considerations and General Conclusions: A change in gravity level has a profound effect on how an organism maintains equilibrium, and the neural response might involve the peripheral otolith receptors, the brain or both. In turn, the change of the sensitivity of peripheral otolith receptors in altered G may depend on the change of physical parameters of the otolith structures responsible for transferring the dynamics of head into the dynamics of the hair cell bundle deformations, as well as on the change of properties of local peripheral neural structures, first of all, synapses.

The early hypersensitivity was unexpected, and the underlying mechanism is unknown. It is possible that the altered gravity initiates first an upregulation in gain in response to a challenge, any challenge. In this scenario we would expect to observe hypersensitivity in the early stages of µG exposure as well. Regrettably, those data are lacking.

One of the hypotheses considered in the project was formulated as follows: ”afferent adaptation mechanisms (unquote, to altered gravity, quote) may involve either self-learning processes confined to the hair cell or responses of the hair cell-afferent system to feedback signal(s) that originate from a misbalance of information coming from otolith and extra-otolith contributor(s), or a combination of both”. The underlying basic assumptions of the feedback hypothesis were provided in the previous report. The experimental findings indicate that sensory plasticity ideed exists in the utricle following exposure to altered gravity conditions. However the mechanisms of neural adaptation in these experiments are still not clear. Theoretical work is in progress, and its progress is dependent on available data. Experimental analysis is still in progress. This analysis includes both the electrophysiological data and synaptic body counts at the transmission electron microscopic level.

Three, afferent adaptation mechanisms may involve either self-learning processes confined to the hair cell or responses of the hair cell-afferent system to feedback signal(s) that originate from a misbalance of information coming from otolith and extra-otolith contributor(s), or a combination of both. (These mechanisms will be considered by modeling based on the experimental findings.) Four, efferent vestibular actions are not uniform on hair cells and afferents, and it is proposed that the changes brought on in otolith afferents by the transitions from one gravity state to another will be most prevalent and coincident in afferents strongly affected by efferent activation. If this proves to be the case, then the next step is to directly query the efferent neurons themselves.

The overall goal of the proposed research is to identify the structural and functional changes that occur in the utricle of the vertebrate vestibular labyrinth as the result of transitions from one gravitational level into another. This information is critical for advancing our understanding of neurovestibular adaptation and its sequelae, and for the effective design and validation of countermeasures to benefit astronaut health and performance. Our strategy is to use centrifugation as a tool for temporarily altering the gravitational stimulus. We are particularly interested in determining the afferent firing rate and response characteristics and the morphological changes in the hair cell-afferent complexes found in the utricle that are modified when the organism moves from normal gravity (1G) to hyper-gravity (hyper-G) and in the opposite sense. This study is a natural analog to our previous studies aboard STS-90 and 95, and will provide a comprehensive evaluation of the neural response to µG and hyper-G in the same species.

We expect that convergence of findings in µG and hyper-G will provide a solid model of neurovestibular plasticity, and will lead to the development of novel test protocols for both ground- and space-based research. As we learn about the basic cellular processes in vestibular neurons, countermeasures will eventually be developed to reduce the symptoms associated with Space Motion Sickness, benefit sensorimotor coordination and performance, or even down regulate synaptic sensitivity in preparation of entry into a higher gravity level.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: The functional importance of head stabilization is seen by our ability to change gaze orientation and maintain binocular fixation of objects of interest during not only standing but also during locomotor activities, and our ability to detect self-motion and adaptively react to applied perturbations of the head and body. In the space environment the vestibular system is profoundly affected by the loss of he gravity vector. However, conditions on Earth can seriously affect the individual's ability to functional normally. In addition aging places a serious challenge to the vestibular and neural centers responsible for posture and movement control. Disorders of the vestibular system affect head, gaze and posture stabilization, and can dramatically alter the patients willingness to induce self-motion and can lead to trepidation of normal activities. These studies will also provide important insights into vestibular function, and may help in the diagnosis and treatment of vestibular patients seen in the clinic.

Four separate and successful centrifugation trails were conducted to examine the response of utricular nerve afferents to 3g and 1.5g hyper-gravity stimulation and the time course of re-adaptation to Earth's 1g following centrifugation. Further, we have been able to successfully record the electrophysiological responses of more that 60-70 separate afferents in each experiment, twice what we had anticipated. This increase in sample has provided a very reliable measure to evaluate response between experiments.

The 3-Day and 5-Day groups were added to our protocol based on the results we obtained at 4-Day exposure to 3G (this will be detailed below). In the next group of Re-adaptation experiments, three (3) fish were studied following 16-days of 3G exposure and re-adaptation periods of 2, 6 and 8 days, and four (4) at 4-days of re-adaptation to 1G (total 13 fish). In the latest centrifugation trial, we successfully completed four (4) fish exposed to 1.5G at 4-days and 16-days (total 8 fish). The next centrifugation will complete the experiments needed for the Re-adaptation portion of the study (approximately 15 fish) and the 1.5G exposure and Re-adaptation (approximately 15 fish). In the next period On-Center control experiments also will be conducted. To this end we recently acquired a separate and adequately equipped room that is perfect for these experiments. An 80 ft-lb DC torque motor/rate table was installed in the room. We are currently developing the suitable aquarium for this task.

Previously, we studied the response of utricular afferents following exposure to microgravity ( Boyle et al. [2001] Neural readaptation to 1G following return from space. J. Neurophysiol. 86: 2118-2122). Data were collected from 2 orbital shuttle missions: STS-90 and STS-95. Within the first day after landing, the magnitude of response to an applied acceleration was significantly greater, 3-fold upregulation of the reponse sensitivity, than for controls. Within about 30 hours post-landing the response return to normal values. The number of synaptic bodies in type II hair cells in rodent is labile, increasing following exposure to microgravity (Ross 1993, 1994, 2000), suggesting an increase in synaptic density was responsible for the upregulation of sensitivity. The results obtained in the present study are not complete, but nonetheless exciting. The response sensitivity begins to increase after 1, 2 and 3 days of 3-g exposure, and by 4-days the sensitivity is 3-fold (p<0.0001) increased, actually mimicking the microgravity data. The sensitivity then begins to fall, and by 16-days of 3-g exposure the sensitivity is 2-fold (p<0.0001) downregulated. This is an important finding: the response of the utricle to hypergravity is modifiable and bi-modal. As mentioned in the experimental groups above, re-adaptation was studied. Here, the subjects are centrifuged at 4- or 16-days, and returned to their habitat (1-g environment), and studied at variable durations. With 4 days of recovery (Re-adaptation) following 3-g exposure for 4- and 16-Days the gain did not return (fall or rise) to control levels. The data, however, must be viewed still as preliminary because i) because of the fact that we can successfully record from more than twice the anticipated number of afferents, detailed analyzes of each experiment requires considerable amount of time, and therefore not all data obtained have been analyzed, ii) additional centrifugation experiments are ongoing and scheduled, and iii) on-center controls have not yet been performed.

Matching the function with the structure requires analysis of the synaptic structures in the hair cells. Synaptic body counts are ongoing. We examine the number of synaptic bodies in hair cells within the striolar and extra-striolar regions of the utricular macula. Synaptic bodies are determined using transmission electron microscopy from serial sections at 5K-40K maginification. To date we have data analyzed from one control utricle and one utricle from a 16-day exposure to 3-g. For 16-day exposure to 3-g there was a reduction in synaptic bodies in both the striolar, more than 2-fold decrease, and extra-striolar regions, and this was highly significant with the striolar region (p<0.0001). This finding matches the reduction in response sensitivity recorded in this experiment. This is an extremely labor-intensive effort. We had a set back in this effort: the CCD camera had a malfunction and the camera was damaged beyond repair during shipping. As result we were required to purchase a new CCD system which resulted in delays in manufacturing and installation. We are now back in full progress.

Functional Considerations and General Conclusions:

A change in gravity level has a profound effect on how an organism maintains equilibrium, and the neural response might involve the peripheral otolith receptors, the brain or both. In turn, the change of the sensitivity of peripheral otolith receptors in altered G may depend on the change of physical parameters of the otolith structures responsible for transferring the dynamics of head into the dynamics of the hair cell bundle deformations, as well as on the change of properties of local peripheral neural structures, first of all, synapses.

I. Let us analyze the first option: the change of physical parameters of fish otolith structure responsible for the sensitivity of afferent in altered gravity, as it is formulated in (1): imp/sec*g. According to (1), the sensitivity of toadfish utricular afferents measured within 10 hr of the shuttle landing (17 and 9 days flights) was sevenfold greater than the control mean. We will assume that the response of otolith afferent is proportional to the displacement of hair cell bundles. These displacements correspond to the displacement of the gel layer in which these hair cells are embedded. For rough estimate of the coefficient of proportionality, K, we can use the relationship between the gel layer displacement, U, and acceleration, A, of the head in the case of two-layered model of otolith represented by gel layer and otolith (stone) itself (2). (The analysis of various models of otolith structures and function is presented in (3)):

Ux(z)=[(1+sg)hg2/Eg][2((Delta roho/hg)+Delta rg)(z/hg)-Delta rg(z/hg)2)]Ax;

(note: this format here does not support equation text and symbols) where Ux(z) denotes the component of displacements of the gel layer parallel to the macular surface at the distance z from this surface. Ax is component of head acceleration parallel to the macular surface. Delta ro and Delta rg are the differences of densities of the otolith and the endolymph and the gel layer and endolymph correspondingly, ho and hg are thicknesses of otolith and gel layer, Eg is Young’s modulus, and sg is Poisson’s ratio of the GL layer. Thus Ux=K Ax. This equation shows that the gel displacement could be considerably increased only if Young’s modulus of gel is decreased. The variation of other parameters of cannot give us the essential growth of K. Since we assumed the linear dependence of afferent response and hair bundle displacements, it means that after micro-G Eg has to be seven times less than in normal 1G. Although mechanical parameters of biogels are very sensitive to the changes of ion compositions and electrical fields, such great changes of gel Young’s modulus seem to be unrealistic. It should be noted that the value of Eg has to be balanced by two contradictory criteria (4): it should be high enough to prevent the otolith from dropping on receptor cells and be low enough to provide the sufficient displacement of this cells caused by head acceleration. It means that if Eg decreases essentially in micro-G, the stone will be lying on the macular surface after return to 1G conditions. Therefore the change of utricular afferent sensitivity in altered G may be connected with neural plasticity of otolith organ.

II. One of the hypotheses considered in the project was formulated as follows: ”afferent adaptation mechanisms (unquote, to altered gravity, quote) may involve either self-learning processes confined to the hair cell or responses of the hair cell-afferent system to feedback signal(s) that originate from a misbalance of information coming from otolith and extra-otolith contributor(s), or a combination of both”.

It may be assumed that the possible "goal" of such self-regulation or adaptation is to compensate the change of the parameters of the system caused by the external influence and to return them into the normal physiological range of the analyzing system (5). The mechanism of adaptation depends on the mechanism of feedback existing in this system. Because of this, the goals of our work (5,6) were the structuring and analysis of the hypothesis of feedback and the demonstration of the ambiguity of possible effects of the altered gravity on formation and function of gravi-receptors derived from the data from mollusks and fish.

Summarizing these data, the feedback hypothesis may be formulated as a set of the following basic assumptions (Model) (5,6): 1) The gravi-sensing system development consists of a set of successive time-dependent parts (subprograms of development) acting during specific periods. 2) There are some mechanisms of feedbacks of local and neural origins to control the course of subprogram fulfillment and the transition from one program to another. The local feedbacks are confined by small volume of precocious receptors. 3) If the afferents and efferents are still immature, the “neural” feedback cannot work and any effect of the change of gravity on gravi-receptors during this period has local origin. It is possible that different feedbacks are involved at different periods in the development of the gravi-sensing system in altered gravity: at the earlier stages they are local and in the mature state they may have neural-like efferent nature (7, 8). 4) The altered gravity may cause direct effects in developing gravireceptors that are not related to any mechanisms designed to compensate for the possible changes of environment normally experienced by the organism. 5) Altered gravity influences those mechanisms of the system whose parameters and processes depend on gravity level. These may be internal parameters of subprograms or control parameters that determine transition from one program to another. It is possible that several feedbacks corresponding to different stages of gravi-receptor development would be sensitive to altered gravity. The examples are the changes of statolith and statoconia in Aplysia in hypergravity conditions observed in in vitro experiments (9,10). 6) It has to be stressed that every response of the gravi-sensing system to the change of altered gravity in the course of gravi-receptor formation may be realized ONLY within the limits of the existing subprograms designed during animal evolution in normal gravity conditions, possibly, for absolutely different purposes. It means, for instance, that lack of otolith weight in _G may be interpreted by the organism as a shortage of mineral component in otolith composition. To remove this limitation, probably, needs the participation of neural feedback. Returning to the previous question about the possibility of sevenfold decrease of Eg it should be noted that it is difficult to imagine the situation in 1 G conditions that would need such crucial changes of gel properties.

(It was supposed that “otolith mineralization hitherto is the only biomineralization process known, which is neuronally regulated in adaptation to an environmental parameter (i.e., gravity)”(11). Our opinion is that the possible neural regulation of otolith growth cannot be considered as some kind of adaptation to the change of gravity as an environmental parameter, because the gravity was never changed during animal development, but this neural feedback may be the response to the effect of the altered gravity on some physical, chemical or informational processes participating in otolith formation).

III. The experimental findings (1, 13) indicate that sensory plasticity exists in the utricle following exposure to altered gravity conditions. However the mechanisms of neural adaptation in these experiments are still not clear.

Two different Models may be considered as a basis of possible explanation. The first Model is relied on self-learning synaptic contacts whose properties are changing in accordance with the change of pre- and post-synaptic activities (14, 15). This mechanism of adaptation is supposed to be confined in pre-and post-synaptic relationship and does not need any external reference signal. It may be called ‘local feedback”(5). If we apply this model to utricular "receptor-afferent" system, it will mean that mechanical stimulus being transformed in hair cells in altered gravity will carry some information on the gravity level that initiates synapse modification including the synaptogenesis.

According to the analysis of feedback mechanisms (5), we may expect that the local feedback is more specific for developing organism with immature nervous system.

The second Model supposes the inclusion of a Reference system and corresponding modulating signal in the “receptor-afferent” chain. It suggests that there are two useful signals in this chain: output afferent signal A that carries the information about the cell response to mechanical stimulation and feedback signal B, the function of which is to modify the state of receptor in accordance with the first signal A information. This hypothesis says that the change of otolith dynamics caused by altered gravity generates signal B as a result of comparison of the signal A with some external reference signal C, where it is reasonable to assume that this signal C is coming from the system that is not influenced by gravity.

Possible candidates for such system can be identified through investigation of mechanisms that allow species to distinguish between gravitational and inertial accelerations. There are indications (16, 17) that the brain resolves this problem by combining and comparing the signals from multiple sensors, the semicircular canal signals being the main extra-otolith contributors. Since gravitational information sensed by the otolith is mainly related to the tilts of the head, this signal is accompanied by the signal C of the corresponding semicircular canal. When the gravity is altered, the dynamics of the otolith, for instance, the specific time of its reaction to the tilt, will be changed while, as it is supposed, the reaction C of the semicircular canals does not depend on the gravity level. The resulting misbalance induces the feedback signal B directed to the receptors to restore the balance, possibly, by modifying their sensitivity. Assuming this hypothesis is correct, the µG and hyper-G conditions will correspond to different situations. We may expect that in µG the relatively slow tilt will not cause the displacement of the otolith relative to receptor cells and thus the signal A will be absent while the signal C will exist. Meanwhile in hyper-G the signal B will be not only present but enhanced relative to 1G conditions.

The functioning of such feedback will be effective if the Reference system would not depend on the signal A information. In our case it suggests the independence of the function of the system of semicircular canals on gravity. The works (18-21) are devoted to the analysis of this problem.

The function of the semicircular canals (SC) as hydromechanical sensors of angular head rotation is based on the equality of specific gravities of the cupula and endolymph. Without this equality, the information provided by the SC would depend on the spatial orientation of canals relative to gravity and the axis of rotation. However, we may assume that the equality of densities of the cupula and endolymph is not precise but approximate because: i) the compositions of the cupula and endolymph are different; ii) these densities can change due to the change of pressure, temperature, metabolic processes or due to the ingestion of proper doses of substances that can pass a blood-labyrinth barrier and has a specific gravity different from endolymph (e.g. ethanol, heavy water, glycerol). Hence, we may expect that due to the very high sensitivity of the SC (~0.058 sec-2 ), even the small density differences (DD) (~10-4g/cm3) can influence its function, and that this influence depends on the conditions of canal stimulation. The works (18-21) aimed to examine this hypothesis and analyze the parameters of the SC and mechanical stimulation under which the effect of the difference of densities on the functioning of a cupulo-endolympatic system could be observed. The consideration was based on a simplified mathematical model where the canal was approximated by a torus and the cupula by a piston. If the densities are equal, the model transforms to Steinhauzen’s phenomenological equation.(The various approaches to modeling the SC are described in (22)). The present analysis is focused on two cases of canal stimulation: the rotation with constant angular velocity w and harmonic oscillation of the angular velocity; and two types of spatial orientation of the SC: i) the gravity vector and axis of rotation lie in the plane of the canal, ii) the axis of rotation and gravity vector are normal to the canal plane. The difference of densities creates the moments of inertial force relative to the axis of the canal and thus cupula dynamics becomes dependent on the orientation of the gravity vector relative to the canal plane and the axis of rotation, as well as the distance between the axis of rotation and the center of semicircular canal (l). It leads to the shift of the cupula to a new position of equilibrium that depends on the gravity and parameters of rotation l and w. The estimates show that this shift can exceed the threshold of sensitivity. A new position of cupula equilibrium may invoke the illusion of the permanent angular acceleration of the head even when the frequency =const. Due to the DD the cupula elasticity coefficient in the equation of the motion becomes effectively dependent on the parameters of harmonic stimulation that results in the onset of cupula oscillations with multiple frequencies and the distortion of cupula dynamics relative to harmonic stimulation. Even for a very small DD, its effect can be revealed by the appropriate conditions of stimulation. Different effects can take place in the different canals of the same labyrinth at the same time. The effects caused by the DD can be developed due to the ingestion of some substances and/or in conditions of artificial gravitation on space stations.

It is important that in altered gravity there are at least two conditions that may cause or enhance the DD effects: the variation of intra-labyrinthine pressure and the change of parameters of heat exchange in inner ear.

These results show that the functioning of the system of semicircular canals has to be considered as gravity-sensitive. Therefore the alteration of gravity level may influence not only the functioning the gravireceptors, but the state of the Reference system too and thus distort the signal B corresponding to misbalance of the receptor signal A and reference signal C. This fact once again indicates that alteration of gravity can not be considered as an usual variation of environmental parameters for which the mechanisms of neural adaptation are designed. Thus the adaptation to such stimulus may have non-traditional form.

Task Description: The overall goal of the proposed research is to identify the structural and functional changes that occur in the utricle of the vestibular labyrinth as the result of transitions from one gravitational level into another. This information is critical for advancing our understanding of neurovestibular adaptation and its sequelae. Our strategy is to use centrifugation as a tool for temporarily altering the gravitational stimulus. We are particularly interested in determining the afferent firing rate and response characteristics and the morphological changes in the hair cell-afferent complexes found in the utricle that are modified when the organism moves from normal gravity (1G) to hyper-gravity (hyper-G) and in the opposite sense.

In the utricular otolith organ it was found that the afferent response sensitivity to head acceleration (13, fish) as well as the number of synaptic bodies in hair cells (56, rats) increase as the result of exposure to microgravity (µG). A preliminary result showed that exposure to hyper-G by centrifugation decreases the number of synaptic bodies (56, rats). The period of afferent re-adaptation to normal gravity in fish (13) was found to be close to the period of behavioral adaptation in humans (55). Re-adaptation is defined here as the process of adaptation to normal gravity. These results suggest the possibility that the process of adaptation to µG might be mimicked by the process of re-adaptation from a hyper-G level. However, the experiments that would combine the results of µG and hyper-G experiments for the same species are absent. Further, the correlations of the synaptic re-arrangement with afferent sensitivity following exposure to altered gravity for the same species remain undefined. The purpose of the present proposal is to fill these gaps.

Our model is the adult toadfish, Opsanus tau. Fish provide an excellent model system for gravitational research (7). We have everything in place to successfully complete the proposed set of experiments and modeling. Of prime importance is the fact that in the same species a comprehensive evaluation of the neural response to µG (13) and hyper-G (present proposal) exposure will be made. There are four specific aims: Aim 1. Response characteristics of utricular afferents to Centrifugation. Fish will be placed in groups and exposed to different magnitudes (1.5 and 3 g) and duration (1-16 days) of centrifugation. On Center Controls and ground controls are included. Using electrophysiological techniques, individual utricular afferents will be physiologically characterized based on gravitoinertial acceleration testing. Afferent response properties to efferent vestibular activation will be specified. The duration of afferent recording session is tempered by the need to freeze the tissue in time to permit the measurements of structure-function correlations. To match the conditions under which post-flight experiments were performed (13) changes in afferent sensitivity caused by hyper-G will be studied from 1-24 hrs after centrifugation, and re-adaptation after 24-96 hrs in 1G . Aim 2. Morphology of hair cell-afferent synaptic complexes. Combining the techniques of labeling of individual afferents with light and transmission electron microscopy a complete characterization of the afferent is possible: its physiology, its dendritic morphology in the macula, and the extent and quantification of synapses formed from the innervated hair cells. Visualization of complex 3-D microcircuitry will also reveal precise connectivity within the macula that is essential for understanding neurovestibular adaptation. Synaptic body counts will be performed to address the fundamental question of the correlation of afferent response sensitivity with hair cell synapse number. Aim 3. Merging the structure and function of otolith afferents and their relationships to hyper-G. The specific times and thresholds of the afferent response adaptation and re-adaptation and the structural changes associated with the hair cell-afferent synapse will be determined by varying the magnitude and duration of centrifugation. Aim 4. Modeling. Based on known methodologies and the present experimental results, research will be directed towards the establishment of phenomenological models describing the adaptive changes in utricular afferents in altered gravity conditions. We will examine the relationship between the afferent response and the number of synapses in normal and in hyper-G conditions. A model of toadfish otolith mechanics in these conditions will be also developed. The four specific aims are directed to achieve the main goals: 1) to test the correlations of change in the gravitational level and the change in afferent response characteristics caused by head acceleration and the potential underlying changes in the morphology of the associated hair cell-afferent synaptic complexes, 2) to examine the role of efferent vestibular system on these correlations, 3) to test (propose) the possible mechanisms responsible for these correlations; and 4) to test if the transition from hyper-G to normal gravity can be used to mimic the effects of neural adaptation to µG. We expect that convergence of findings in µG and hyper-G will provide a model of neurovestibular plasticity, and will lead to the development of novel test protocols for both ground- and space-based research. As we learn about the basic cellular processes in vestibular neurons, countermeasures will eventually be developed to reduce the symptoms associated with Space Motion Sickness or even modify synaptic sensitivity in preparation of entry into a higher gravity level.

The overarching hypotheses to be evaluated are: One, the gravitoinertial sensitivity of otolith nerve afferents is altered as a consequence of the animal's exposure to hyper-G conditions as well as µG. It is reasoned that the transition from 1G to hyper-G will decrease otolith afferent sensitivity (output in impulses per sec/input in G), and the transition from hyper-G back to 1G will increase it (and back to normal levels). Thus, the transition from hyper-G to normal gravity may serve as an imitator of neurovestibular sequelae to µG. Two, hair cell-afferent synaptic complexes are modified by the altered gravity condition: the up- or down regulation of synaptic bodies is specific to the direction of transition from 1G. The change in number of synapses is directly correlated with afferent response sensitivity. Three, afferent adaptation mechanisms may involve either self-learning processes confined to the hair cell or responses of the hair cell-afferent system to feedback signal(s) that originate from a misbalance of information coming from otolith and extra-otolith contributor(s), or a combination of both. (These mechanisms will be considered by modeling based on the experimental findings.) Four, efferent vestibular actions are not uniform on hair cells and afferents, and it is proposed that the changes brought on in otolith afferents by the transitions from one gravity state to another will be most prevalent and coincident in afferents strongly affected by efferent activation. If this proves to be the case, then the next step is to directly query the efferent neurons themselves.

The overall goal of the proposed research is to identify the structural and functional changes that occur in the utricle of the vertebrate vestibular labyrinth as the result of transitions from one gravitational level into another. This information is critical for advancing our understanding of neurovestibular adaptation and its sequelae, and for the effective design and validation of countermeasures to benefit astronaut health and performance. Our strategy is to use centrifugation as a tool for temporarily altering the gravitational stimulus. We are particularly interested in determining the afferent firing rate and response characteristics and the morphological changes in the hair cell-afferent complexes found in the utricle that are modified when the organism moves from normal gravity (1G) to hyper-gravity (hyper-G) and in the opposite sense. This study is a natural analog to our previous studies aboard STS-90 and 95, and will provide a comprehensive evaluation of the neural response to µG and hyper-G in the same species.

We expect that convergence of findings in µG and hyper-G will provide a solid model of neurovestibular plasticity, and will lead to the development of novel test protocols for both ground- and space-based research. As we learn about the basic cellular processes in vestibular neurons, countermeasures will eventually be developed to reduce the symptoms associated with Space Motion Sickness, benefit sensorimotor coordination and performance, or even down regulate synaptic sensitivity in preparation of entry into a higher gravity level.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: The functional importance of head stabilization is seen by our ability to change gaze orientation and maintain binocular fixation of objects of interest during not only standing but also during locomotor activities, and our ability to detect self-motion and adaptively react to applied perturbations of the head and body. In the space environment the vestibular system is profoundly affected by the loss of he gravity vector. However, conditions on Earth can seriously affect the individual's ability to functional normally. In addition aging places a serious challenge to the vestibular and neural centers responsible for posture and movement control. Disorders of the vestibular system affect head, gaze and posture stabilization, and can dramatically alter the patients willingness to induce self-motion and can lead to trepidation of normal activities. These studies will also provide important insights into vestibular function, and may help in the diagnosis and treatment of vestibular patients seen in the clinic.

Summary of Research Progress and Accomplishments

Significant progress has been made on each of the specific aims originally presented and on the new aim to characterize the response of the horizontal semicircular canal afferents to the centrifugation stimulation paradigm. We are seven months into our project; we have completed the majority of the required control experiments; have two (2) abstracts (see next section) at a national and international meeting; and one paper nearly complete to submit for publication (see next section).

1) Control Utricular Afferent Characterization

Since the start of the project on October 1 2004, we have recorded the firing rate characteristics of 223 utricular afferents to controlled translational accelerations and dynamic and static tilts from pitch through roll, and their response to activation of the central efferent neurons using electrical pulse stimuli. In 82 afferents a complete characterization was successful; in the remaining 141 afferents the are still relevant and will be used to compile the statistical properties of the discharge characteristics. In these studies the discharge of single utricular afferents is obtained using glass microelectrodes, and acceleration stimuli are applied at systematically varying head (body) orientation to specify the afferent response characteristics. It is imperative to obtain the afferent’s maximum and minimum sensitivity to the applied stimuli for direct comparison of control responses to those obtained following centrifugation. We are currently analyzing and compiling the data obtained to date. We will conduct between 6-12 more control experiments to complete this dataset, and verify that an abbreviated test paradigm captures the required data. We will present our results to the Society for Neurosciences this fall (see abstract in next section). It is also imperative that we record as quickly as possible from the largest sample of post-centrifugation afferents. We have determined that incremental steps head angle orientation can be raised from 15° to 30°, thus reducing in half the recording time needed to obtain the afferent’s directional selectivity and the maximum and minimum sensitivity.

Figures 1-3 show the battery of acceleration tests delivered to characterize the directional sensitivity of the afferent to inertial and gravitational accelerations. As evident in the figures the control utricular afferent is highly directionally tuned and its maximum and minimum sensitivity can be accurately calculated. These properties of the utricular afferent will play strongly into determining the structural and functional changes that occur following centrifugation. Figure 1 gives the responses of a utricular afferent to controlled translational accelerations. The afferent was recorded on the right side on the animal, and using the right-hand rule (lab convention) acceleration directed out the animal’s snout are at 0°, out the contralateral labyrinth at 90°, out the tail at 180°, and out the ipsilateral labyrinth at 270°. Panel A provides the raw records. Upper trace is the animal’s head orientation (yaw axis) at -90° (=270°), and the accelerations are thus delivered along the interaural axis. The next traces are the linear acceleration (in cm/s2), cycle sync ticks, the linear velocity (in cm/s), and the firing rate (in imp/s) of the afferent. The cycles of stimulation are averaged and presented in phase histograms of panel B. The upper histogram is the averaged firing rate of the afferent with the least-squares fit of the 1st harmonic component of the response (dotted curve). The lower histogram is the averaged acceleration stimulus (in g) with the least-squares fit of the 1st harmonic component of the stimulus (dashed curve). The stimulus was 2 Hz acceleration at ± 0.02g. The averaged response gain was 4658 imp/s/g and the peak of the response lead the peak of the acceleration at that angle by 43.4°. The directional sensitivity of the afferent is shown in panels C and D. In panel C the response gain (in imp/s/g) and response phase with respect to peak stimulus acceleration (in °) are plotted as a function of head angle (in °). The data points were obtained by applied the acceleration stimulus and rotating the fish about the Earth’s vertical in 15° steps. A cosine function was applied (with equal weighting) to the gain and phase of the response to determine the degree of spatial tuning and the sensitivities: maximum sensitivity was 4803.5 imp/s/g at a head angle of 90° (and 270°) and the minimum was 0 imp/s/g at a head angle of 0° (and 180°). The Smin/Smax is equal to 0. A graphic representation of the data is given in panel D. The responses (in imp/s/g) with respect to the naso-occipital (NO) axis are plotted against the same responses with respect to the interaural (IA) axis. The green line with arrowheads shows the orientation of the best response - along the IA axis. (This is the most common response and agrees with the prevalent morphological polarization of the utricular hair cell bundle. Other directional sensitivities are also present.) The fact that the 2 red curve fits converge at zero indicated the tightness of the directional sensitivity (for example, the responses of an afferent to acceleration without directional selectivity would fall on a single, open function).

Figure 2 gives the responses of the same afferent to dynamic tilts and clearly shows the correspondence between the inertial and gravitational accelerations. The panels are similar in design to those presented in Fig.1. Following the right-hand rule and lab convention the fish’s snout is straight forward at 0° and 180°; in this orientation the tilt is a pure roll stimulus about the fish’s long axis. At 90° and 270° the tilts are pure pitch stimuli. Panel A provides the raw data: head orientation (yaw) at -30° or 330°, tilt velocity (in °/s), cycle sync ticks, tilt position (in °), and firing rate (in imp/s). The averaged response over 46 stimulus cycles is shown in panel B for the 2 Hz tilt at ±0.11° displacement: 445.4 imp/s/° and a phase lead of 49.3°. The directional selectivity of the afferent to dynamic tilt is given in panels C and D (same format as Fig. 1). Again, the afferent is highly tuned to this stimulus: maximum and minimum sensitivity are 793.9 imp/s/° at 19.7° (and 199.7°) and 45.6 imp/s/° at 109.7° (and 289.7°), respectively (Smin/Smax = 0.057), and the orientation is 19.7° (green line with arrowheads in panel D) off pure roll.

Figure 3 shows the responses of the same afferent in Figs. 1 and 2 to static displacements of the fish about the long axis (roll stimuli). For this afferent maximum responses were observed for translational accelerations directed out the contralateral labyrinth (Fig. 1), for dynamic roll tilts with ipsilateral side down (Fig. 2), and for static displacements moving toward ipsilateral displacement (Fig. 3). In Fig. 3 the upper trace is the displacement of the fish (ipsilateral or right side down is positive, contralateral or left side down is negative), and the bottom trace is the afferent firing rate over the 12-minute record. The insert shows the dynamic roll responses before the delivery of static displacement stimuli. The afferent gave an initial large excitatory response to ipsilateral displacement, followed by an adaptive decline in firing rate along two separate time courses - one fast with a dominant time constant of 4.3 sec and one slow with a dominant time constant of 44.6 sec. These data agree closely with recent results of hair cell receptor potential and afferent firing rate in the horizontal semicircular canal following constant copular displacement after canal lumen block (Rabbitt RD, Boyle R, Holstein GR, and Highstein SM (2005) Hair-cell versus afferent adaptation in the semicircular canals. J. Neurophysiol. 93: 424-36). The data on the control responses of utricular afferents will be presented at the Society for Neurosciences in November of this year, and the results will described in a separate publication. These data provide the benchmarks in response sensitivity and spatio-temporal properties required to accurately evaluate the utricular response to centrifugation.

2) Control Horizontal Semicircular Canal Afferent Characterization.

We have accepted a suggestion made by the study section review committee to include the study of the horizontal semicircular canal afferents. We found this to be a valuable and helpful suggestion. During centrifugation, the animal is subjected not only to a linear acceleration component due to the eccentricity of the animal with respect to the axis of rotation, but also to an angular acceleration stimulus. For this reason on-center control experiments are conducted to parse out the influence of the two forces. The question to be directly addressed is whether the semicircular canal undergoes structural and functional changes as a result of prolonged angular rotation. We have established the protocol that we will use to study the afferent responses, and have recorded to date the responses of 87 horizontal canal afferents. In addition we can now successfully record 25-35 canal afferents in two hours. As we previously described (Boyle R and Highstein SM (1990) Resting discharge and response dynamics of horizontal semicircular afferents of the toadfish, Opsanus tau. J. Neuroscience 10: 1557-1569), the canal afferents show a continuum of responses from velocity sensitivity to acceleration sensitivity. Our protocol uses frequencies from 0.2 Hz to 3 Hz, and will allow us to capture these responses. Results show a velocity-sensitive canal afferent and the lower panels show a more acceleration-sensitive canal afferent. Panels A1and B1 provide the raw data (acceleration, velocity and position of the stimulus, and the firing rate), A2 and B2 give the averaged responses in the form of a phase histogram and print the pertinent data for both the stimulus and the response, and A3 and B3 show the afferents’ excitatory responses to electrical activation of the efferent vestibular system. Although we have already defined these responses, we needed to collect these data on our new system to permit direct correspondence with the centrifuged fish. On-center control experiments will also be performed. In the centrifugation experiments we will first study the utricular afferents (our principal goal) and then the canal afferents.

3) Morphology of Utricular Afferents.

We are currently completing the morphological evaluation of the utricle at the scanning and transmission electron microscopic levels. This is a supplemental study that will benefit the analysis and interpretation of our current and projected results. This work will be written for publication in an anatomical journal.

We have intracellularly labeled utricular afferents in four separate experiments; a fifth labeling experiment is scheduled for May 25th. These data will serve as the control for the determining the regulation of the hair cell synaptic bodies and synaptic formation in fish subjected to centrifugation. In these experiments the afferent fiber is penetrated with a glass micropipette filled with a 3% solution of neurobiotin or biocytin. The responses of the afferent to short translational stimulations and electrical efferent stimulation are collected during intracellular penetration, and the label is iontophorezed into the fiber using positive current. Only one afferent is labeled in the fish to ensure positive identification. The utricle is then removed after several hours, fixed, and then immunohistochemically treated to visualize the label.

We are currently evaluating the labeled utricular afferents migrating through the supporting cell zone of the macula and into the hair cell zone at its base with thin sections (180 nm) sections at the transmission electron microscopic level. To develop a 3D reconstruction of the afferent a mosaic is first constructed at a reduced magnification to serve as a guide. The tissue is viewed at higher magnifications to identify the synaptic bodies in contact with the afferent processes. The mosaic technique is also used to obtain the density of synaptic bodies in the utricle, and this analysis is underway in control utricles. This effort is very laborious, and we are systematically assembling the TEM images for each afferent. The reconstructions will be completed in both the control and experimental fish.

Of the 12 hair cells in the utricular afferent, the synaptic bodies in all but one hair cell were exclusively associated with the labeled afferent. The dendritic process of the utricular afferent follows a line or beam along a set of hair cells. The hair cells share a similar morphological polarization of their hair bundles.

Abstract accepted for presentation:

“Response properties of utricular nerve afferents to gravitational and translational accelerations in the toadfish.”

Y. Popova, A. Twombly, J. Varelas, R. Ehsanian, A. Kondrachuk and R. Boyle.

Ames Research Center, NASA, Moffett Field, CA 94035 USA.

The utricle is an otolith organ in the vertebrate inner ear that senses gravity and inertial acceleration of the head. The detection of these forces relies on the otolith mass being weighted in Earth’s 1g. Under weightless conditions experienced in space flight the sensation is altered, and structural and functional changes are known to occur in the utricle. These adaptive changes pose a serious challenge to adequate sensorimotor function during transitions from one gravity level to another. The aim of the present study was to characterize the firing rate response of the utricular afferents to controlled gravitoinertial accelerations in the anesthetized oyster toadfish, Opsanus tau. The data will provide the benchmark for interpretation of subsequent results in altered gravity experiments, such as centrifugation, modeling the adaptive changes and efficacy of countermeasures. Translational accelerations along the earth’s horizon were applied as the animal was rotated about the earth’s vertical at 15° intervals over 360°. Rotations about the earth’s horizon were delivered in a similar fashion to apply pitch to roll stimuli; static reorientations in gravitational field were also applied. Single fiber recording techniques were used to acquire the firing rate responses of the utricular afferents. Responses to both translational and gravitational accelerations are highly directionally selective. This agrees closely with the afferent’s tight dendritic field in the sensory epithelium. Firing rate recovery following a ramp displacement of the animal at an orientation that elicits a maximum or minimum sensitivity had a dominant time constant in the range of 30-50 sec. Afferents were also studied during combined electric shock activation of the efferent vestibular pathway. The firing rate was facilitated (most common), unresponsive or disfacilitated; when responsive to efferent stimulation, the afferent sensitivity to an applied acceleration was reduced. “The role of possible feedback mechanisms in the effects of altered gravity on formation and function of gravireceptors of mollusks and fish.”

Alexander V. Kondrachuk 1 and Richard D. Boyle 2 1 Dept. Theoretical Physics, Institute of Physics, Natl. Acad. Sci., Kiev, Ukraine, kondr@kondr.kiev.ua 2 BioVIS Technology Center and Vestibular Research Facility, Ames Research Center, NASA, Moffett Field, CA 94035-1000, USA. richard.boyle@nasa.gov

Altered gravity is known to influence the state of gravireceptors. Although these effects are more pronounced during the earlier stages of organism development, structural changes also take place in mature gravireceptors. Despite the number of experiments, the results have not led to a clear interpretation. Many interpretations were guided by popular hypothesis that says that regulation of the growth of otoliths or mass of statoconia, i.e., the test mass upon which gravity and linear acceleration act, is based on the weight of the mass. Thus, a larger-than-normal mass should be produced in a reduced-g environment and a smaller-than-normal mass would be produced in hyper-g. This hypothesis is based on the idea of feedback (or feedbacks) and seems to be intuitively clear; however, its grounds and subsequences were not sequentially analyzed.

In the past years there was significant progress in discovering the details of the early stages of development and function of gravireceptors in mollusks and fish, particularly in the conditions of hyper- and microgravity. From an evolutionary point of view we may expect that the main processes of the earlier stages of formation of gravireceptors in mollusks and fish are consistent with the development of gravireceptors in higher animals. An important consideration is that the mechanisms of self-regulations might differ between the developing and the mature organisms, and thus their respective sensitivities to transitions of gravity are different. If this is indeed the case, then different structural and functional changes in their gravireceptors will result by alteration of gravity. The goals of the present work are: 1) to review and compare the existing experimental data concerning the effects of altered gravity on the structure and function of gravireceptors in mollusks and fish; 2) to analyze possible mechanisms, structures and hierarchy of feedbacks that regulate formation and function of gravireceptors in mollusks and fish in normal and altered gravity conditions; 3) to discuss the reasons for difference between the feedbacks in the developing and mature gravireceptors and the possible role of the efferent system in their self-regulation; 4) to propose qualitative model(s) of the effects of altered gravity on gravireceptors of mollusks and fish in the context of the feedback problem; and 5) to formulate possible experiments that may be useful to test the model proposed. “On the mechanisms of stimulation of the hair cell bundles in otolithic organs”

Alexander V. Kondrachuk 1 and Richard D. Boyle 2 1 Dept. Theoretical Physics, Institute of Physics, Natl. Acad. Sci., Kiev, Ukraine 2 BioVIS Technology Center and Vestibular Research Facility, Ames Research Center, NASA, Moffett Field, CA 94035-1000, USA.

Abstract

The interaction of the hair cell bundle (HCB) with the otolithic membrane (OM) caused by linear acceleration or gravity initiates the transduction cascade and the generation of the HCB receptor potential that can trigger a neural afferent discharge. Therefore, the response of the OM components that surround the HCBs to the applied acceleration is important for the transfer of mechanical input to the HCB level. Experimental investigations of the function of hair cell bundles are based on artificial mechanical stimulation of the HCB by probes and fluid-jets. The analysis of hair cells function must consider the movements of the HCB and its surroundings as a single system. Meanwhile, a detailed picture of the nature of the interactions between the HCBs and the surrounding gel and nearby structures remains unclear. The present work considers the question: whether the conditions of artificial stimulation justify the conditions of natural stimulation that follow from the morphological data on the structure of otolithic membrane and results of modeling The HCBs can be divided into two general groups based on their type of contact with environment: 1) those with one or several point contacts between the highest cilia and the otoconial layer, as may occur in the frog saccule, and 2) those with spatially continuous contacts with the environment. Freestanding HCBs are the example of the second group. The purpose of the present work is to estimate the parameters of the artificial stimulations and to analyze their correspondence to natural stimulation of the HCB in otoliths. This analysis is based on results of the modeling of transformations of mechanical input in the following sequence: acceleration of the otolithic membrane, displacement of the OM gel layer, deflection of hair cell bundle, deformation of the ciliary tip-links, and formation of a temporal pattern of polarization in the framework of simplified models of the HCB+OM interaction corresponding to point and spatially continuous contacts of the HCBs with the OM. The comparison of experimental and modeling data indicates that probe stimulation cannot imitate the effects of spatially continuous contacts, but may imitate the natural stimulation of the HCBs with point contacts. Stimulation of the HCB by fluid jet mimics the fluid-like gel interaction with the HCB, but application of the fluid jet is restricted by the low viscosity of the solution. The parameters of fluid jet stimulation indicate that inertial force, rather than viscous force, is responsible for the HCB deflection in these experiments. This could be verified by direct measurements of the parameters of fluid-jet stimulation. The possible experiments that may improve the adequacy of artificial stimulation of the HCB to natural one are discussed. Support Contributed By: NASA 03-OBPR-04

Future Research Plan.

We are following closely the original research plan. Additional control experiments are planned, but these will constitute a minor percent effort. The priority is to conduct the centrifugation experiments, and answer the prime question: can we accurately and precisely quantify the influence of exposure to hyper-g on the utricular end organ. Based on interpretation of data from human, rat and toadfish, we expect to find reduced response sensitivity to gravitoinertial accelerations in the utricular afferents and a reduction in the number of synaptic bodies in the hair cells. The experimental plan was designed to extract the influence of both the time course and g-level on the utricular response. First experimental runs will start in July. The 8-foot centrifuge has 4 boxes at a fixed radius from the midline. Consequently, more experimental runs will be needed; however, our project should have unlimited access to the centrifuge. The first run will begin in July 2005. We are scheduling a 3-g run of 25-day duration to obtain data in the following groupings: 4-5 day (4 fish), 8-9 days (3 fish) and 16-17 days (3 fish).
