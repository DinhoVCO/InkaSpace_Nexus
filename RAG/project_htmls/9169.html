Flight Assignment/Project Notes: ISS

NOTE: Extended to 6/11/2015 per PI and NSSC information (Ed., 6/20/14)

Task Description: This research has capitalized on a rapid turn-around spaceflight opportunity and the Biological Research in Canisters-Petri Dish Fixation Units (BRIC-PDFU) hardware to address how spaceflight affects gene expression related to low oxygen/hypoxic response in the model plant Arabidopsis thaliana. Root zone hypoxia is thought develop in spaceflight as weightlessness leads to a reduction in the buoyancy-driven convection that usually aids in gas exchange around organisms. This in turn leads to the development of oxygen-limiting conditions with adverse effects on plant vigor and yields.

Our analysis of Ca2+ signaling responses and associated gene expression patterns in Arabidopsis indicates Ca2+ signaling is linked to hypoxic response in plants. We have identified a suite of Ca2+-dependent proteins that show rapid transcriptional responses to anoxia in both roots and shoots. Mutants in two such genes, aca1-2, and cax2-2 (both Ca2+ transporters) are resistant to hypoxic challenge. These mutants also show disrupted gravitropic response suggesting that Ca2+ signaling may provide a point of cross-talk between the anoxic and gravity response systems.

We therefore compared growth and gene expression patterns in cax2-2, and wild-type plants grown on the ground and ion the International Space Station. Seedlings were grown on orbit and fixed in RNAlater for subsequent analysis. Differences in the of gene expression were analyzed post-flight by a combination of RNAseq and qPCR analyses. Comparison to ground-based controls maintained under normal and varying degrees of low oxygen conditions at 1xG, have been used to ask how much of the responses seen on orbit can be explained by development of low oxygen conditions around the plants due to microgravity conditions.

Results from this analysis are not only helping advance our understanding of the development of anoxic response in space and on the Earth but also test whether the Ca2+ signaling system provides targets for genetically engineering potential countermeasures to this stress.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: This research is addressing how low oxygen sensing operates in plants, targeting the Ca2+-dependent signaling machinery of the plant root. The results from this work are not only providing insight into how low oxygen levels impact plant growth in space but also are contributing to our understanding of similar responses on Earth. The low oxygen sensing/response systems triggered by anoxic conditions in space are also those employed by plants challenged by low oxygen on Earth, such as during flooding of the soil or intense soil microbe respiration that locally depletes oxygen around the plant. Thus, this work aimed at analysis of how Ca2+ signaling is involved in the low oxygen response induced during spaceflight is also revealing a role for this system in this general flooding/low oxygen response system. These results are providing novel molecular targets for potential manipulation to tailor plant growth to varied soil oxygen status, such as promoting flooding tolerance in crop plants.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2015 
Task Progress: FLIGHT:

This project has completed the flight, sample recovery, and sample analysis phases.

The experimental setup for the BRIC-17 experiment was using surface sterilized seeds of Arabidopsis plated on nutrient agar medium in 60 mm Petri dishes (4 plates of wild-type and 3 each of two anoxia-related CAX2 Ca2+ transporter mutants). The sterile plates were then loaded into Petri Dish Fixation Units (PDFU) and 5 PDFUs plus a HOBO data logger secured inside each of two of the Biological Research in Canister (BRIC) hardware. Germination was delayed by holding the BRIC at 4°C until the hardware was installed on the International Space Station (ISS). Germination was then started by warming the equipment to room temperature. After 8 days at room temperature, the crew injected RNAlater fixative into the PDFUs and the following day, the canisters were placed in the MELFI freezer onboard the ISS at -95°C. Samples were returned using the Dragon capsule in a -32°C Cold Bag, de-integrated, and transported frozen to the University of Wisconsin-Madison for further analysis. All flight procedures were completed successfully.

POSTFLIGHT ANALYSIS:

Seedling growth was assessed from digital images of the thawed seedlings. A small but significant (P<0.05, t-test) redirection from shoot to root growth was observed in the spaceflight wild type plants versus the ground-based controls. This reallocation of growth was missing in the cax2-3 mutant suggesting this line may be resistant to some of the stresses of spaceflight that are responsible for remodeling growth in wild type.

TRANSCRIPTIONAL PROFILING:

All samples were dissected into root and shoot and all yielded sufficient quantity 12-36 µg/plate and quality of RNA for both quantitative PCR and whole genome transcriptional analyses to monitor gene expression.

Isolated RNA was sent to the University of Wisconsin-Madison Biotech Center who performed ribosomal RNA reduction, library construction, and RNAseq-based transcriptome profiling for whole genome gene expression analysis. The complete raw data-set from this RNAseq analysis was mapped to the TAIR version 10 of the Arabidopsis genome using the Tuxedo protocols. These transcriptional patterns were then analyzed for over-represented classes of genes.

Analysis of genes induced >5-fold indicated 38 genes were up-regulated in spaceflight in wild-type and in the cax2 mutant plants relative to ground controls, implying a core set of spaceflight-related genes. These genes cover a range of functions from hormone response genes to those related to oxidative stress. In addition, the induction of 210 other genes was suppressed in the cax2 mutants relative to wild type, suggesting these are targets of a CAX2-dependent machinery that is triggered by spaceflight, and so strong candidates for genes that are part of the spaceflight anoxic response that is triggered by the CAX2 protein. In addition, 329 genes were up-regulated in both cax2 mutants but not in wild type plants. As both of these CAX2 mutants are resistant to low oxygen stress on Earth, these up-regulated genes represent strong candidates for potential hypoxia resistance genes. Many of these genes are also elevated in the CAX2 mutant backgrounds in the ground controls but are further increased by the spaceflight environment. Analysis of this subset of genes using the GOrilla gene ontology analysis tool revealed significant enrichment in genes related to hypoxia, oxidative stress, and biotic stress responses.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2014 
Task Progress: This project has completed the development, flight, and sample recovery phases and sample analysis is currently underway.

Flight

The experimental setup for this BRIC-17 experiment was using surface sterilized seeds of Arabidopsis plated on nutrient agar medium in 60 mm Petri dishes. The sterile plates were then loaded into Petri Dish Fixation Units (PDFU) and 5 PDFUs plus a HOHO data logger secured inside the Biological Research in Canister (BRIC) hardware. Payload verification tests showed >90% germination of these seeds in the BRIC-PDFU hardware. Germination was reliably delayed by holding the plates at 4°C until the required time for seedling growth, indicating this as a suitable approach to place un-germinated seeds onto the International Space Station. Germination was then reliably started by warming the equipment to room temperature. This configuration was successfully launched using the SpaceX2 vehicle on March 1st, 2013. The hardware consisted of two BRIC canisters, each containing five PDFUs and one HOBO temperature data logger. Plates of seeds (4 plates of wild-type and 3 each of two anoxia-related CAX2 Ca2+ transporter mutants were integrated into the PDFUs on 2/27/2013). These were then placed in a double cold stowage bag maintained at 4°C and stowed into the SpaceX-2 Dragon capsule at Launch -24 hrs. This hardware was then successfully carried to the ISS where it was de-stowed from the Dragon capsule and transferred to the US Lab. After 8 days at room temperature, the crew injected RNAlater fixative into the PDFUs and the following day, the canisters were placed in the MELFI freezer onboard the ISS at -95°C, stored for 11 days and then returned using the Dragon capsule in a -32°C Cold Bag on March 24, 2013. These samples were de-integrated from the hardware on April 4, 2013 and transported to the University of Wisconsin-Madison for further analysis

Postflight analysis:

Initial analysis revealed some fungal contamination on some plates (identified as penicilium) but sufficient intact samples were recovered to allow for the gene expression analyses required from this experiment. Germination efficiency was >90% on all plates. Seedling growth was assessed from digital images of the thawed seedlings and a redirection from shoot to root growth was observed in the spaceflight materials versus the ground-based controls. This reallocation of growth was missing in a cax2 mutant suggesting the mutant may be resistant to some of the stresses of spaceflight that are remodeling growth. All samples yielded sufficient quantity and quality of RNA for both quantitative PCR and whole genome transcriptional analyses to monitor gene expression.

Isolated RNA was provided to the University of Wisconsin-Madison Biotech Center who performed library construction and RNAseq-based transcriptome profiling for gene expression analysis. Raw data from this RNAseq analysis was available towards the beginning of 2014 and is currently under study. Preliminary analysis indicates 38 genes are up-regulated in spaceflight in wild-type and cax2 mutant plants, implying a core set of spaceflight-related genes. These cover a range of functions from hormone response genes to those related to oxidative stress. In addition, the induction of 210 other genes was suppressed in the cax2 mutants relative to wild type, suggesting these are targets of the CAX2-dependent machinery, and so strong candidates for genes that are part of the spaceflight anoxic response that is triggered by the CAX2 protein. Current work is further analyzing these patterns of gene transcription and comparing them to patterns seen in response to experimentally-imposed low oxygen levels on Earth.

Ground-based analysis

In parallel to the flight experiment, wild-type, and the Ca2+ transporter mutants used in the spaceflight experiment have been monitored for their abilities to trigger cellular Ca2+ changes in response to low oxygen stress. Both cax2 mutants showed aberrant Ca2+ signals in the root tip to low oxygen, with increased magnitude of Ca2+ elevations. As a positive control for a stress that is known to induce large Ca2+ changes, salt stress was also used. Local application of salt stress to the root was observed to trigger a previously unknown wave of Ca2+ response that traversed the root system, traveling at 400 µm/sec. This Ca2+ wave was dependent on the vacuolar Ca2+ channel Two Pore Channel 1 (TPC1) and was required to trigger stress responses in the plant, as measured by patterns of stress-related gene transcription. Effects of mutations in cax2 on this Ca2+ wave signaling system are under way.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2013 
Task Progress: This project has completed the development, flight, and sample recovery phases and sample analysis is currently underway.

Preflight testing:

The initial experimental setup to be used for this research was to develop a system that would operate within the 6 cm petri dish chambers used in the BRIC-PDFU flight hardware but that would permit use of dry seeds adhering to a filter wicking system. This would allow the transport of dry, highly resilient seeds to orbit and then injection of germination medium by the crew onboard the ISS to start the experiment. However, analysis of this system in the flight hardware revealed poor germination, likely due to an interaction between the wicking system setup and the closed sample environment of the Petri Dish Fixation Unit (PDFU) required for flight. An alternative experimental setup was therefore developed of using surface sterilized seeds plated on nutrient agar medium. Verification tests showed >90% germination of these seeds in the BRIC-PDFU hardware. Germination was reliably delayed by holding the plates at 4°C until the required time for seedling growth, which was started by warming the equipment to room temperature.

Flight:

This configuration was successfully launched using the SpaceX2 vehicle on March 1, 2013. The hardware consisted of two BRIC canisters, each containing five PDFUs and one HOBO temperature data logger. Plates of seeds (4 plates of wild-type and 3 each of two anoxia-related CAX2 Ca2+ transporter mutants) were integrated into the PDFUs on February 27, 2013. These were then placed in a double cold stowage bag maintained at 4°C and stowed into the SpaceX-2 Dragon capsule at Launch -24 hrs. This hardware was then successfully carried to the ISS where it was destowed from the Dragon capsule and transferred to the US Lab. After 8 days at room temperature, the crew injected RNAlater fixative into the PDFUs and the following day, the canisters were placed in the MELFI freezer onboard the ISS, stored at -95°C for 11 days, and then returned on March 24, 2013 in a -32°C Cold Bag using the Dragon capsule . These samples were deintegrated from the hardware on April 4, 2013 and transported frozen to the University of Wisconsin-Madison for further analysis

Postflight analysis:

Initial analysis revealed sufficient intact samples were recovered to allow for the gene expression analyses required from this experiment. Germination efficiency was >90% on all plates. Initial processing indicated the samples of seedlings would yield RNA in sufficient quantity and of high enough quality for both the quantitative PCR and whole genome gene expression analyses required for the project. Full characterization, extraction and analysis of these samples is now underway.

Ground-based analysis:

In parallel to the flight experiment, wild-type and the Ca2+ transporter mutants used in the spaceflight experiment have been monitored for their responses to gravitropic stimulation on Earth. Plants expressing the GFP-based 'Yellow Cameleon' Ca2+ sensor were generated and Ca2+ changes in response to gravitropic stimulation (90° rotation) monitored by confocal ratio imaging. Both mutants showed aberrant Ca2+ signals in the root tip to gravistimulation, with reduced onset of Ca2+ changes. These results suggest that the CAX2 Ca2+ transporter is involved in both the anoxia and gravity sensing systems of the plant root.
