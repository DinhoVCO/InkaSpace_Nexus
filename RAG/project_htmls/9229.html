Task Description: Plant perception and response to gravity is critical for appropriate growth, development, and environmental responses. Shoot gravitropism involves the perception of gravity by sedimentation of starch-containing amyloplasts in gravity-sensing cells, followed by transmission of the signal to additional cell types to promote directional growth. Vesicle trafficking to the vacuole is essential for the perception of the gravity signal, but the molecular basis for this requirement is unknown. Our long term goal is to determine the mechanism by which gravity is perceived by shoots and the gravity signal transduced to other cell types to produce a response. The objective of this application is to determine the function of vacuolar trafficking proteins in shoot gravitropism. We hypothesize that appropriate physical properties of the vacuole are essential for amyloplast sedimentation, and therefore for gravity perception. We will test this hypothesis by measuring the elasticity of wild type and mutant vacuoles using atomic force microscopy and by comparing the lipid constituents of the vacuolar membranes. We will also identify additional proteins involved in vacuolar trafficking by a combination of in vitro membrane fusion assays and in vivo immunoprecipitations and test their function in gravitropism using genetic approaches. This research will provide a molecular explanation for the observation that shoot gravitropism is specifically defective in vacuolar trafficking mutants and allow us to make testable predictions about the mechanistic basis for their agravitropic phenotype. It will also provide a foundation for the generation of transgenic plants with altered gravity response thresholds.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: The primary impact of our research will be to increase understanding of the mechanisms by which plants respond to gravity. In addition to being important for the growth of plants in the altered gravitational conditions likely to be encountered during space flight, this research will also contribute to our knowledge of plant growth, development, and response to environmental cues. The gravitational response interacts extensively with other developmental, hormonal, and environmental response pathways, leading to morphological and anatomical changes to the plant structure. Understanding of the molecular mechanism of gravitropism is therefore vital for the development of strategies to improve plant growth and yield under varying conditions. The development of technologies to analyze the physical properties of vacuoles and in particular the fusion between membranes is also likely to impact research in plant cell structure, stress responses, and growth and development under different conditions.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2013 
Task Progress: Personnel

Rahul Roy (PhD student) continues to work full time on this project. A PhD student, Xiaochen Yang, is now also working on the project. Three undergraduate students have assisted in routine aspects of the research.

Research progress

Our focus in the past year has been continued characterization of two vacuolar trafficking mutants that we demonstrated are required for correct growth responses to gravity. Our goal is to understand the mechanistic basis for the gravitropic phenotype and its connection to the vacuolar trafficking defects. 1. TNO1. We showed previously that the TNO1 protein is required for efficient transport of proteins to the vacuole and initiated a characterization of the gravitropic defect of a tno1 mutant. Dark grown hypocotyls of tno1 knockout mutants bend slower than wildtype and TNO1 complemented lines when gravistimulated. In the past year we have made several significant discoveries related to this phenomenon:

(a) Analysis of gravitropic bending kinetics in dark grown hypocotyls indicates that the knockouts show a significant lag in bending kinetics compared to WT and complemented lines.

(b) TNO1 is also involved in root gravitropism. This was unexpected, as root and shoot gravitropism occur by largely distinct mechanisms and there is little overlap in the components required for each.

(c) tno1 mutant roots also show a growth pattern that is skewed from the vertical, indicating a defect in plant growth or root morphology.

(d) We hypothesized that these root and shoot gravitropic and growth defects could be due to either disruption of auxin fluxes or disorganization of microtubules. In support of this, the tno1 knockout mutants are hypersensitive to microtubule drugs, both in terms of root skewing, hypocotyl elongation, and cell morphology. Mutant roots were also significantly more sensitive to a synthetic auxin, 2,4D.

(e) We are now in the process of visualizing auxin gradients and microtubule structure using fluorescent markers.

(f) Electron microscopy of Golgi stacks in tno1 mutants has allowed quantification of defects in Golgi and trans-Golgi network structure between wild-type and mutant plants, providing insight into the cellular function of TNO1.

2. VPS45. VPS45 is a vacuolar trafficking protein that we have previously characterized. However, a knockout mutant in VPS45 is lethal, making the study of its role in gravitropism difficult. We recently identified a point mutation in the VPS45 gene via TILLING that causes a single amino acid change in the protein sequence. The mutant has a weak phenotype and has defects in both shoot and root gravitropism as well as general growth and morphological defects. We have complemented this mutant phenotype using a genomic fragment containing VPS45 under its own promoter, indicating that the mutant phenotypes seen are indeed due to the change in VPS45. Further analysis of the gravitropism defects is underway.

3. SYP51/52. A homozygous mutant in which expression of the t-SNARE SYP51 is reduced was identified. Initial gravitropism assays showed no significant shoot gravitropism defects, possibly due to functional redundancy or the residual SYP51 expression. Due to these difficulties, we are taking a different approach of using RNA interference to known down expression of SYP51 and SYP52 simultaneously. Constructs have been generated and introduced into Arabidopsis plants and transformants are in the process of being screened for reduced expression.

Vacuolar properties. We hypothesize that changes in the physical properties of vacuoles contributes to shoot gravitropism defects in vacuolar trafficking mutants, either due to differences in protein content or in lipid composition. To test this hypothesis, we isolated vacuoles from wild-type Arabidopsis plants and from a vacuolar trafficking mutant (vti11) that has shoot gravitropic defects. Lipids were extracted from the vacuoles and sent to the Kansas State lipidomics facility for analysis. The data obtained were inconsistent, most likely because of the low concentrations of lipid in the samples. These experiments are in the process of being repeated with larger lipid quantities.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2012 
Task Progress: Personnel

Rahul Roy (PhD student) is continuing to work full time on this project. A PhD rotation student, Xiaochen Yang, has also contributed to the project this year, along with two undergraduate students.

Research progress

Objective #1. Analysis of vacuolar properties in the agravitropic vti11 vacuolar trafficking mutant.

We are developing a novel microfluidics platform for the analysis of vacuolar physical and structural properties. Microfluidics channels have been designed and synthesized. Initial experiments will involve changes in osmotic potential of the buffer system to determine the lysis point of vacuoles of different genotypes. Preliminary experiments using protoplasts have demonstrated the feasibility of the approach and are allowing us to optimize conditions for buffer exchange.

Objective #2. Identification of additional vacuolar trafficking components that are required for shoot gravitropism, and characterization of their molecular function.

We previously reported the identification and phenotypic characterization of a new vacuolar trafficking mutant, tno1. We have now analyzed in detail the phenotype of this mutant with respect to gravitropism. A statistically significant defect in gravitropic responses of etiolated hypocotyls of the mutant has been demonstrated using two different assays. Roots also appear affected in gravitropic responses, although this has not yet been quantified. By contrast, inflorescence stems do not show gravitropic defects. From these results we conclude that TNO1 is involved in the gravitropic response of hypocotyls and probably also roots but not of inflorescence stems and we are now attempting to determine the basis for the gravitropic phenotype.

A new weak allele has been identified for the vacuolar trafficking component VPS45, for which a knockout mutation is lethal. This mutant has defects in both shoot and root gravitropism as well as general growth and morphological defects.

A homozygous mutant in which expression of the t-SNARE SYP51 is reduced has been identified. Initial gravitropism assays show no significant shoot gravitropism defects, possibly due to functional redundancy or the residual SYP51 expression. Further analysis of each of these mutants is ongoing.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2011 
Task Progress: Personnel

A PhD rotation student, Rahul Roy, has been recruited to work on this project full time and the research will form the basis of his PhD thesis. Another graduate student, Sang-Jin Kim, participated in the project and has now graduated with his PhD. Sang-Jin established assays to measure vesicle fusion and identified and characterized putative vacuolar trafficking proteins.

Research progress

Identification of mutants in potential shoot gravitropism genes.

Our working hypothesis is that vacuolar trafficking proteins VTI11 and SYP22, both of which are required for gravitropism, are part of a SNARE complex that delivers membrane and cargo to the vacuole. To identify new vacuolar trafficking proteins that may function in gravity responses, co-immunoprecipitation was performed to isolate proteins that interact with the known vacuolar trafficking protein SYP41. A previously uncharacterized protein, named TNO1, was identified as a SYP41-interacting factor. Vacuolar proteins were partially secreted to the apoplast in a tno1 mutant, suggesting that TNO1 is required for efficient protein trafficking to the vacuole. The function of TNO1 in gravitropism in both hypocotyls and inflorescence stems is now underway.

Based in previous immunoprecipitation results, we predict that the additional trafficking proteins SYP51 and/or SYP52 may be present in the same complex and therefore may also be required for shoot gravitropism. To test this hypothesis, Arabidopsis mutants with T-DNA insertions in the SYP51 and SYP52 genes have been identified by genomic PCR using primers specific to the SYP51 and SYP52 genes and to the TDNA borders. RT-PCR analysis indicates that homozygous syp52 mutants have lost expression of the SYP52 gene and gravitropism assays are now being performed to determine the effect on gravity responses. No homozygous mutants for SYP51 have been identified, suggesting that this is an essential gene.

Analysis of membrane fusion activity of the potential vesicle fusion proteins

To biochemically analyze the function of putative vesicle fusion proteins involved in vacuolar trafficking, in vitro liposome fusion assays are being performed using recombinant proteins to determine whether these proteins are able to catalyze membrane fusion as predicted. Constructs have been generated in E. coli expression vectors for each protein, fused to a 6xHis-tag to allow protein purification. Several of these proteins have been successfully expressed in large quantities in E. coli and affinity purified to remove unwanted contaminants. These purified proteins are being reconstituted into artificial proteoliposomes and assayed for their ability to fuse vesicles. Several of the proteins that are thought to be involved in vacuolar trafficking based on their mutant phenotypes have been analyzed in this way and shown to be sufficient to catalyze the fusion of liposomes. Additional factors are now being analyzed in the same way.

Research progress

There are two major objectives of this research:

Objective #1. Analysis of vacuolar properties in the agravitropic vti11 vacuolar trafficking mutant.

Objective #2. Identification of additional vacuolar trafficking components that are required for shoot gravitropism, and characterization of their molecular function.

Progress toward Objective #1.

Substantial progress has been made towards this aim, primarily in developing methods for measuring the elasticity of vacuolar membranes. Protocols have been optimized for the isolation of vacuoles from wild-type and gravitropism-defective vti11 mutants, and intact vacuoles from both genotypes that are suitable for elasticity measurements can now consistently be purified. Vacuoles from wild-type plants have been used in atomic force microscopy to measure the elasticity/flexibility of the vacuolar membrane. This has required extensive optimization and troubleshooting, as to our knowledge measurements of this type have not previously been performed using plant cells. We have now reached the point where consistent and reproducible results can be obtained using wild-type vacuoles, and we are beginning experiments to compare these results with vti11 mutant vacuoles to identify changes in the physical properties of the vacuolar membrane that may correlate with loss of gravitropism.

Progress toward Objective #2.

(a) Identification of mutants in potential shoot gravitropism genes.

Our working hypothesis is that the vacuolar trafficking proteins VTI11 and SYP22, both of which are required for gravitropism, are part of a SNARE complex that delivers membrane and cargo to the vacuole. Based in previous immunoprecipitation results, we predict that the additional trafficking proteins SYP51 and/or SYP52 may be present in the same complex and therefore may also be required for shoot gravitropism. To test this hypothesis, Arabidopsis mutants with T-DNA insertions in the SYP51 and SYP52 genes have been identified by genomic PCR using primers specific to the SYP51 and SYP52 genes and to the TDNA borders. Genotyping of a segregating population of the mutants (two different alleles for each gene) has identified homozygous individuals for each SYP52 allele, which are in the process of being analyzed by RT-PCR for loss of gene expression. No homozygous mutants for SYP51 have yet been identified, raising the possibility that this is an essential gene, although further analysis is needed to confirm this.

(b) Analysis of membrane fusion activity of the potential vesicle fusion proteins

To biochemically analyze the function of the putative vesicle fusion proteins VTI11, SYP22, SYP51 and SYP52, in vitro liposome fusion assays will be performed using recombinant proteins to determine whether these proteins are able to catalyze membrane fusion as predicted. Constructs have been generated in E. coli expression vectors for each protein, fused to a 6xHis-tag to allow protein purification. Several of these proteins have been successfully expressed in large quantities in E. coli and affinity purified to remove unwanted contaminants. These purified proteins will be reconstituted into artificial proteoliposomes and assayed for their ability to fuse vesicles.

Task Description: Plant perception and response to gravity is critical for appropriate growth, development and environmental responses. Shoot gravitropism involves the perception of gravity by sedimentation of starch-containing amyloplasts in gravity-sensing cells, followed by transmission of the signal to additional cell types to promote directional growth. Vesicle trafficking to the vacuole is essential for the perception of the gravity signal, but the molecular basis for this requirement is unknown. Our long term goal is to determine the mechanism by which gravity is perceived by shoots and the gravity signal transduced to other cell types to produce a response. The objective of this application is to determine the function of vacuolar trafficking proteins in shoot gravitropism. We hypothesize that appropriate physical properties of the vacuole are essential for amyloplast sedimentation, and therefore for gravity perception. We will test this hypothesis by measuring the elasticity of wild type and mutant vacuoles using atomic force microscopy and by comparing the lipid and protein constituents of the vacuolar membranes. We will also identify additional proteins involved in vacuolar trafficking by a combination of in vitro membrane fusion assays and in vivo immunoprecipitations and test their function in gravitropism using genetic approaches. This research will provide a molecular explanation for the observation that shoot gravitropism is specifically defective in vacuolar trafficking mutants and allow us to make testable predictions about the mechanistic basis for their agravitropic phenotype. It will also provide a foundation for the generation of transgenic plants with altered gravity response thresholds.
