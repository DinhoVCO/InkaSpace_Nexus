Flight Assignment/Project Notes: STS-131 , STS-133 , STS-135 , Bion M1

NOTE: Extended to 7/31/2014 per A. Chu/ARC (Ed., 4/30/2014)

Task Description: Tail suspension or hindlimb unloading of rodents has been used as a ground-based model of microgravity because tail suspension, like actual microgravity, 1) induces a headward shift of fluids and 2) diminishes the contractile activity of hindlimb skeletal muscles that are normally involved in maintaining posture. It is now evident that the shift of fluid toward the head and the unloading of postural muscles alter the mechanical forces exerted on arteries, the vessels responsible for regulating tissue blood flow and arterial blood pressure. For example, the smooth muscle cells in the small arteries of hindlimb skeletal muscle atrophies with tail suspension, and results in a diminished ability of these vessels to vasoconstrict. The compromised ability to constrict reduces the ability of the rats to maintain normal blood pressure, and consequently, these animals are susceptible to abnormal reductions in arterial pressure when the body position of the animal is moved from a horizontal position to a 70o head-up position. Conversely, cerebral arteries in the head hypertrophy, which can limit brain blood flow and result in loss of consciousness during head-up tilt. The purpose of this proposal is to determine whether the fluid shifts and reduced activity of postural muscles that are induced by microgravity similarly alter rodent arterial vessel structure and some key cellular signaling pathways. In other words, does space flight result in smooth muscle atrophy of peripheral arteries and hypertrophy of cerebral arteries? This work will provide potentially important information about the mechanisms underlying the reduced fitness and the inability to properly regulate blood pressure of astronauts returning to Earth.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: These experiments will contribute toward attaining a better understanding of how fundamental biological systems, such as the cardiovascular system, respond to the microgravity environment. The detailed study of the resulting vascular adaptations triggered by microgravity will yield essential information on the basic physiological responses of individual blood vessels involved in blood flow and pressure regulation. Further, analysis of cerebral arteries will provide potentially important information about the visual impairment of astronauts induced by microgravity This information will also support the development of treatments or countermeasures to improve crew health and performance following their return to a gravitational environment.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2014 
Task Progress: In the cerebral (basilar) arteries from the Bion-M1flight, vasoconstriction elicited through a voltage- gated Ca2+ mechanism (KCl) and thromboxane A2 receptors (U46619) were lower in crebral artieries from spaceflight mice. Inhibition of Rho-kinase activity (Y27632) abolished group differences in U46619-evoked contractions. Endothelium-dependent vasodilation elicited by acetylcholine was lower in cerebral arteries from flight mice. The pressure-diameter rlation was lower in arteries from flight mice relative to that in habitat control mice. This decrease in vascular distensibility did not appear to be related to differences in the extracellular matrix protein elastin or collagen content or the lastin/collagen ratio in the basilar arteries. Diameter, medial wall thickness, and medial cross-sectional area of unpressurized basilar arteries were not different among groups.

A publication on the Bion results has been submitted:

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2012 
Task Progress: It is hypothesized that the structure and function of arteries is altered by microgravity. Specifically, the wall thickness and vasoconstrictor function of mesenteric and skeletal muscle resistance arteries will be diminished, and the wall thickness and vasoconstrictor responsiveness of cerebral arteries will be enhanced.

On STS-131 flight, physiological responses in mesenteric and gastrocnemius muscle resistance arteries were obtained in 11 control and 11 flight animals. On STS-133 flight, physiological responses in mesenteric resistance arteries were obtained in 10 control and 2 flight animals at R+1, 2 flight animals at R+5, and 2 flight animals at R+7. On STS-135 flight, physiological responses in cerebral resistance arteries were obtained in 12 control and 7 flight animals.

The mesenteric, gastrocnemius muscle, and cerebral arteries were isolated and placed in cold buffer solution at KSC. The arteries were then transported to the University of Florida where they were mounted on glass pipettes. Wall thickness and lumen diameter were measured using video microscopy. The resistance arteries were further studies to determine physiological responses to vasoconstrictor agonists working through different mechanisms.

Results in the mesenteric and gastrocnemius muscle resistance arteries demonstrate that microgravity attenuates vasoconstrictor responses to norepinephrine, a receptor-mediated vasoconstrictor mechanism, as well as to KCl, a non-receptor vasoconstrictor mechanism. Furthermore, vasoconstrictor responses to caffeine, an intracellular calcium-release mechanism through ryanodine receptors, are also attenuated in these resistance arteries after spaceflight. Analysis of mRNA expression of ryanodine receptors in mesenteric and gastrocnemius arteries demonstrated depressed expression in the flight animals relative to controls mice. Vascular structure (maximal diameter and wall thickness) were not altered by spaceflight in the mesenteric and gastrocnemius resistance arteries.

In the cerebral (basilar) arteries, myogenic vasoconstrictor tone was lower in flight animals relative to that in ground control mice. Passive pressure-diameter responses were also greater in basilar arteries from flight mice, indicating greater distension of these cerebral arteries. Structural analysis demonstrated that wall thickness was unaffected by spaceflight, whereas maximal intraluminal diameter was greater in flight animals.

Three manuscripts are currently in preparation.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2010 
Task Progress: Vascular, heart and skeletal muscle tissues were obtained from 13 control mice and 11 flight mice (STS-131). The mesenteric, gastrocnemius muscle and cerebral arteries were isolated and placed in cold buffer solution at KSC. The arteries were then transported to the University of Florida where they were mounted on glass pipettes. Vascular structure (wall thickness and lumen diameter) was measured using video microscopy. The mesenteric and gastrocnemius muscle resistance arteries were further studies to determine physiological responses to vasoconstrictor agonists working through different mechanisms. The structural data has yet to be analyzed, and the functional mesenteric data analysis is currently ongoing. However, analysis of the functional gastrocnemius artery data is complete. Results in the gastrocnemius muscle resistance arteries demonstrate that microgravity attenuates vasoconstrictor responses to KCl, a non-receptor vasoconstrictor mechanism, as well as to norepinephrine, a receptor-mediated vasoconstrictor mechanism. Preliminary results also indicate that in skeletal muscle, modest increases in autophagy-related proteins, Beclin-1 and calpain-1 occurred, while a significant 41% elevation occurred in the Bax:Bcl-2 ratio, an apoptotic index, in spaceflight animals. The heart muscle showed similar but more modest changes in the apoptotic index.

Task Description: Tail suspension or hindlimb unloading of rodents has been used as a ground-based model of microgravity because tail suspension, like actual microgravity, 1) induces a headward shift of fluids and 2) diminishes the contractile activity of hindlimb skeletal muscles that are normally involved in maintaining posture. It is now evident that the shift of fluid toward the head and the unloading of postural muscles alter the mechanical forces exerted on arteries, the vessels responsible for regulating tissue blood flow and arterial blood pressure. For example, the smooth muscle cells in the small arteries of hindlimb skeletal muscle atrophies with tail suspension, and results in a diminished ability of these vessels to vasoconstrict. The compromised ability to constrict reduces the ability of the rats to maintain normal blood pressure, and consequently, these animals are susceptible to abnormal reductions in arterial pressure when the body position of the animal is moved from a horizontal position to a 70o head-up position. Conversely, cerebral arteries in the head hypertrophy, which can limit brain blood flow and result in loss of consciousness during head-up tilt. The purpose of this proposal is to determine whether the fluid shifts and reduced activity of postural muscles that are induced by microgravity similarly alter rodent arterial vessel structure and some key cellular signaling pathways. In other words, does space flight result in smooth muscle atrophy of peripheral arteries and hypertrophy of cerebral arteries? This work will provide potentially important information about the mechanisms underlying the reduced fitness and the inability to properly regulate blood pressure of astronauts returning to Earth.
