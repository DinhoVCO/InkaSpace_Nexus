Flight Assignment/Project Notes: NOTE: Extended to 2/28/2014 per A. Chu/ARC (Ed., 3/4/2013)

Task Description: The space environment of microgravity and radiation imposes stress conditions that affect the cellular and molecular responses of organisms and dictate their survival. A very fast response to stress is the production of reactive oxygen species (ROS), followed by intracellular oxidation. Space flight is associated with increased oxidative damage after return to 1 g, suggesting that microgravity and/or the transition between gravity fields triggers a response.

We propose to use a model organism used previously for space flight, the fruit fly Drosophila melanogaster. Studies in both hyper- and microgravity have already established many of the physiological alterations, and work from our own laboratory suggests that oxidative stress is implicated in the molecular response under both conditions. We will use hypergravity as a test environment for gravity alterations, and to dissect out molecular and cellular responses as well as long-term physiological outcomes.

The goal is to provide a complete picture of the oxidative stress pathway in hypergravity, and to determine and select the key molecular functions and physiological read-outs to be tested in microgravity.

The aims are to quantify gravity-dependent cellular ROS production and to determine the key points of entry into the oxidative pathway by using mutants that show a physiologically altered response to hypergravity. This approach will give a picture of the stress pathway, starting from fast cellular changes triggered by sudden changes in the gravity vector, to tissue-specific gene and enzyme activity alterations.

Understanding the stress pathway in hypergravity opens the venue into looking at the opposite transition in the gravity load, from 1 g to microgravity during space flight, and will help predict the cellular and physiological response to other stresses imposed by the space environment.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: Some of the imaging tools developed to measure the behavioral changes for this project will be useful for a variety of behavioral studies in different organisms on Earth. The general issue of oxidative stress is also of great relevance to aging and stress on Earth, and the studies here therefore provide yet another paradigm in which to study and understand these molecular responses.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2014 
Task Progress: This work has proved our starting hypothesis that hypergravity is linked closely with alterations in oxidative stress mechanisms and that these can also influence behavioral responses to gravity. By using a genetically tractable organism like the fruit fly to identify the specific molecular pathways involved in the response to altered gravity conditions, we have been able to identify specific genes and specific anatomical locations within the fly brain that are important for these responses to gravity. By using the hypergravity environment to identify important genetic determinants of physiological changes in the fly, we hope to extend this work in the future to the microgravity environment of space flight and test different ways in which to make the organism more resistant to the changes induced by the space flight environment.

Two manuscripts are in preparation for submission to peer-reviewed journals with expected publication later in 2015:

Ravikumar Hosamani, Oana Marcu, and Sharmila Bhattacharya "Hypergravity-induced altered behaviour in Drosophila" Manuscript in preparation.

Ravikumar Hosamani and Sharmila Bhattacharya "Acute Hypergravity Causes Altered Gene Expression, and Affects the Oxidative Stress Pathway in Drosophila" Manuscript in preparation.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2013 
Task Progress: The specific objectives achieved during this period were:

1. Quantified the behavioral changes during acute hypergravity exposure by deleting specific gene functions in the brain by RNA interference methodology (either knock-down function in the whole brain or targeted to specific regions of the brain), and also using stable ubiquitous loss-of-function mutants in similar gene pathways to study the same hypergravity induced changes.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2011 
Task Progress: 1. Neuronal drivers and approach.

The approach was to identify genes involved in the behavioral response to altered gravity (3g) by comparing fruit fly lines mutant for select genes with control lines. The RNA interference technique was used to obtain tissue-specific mutation in genes of interest. The initial experiments used the panneuronal driver elav-Gal4 to express doublestranded RNA in the entire brain. elav is present in both larvae and adults, and it gave a good starting point for an overall brain expression. Five other neuronal drivers were obtained and are being amplified and crossed.

2. Genotype screening for behavior alterations following hypergravity.

Genes in five pathways (oxidative stress, longevity, behavior, autophagy and DNA repair, some overlapping) were identified as putative candidates for the response to altered gravity. Flies were obtained from the Vienna Drosophila RNAi Center (VDRC) and lines were amplified and crossed with the neuronal drivers.

Task Description: The space environment of microgravity and radiation imposes stress conditions that affect the cellular and molecular responses of organisms and dictate their survival. A very fast response to stress is the production of reactive oxygen species (ROS), followed by intracellular oxidation. Space flight is associated with increased oxidative damage after return to 1g, suggesting that microgravity and/or the transition between gravity fields triggers a response.

We propose to use a model organism used previously for space flight, the fruit fly Drosophila melanogaster. Studies in both hyper- and microgravity have already established many of the physiological alterations, and work from our own laboratory suggests that oxidative stress is implicated in the molecular response under both conditions. We will use hypergravity as a test environment for gravity alterations, and to dissect out both short-term molecular and cellular responses as well as long-term physiological outcomes.

The goal is to provide a complete picture of the oxidative stress pathway in hypergravity, and to determine and select the key molecular functions and physiological read-outs to be tested in microgravity.

The aims are 1. to quantify the short-term gravity-dependent cellular ROS production and alterations in oxidative enzyme gene expression; 2. to determine the key points of entry into the oxidative pathway by using mutants that show a physiological long-term altered response to hypergravity; 3. to determine the long-term effects of hypergravity over multiple generations. This approach will give a complete picture of the stress pathway, starting from fast cellular changes triggered by sudden changes in the gravity vector, to tissue-specific gene and enzyme activity alterations, all the way to physiological responses during animal lifetime and effects on population dynamics.

Understanding the stress pathway in hypergravity opens the venue into looking at the opposite transition in the gravity load, from 1 g to microgravity during space flight, and will help predict the cellular and physiological response to other stresses imposed by the space environment.
