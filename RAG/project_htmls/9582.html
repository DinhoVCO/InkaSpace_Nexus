Task Description: Plants respond to gravity in numerous ways; the best understood is the change in growth orientation in response to reorientation of plants relative to the gravity vector. A growth asymmetry develops, which allows the plant organ to return to the original angle of growth relative to gravity. Understanding gravitropism is essential to facilitate growth of plants in the microgravity environment of space. Gravitropic bending is mediated, at least in part, by redistribution of the plant hormone auxin. This proposal examines the mechanisms by which the cellular asymmetry of auxin transport proteins is established and the molecular mechanisms that allow translocation of the auxin signals from the site of perception to the site of reaction.

We will employ three approaches to transiently alter the expression and localization of auxin transport proteins to examine the effect on gravitropic curvature in roots of the model species Arabidopsis thaliana. First, we will use a temperature sensitive mutant, scd1-1, with a defect in endomembrane targeting to transiently disrupt auxin transport protein localization and gravitropic curvature.

Second, we will transiently perturb the activity and/or localization of auxin transport proteins through a pharmacological approach and measure the resulting effect on gravitropic kinetics. In our third approach, we will use well characterized null mutants with defects in auxin transport proteins and will express wild type copies of defective genes in an inducible expression system. This approach of reintroducing a gene function at a controlled time point during the gravitropic process, should allow us to understand when these proteins function in controlling auxin transport and gravitropism. The planned experiments will combine high resolution laser scanning confocal microscopy and high temporal resolution gravitropic analyses with transient perturbation of auxin transport to understand the dynamics of auxin transport protein localization and resulting auxin asymmetries that drive differential gravitropic growth.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits:

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2014 
Task Progress: We have now completed an analysis of the gravitropic and auxin transport phenotypes of the temperature sensitive scd1-1 mutant, which has a defect in a gene encoding a protein predicted to function in Rab GTPase-dependent protein trafficking. All aspects of root growth and gravitropism are normal in this mutant at the permissive temperature of 18°C, while at the nonpermissive temperature of 25°C, scd1-1 exhibits significant reductions in gravitropic curvature, growth rate, and auxin transport. Basipetal or shootward IAA transport is reduced in the scd1-1 mutant relative to wild-type at 25°C, while similar transport amounts are detected at 18°C. These differences were observed using both traditional radioactive IAA transport assay and by applying auxin at the root tip and measurement of the shootward progression of fluorescent signal resulting from auxin induced gene expression, using the DR5:GFP reporter. Just 8 hours of incubation at RT (25°C) after growth at 18°C is sufficient to show a significant reduction in IAA transport, nicely mirroring the timing of the defect in gravitropism.

The final line of experiments in scd1-1 focuses on examination of the effects of temperature changes on the distribution of auxin transport proteins in this mutant background. We have used laser scanning confocal microscopy to examine the tissue level and sub-cellular distribution of auxin efflux carriers PIN1, PIN2, PIN3, AUX1, and ABCB19 that are translational fusions to GFP, with PIN2:GFP being best linked to gravity response. The pattern and intensity of fluorescence was similar in wild-type and scd1-1 at 18 degrees C. At 25°C, the PIN1 and PIN2:GFP fusion in scd1-1 were found at substantially lower levels in the plasma membrane, and accumulated in endomembranes, while changes in the other reporters were subtle. The membrane asymmetries of PIN1:GFP and PIN2:GFP reporters were generally maintained, although the membrane associated fluorescence signal was substantially weaker. The apparent structure and character of endomembranes that accumulated PIN1-GFP and PIN2-GFP were quite different. This result suggests that in the mutant, these two auxin transport proteins may be trafficked by different pathways in the different cell types in which they are found, causing them to be shunted to different structures where they accumulate. The final step of these experiments has been the identification of the nature of the endomembranes that accumulate PIN2:GFP. We have crossed endomembrane specific fluorescent markers for early and late endosomes, Golgi, endoplasmic reticulum, and vacuole into scd1-1xPIN1 GFP and scd1-1xPIN2GFP. Our studies indicate that PIN2:GFP accumulates in the late endosomes in scd1-1, consistent with a defect in a RAB-GEF that acts at this final stage of targeting. We are currently completing the manuscript describing these results.

A second line of experimentation utilized a pharmacological approach to transiently disrupt activity and/or targeting of auxin transport proteins to understand their role in gravitropic responses. We have examined compounds which inhibit protein phosphorylation and dephosphorylation, specifically staurosporine and cantharidin, respectively, and combined these inhibitors with mutants in specific kinase and phosphatase genes, to provide evidence of reciprocal regulation of auxin transport by protein phosphorylation and regulation of gravitropic responses (Sukumar et al., 2009). An undergraduate student completed a study of three endosidin compounds, ES10, ES11, and ES12, on gravity response and auxin transport. These endosidin chemicals were isolated in a chemical genomic screen by Glenn Hicks and Natasha Raikhel at UC Riverside for inhibition of hypocotyl gravitropism. These experiments quantified the kinetics of inhibition of root gravitropism by these three compounds using high resolution imaging and identified doses of compounds that inhibited gravitropism, but not root elongation. ES11 and ES12, but not ES10, perturb the formation of asymmetric gradients of auxin induced gene expression across a gravity stimulated root. The effect of these compounds on auxin transport was quantified and only ES12 reduced shootward or basipetal IAA polar transport, which supplies auxin for root gravitropic curvature. Therefore these three compounds fall into three distinct activity pathways, suggesting they act on different protein targets. The effect of a fourth endosidin, ES2, on gravitropism and auxin transport was examined initially by Jonathan Watkins, and is now being examined in ES2 resistant mutants isolated from Natasha Raikhel’s lab, as the final step in publication of an ES2 focused manuscript.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2012 
Task Progress: During this year of funding (June 1, 2011-May 31, 2012) we completed our analysis of the gravitropic and auxin transport phenotypes of the temperature sensitive scd1-1 mutant, which has a defect in a gene encoding a protein predicted to function in Rab GTPase-dependent protein trafficking. After a 90° reorientation relative to the gravity vector, the wild type plants grown at 25°C show a gravitropic response is characterized by a 90° root angle within 8 hours, while scd1-1 has delayed gravitropic response reaching only a 45° root angle after 8 hours. To determine the rate of response to a change in temperature in the mutant, gravitropic curvature after transition of seedlings from the permissive to nonpermissive temperature was analyzed. The reductions in root gravitropic curvature in scd1-1 are evident in seedlings that were exposed to the nonpermissive temperature (25°C) for 8 or 24 hours.

In contrast, PIN3-GFP looked qualitatively the same in both wild type and scd1-1 at the subcellular level, but was restricted to fewer cells at 25°C compared to wild type. During this year, an undergraduate student focused on examining the kinetics of this response, doing live imaging of PIN1:GFP labeled endomembrane organelles and Flourescence Recovery After Photobleaching (FRAP), which quantifies the rate of return of fluorescently labeled protein to a location on the membrane that is bleached by a laser. These experiments demonstrated that PIN1:GFP fluorescence returns to the plasma membrane at a slower rate in the scd1-1 mutant than in wild-type, consistent with a targeting defect in the scd1-1 mutant. Together, these results are a complete story linking SCD1 protein to control of targeting of auxin transport proteins to the appropriate location in the cell to control auxin transport and gravitropism.

If an endomembrane specific marker line shows fluorescence that colocalizes with the observed PIN: GFP endomembranes, this will provide insight into the role of SCD1 protein in targeting auxin transport proteins, which are then needed for gravitropic responses. We have received a no-cost extension to complete these imaging experiments in a final year of funding.

Finally, the effect of these compounds on gravity response in mutants that have altered gravitropic responses are still underway, but initial experiments suggest that agravitropic mutants show differential responses to these drugs. These mutant experiments are also to be completed during our no cost extension as is the publication describing these results.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits:

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2011 
Task Progress: We have largely completed our examination of the scd1-1 mutant, which has a defect in a gene encoding a protein predicted to function in RAB GTPase-dependent protein trafficking, specifically a RAB-GEF (guanine exchange factor, which is needed to activate RAB protein activity). We found that IAA transport, the distribution of IAA efflux proteins, and gravity response were altered in the scd1-1 mutant at at the nonpermissive temperature of 25°C (Isley, 2011). In contrast, at the permissive temperature of 18ºC, these processes were at wild-type levels. The gravitropic response of scd1-1 seedlings was quantified by high resolution imaging, taking images every 2 minutes for 8 hours to measure the full gravitropic response. After a 90° reorientation relative to the gravity vector, the wild type plants grown at 25°C showed a complete gravitropic reorientation with a 90° root angle within 8 hours, while scd1-1 has a delayed gravitropic response reaching only a 45° root angle after 8 hours. We monitored gravitropic curvature and growth rate in scd1-1 as seedlings were switched from growth at 18°C to 25°C. The reductions in root gravitropic curvature in scd1-1 were evident in seedlings that were exposed to the nonpermissive temperature (25°C) for as little as 8 hours.

Second, we examined the transport of auxin in the scd1-1 mutant, to determine if the mechanism by which gravity response is impaired is tied to the ability to transport IAA. Auxin moves in two directions in the root in two distinct tissues. Basipetal or shootward IAA transport, regulates differential gravitropic growth, and acropetal or rootward transport, provides auxin for lateral root development. We examined both polarities of IAA transport and find that basipetal and acropetal transport are reduced in the scd1-1 mutant relative to wild-type at 25°C, while similar amounts of IAA transport were detected at 18°C. Just 6 hours of incubation at RT (25°C) after growth at 18°C was sufficient to show a difference in IAA transport. We found that the time required to alter basipetal auxin transport was similar to the time required to altered gravity response suggesting a physiological relationship between these two parameters.

The third line of experiments in scd1-1 focused on examination of the effects of temperature changes on the distribution of auxin transport proteins. We used LSCM to examine the tissue level and sub-cellular distribution of auxin efflux carriers ABCB4, PIN1, PIN2, and PIN3 that are translational fusions to GFP and the influx carrier AUX1 fused to YFP. When scd1-1 was grown at 25º C, the expression of the PIN1, 2, and 3 reporters was restricted to fewer cells and was reduced in intensity, and PIN2 and 3 accumulated in endomembranes. The subcellular distribution of these three reporters was similar in wild-type and scd1-1 at 18ºC with the expected asymmetrical pattern at the plasma membrane in wild type and scd1-1. AUX1 and ABCB4 fusions were unaffected in the mutant. Current experiments focus on real time imaging to watch the movement of PIN-GFP fusions from the membrane into endomembrane organelles in real time to understand the dynamics of this process.

The last set of experiments that is underway with scd1-1 is to identify the subcellular compartments that accumulate PIN proteins at the nonpermissive temperature. We have completed a colocalization analysis with FM464 and found that there is no colocalization of PIN2-GFP with FM464 stained endosomes (Isley, 2011). Therefore we are confident that SCD1 functions in targeting PIN2 to organelles other than the endosome. We have crossed endomembrane specific fluorescent markers for Golgi, endoplasmic reticulum, and vacuole into scd1-1x PIN1 GFP and scd1-1xPIN2GFP. Imaging is just beginning for these combination lines. If an endomembrane specific marker line shows fluorescence that colocalizes with the observed PIN: GFP endomembranes, this will provide insight into the role of SCD1 protein in targeting auxin transport proteins, which are then needed for gravitropic responses.

A second line of experimentation utilized a pharmacological approach to transiently disrupt activity and/or targeting of auxin transport proteins to understand their role in gravitropic responses. We examined the effect of four chemicals that disrupted gravity responses in a chemical genomic screen by Glenn Hicks and Natasha Raikhel at UC Riverside. We measured the effects of four of these compounds on gravitropism, root elongation, auxin transport, and asymmetric expression of DR5-GFP (an auxin responsive promoter driving GFP expression). These compounds reduced gravitropic curvature, but with a distinct set actions. Some alter auxin transport, some alter auxin signaling, and some affect other aspects of gravitropic signaling (Hughes, 2011).

The final avenue of experimentation is to construct inducible constructs expressing auxin transport proteins in mutants that do not synthesize these proteins. Tanya Falbel is leading this process with assistance from two undergraduates, Lorena Rios Acosta (University of Puerto Rico - Mayaguez) and Catherine Ballenger (University of Wisconsin - Madison). We will then monitor gravitropic curvature under different induction regimes, as well as monitor the levels of the auxin transport proteins at the times at which they are effective.

Task Description: Plants respond to gravity in numerous ways; the best understood is the change in growth orientation in response to reorientation of plants relative to the gravity vector. A growth asymmetry develops, which allows the plant organ to return to the original angle of growth relative to gravity. Understanding gravitropism is essential to facilitate growth of plants in the microgravity environment of space. Gravitropic bending is mediated, at least in part, by redistribution of the plant hormone auxin. This proposal examines the mechanisms by which the cellular asymmetry of auxin transport proteins is established and the molecular mechanisms that allow translocation of the auxin signals from the site of perception to the site of reaction.

We will employ three approaches to transiently alter the expression and localization of auxin transport proteins to examine the effect on gravitropic curvature in roots of the model species Arabidopsis thaliana. First, we will use a temperature sensitive mutant, scd1-1, with a defect in endomembrane targeting, to transiently disrupt, auxin transport protein localization and gravitropic curvature. Second, we will transiently perturb the activity and/or localization of auxin transport proteins through a pharmacological approach and measure the resulting effect on gravitropic kinetics. In our third approach, we will use well characterized null mutants with defects in auxin transport proteins and will express wild type copies of defective genes in an inducible expression system. This approach of reintroducing a gene function at a controlled time points during the gravitropic process, should allow us to understand when these proteins function in controlling auxin transport and gravitropism. The planned experiments will combine high resolution laser scanning confocal microscopy and high temporal resolution gravitropic analyses with transient perturbation of auxin transport to understand the dynamics of auxin transport protein localization and resulting auxin asymmetries that drive differential gravitropic growth.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2010 
Task Progress: Our initial focus has been on examination of the scd1-1 mutant and we have asked if IAA transport, the distribution of IAA efflux proteins, and gravity response are altered in the scd1-1 mutant, which has a defect in a gene encoding a protein predicted to function in Rab GTPase-dependent protein trafficking. We monitored gravitropic curvature, growth rate, and auxin transport in scd1-1 as seedlings were grown at the permissive temperature (18°C) or the non-permissive temperature (25°C). At the permissive temperature gravitropism, growth, and auxin transport are at equivalent levels in wild-type and scd1-1. The gravitropic response of scd1-1 seedlings was quantified by high resolution imaging, taking images every 2 minutes for 8 hours to measure the full gravitropic response. At the nonpermissive temperature, this process is profoundly reduced. Second, we examined the transport of auxin in the scd1-1 mutant, to determine if the mechanism by which gravity response is impaired is tied to the ability to transport IAA. Auxin moves in two directions in the root in two distinct tissues. Basipetal or shootward IAA transport, regulates differential gravitropic growth, and acropetal or rootward transport, provides auxin for lateral root development. We examined both polarities of IAA transport and find that both basipetal and acropetal transport are reduced in the scd1-1 mutant relative to wild-type at 25°C, while similar transport amounts are detected at 18°C. We also defined the time required after transition from permissive to nonpermissive temperature to lead to the altered auxin transport phenotype. Just 6 hours of incubation at RT (25°C) after growth at 18°C is sufficient to show a difference in acropetal IAA transport between scd1-1 and wild-type. We are currently measuring IAA transport in seedlings transitioned between temperatures and correlating the time required to alter gravitropic response to the time required to altered gravity response and lateral root formation, to show a physiological relationship between these two parameters.

The third line of experiments in scd1-1 focuses on examination of the effects of temperature changes on the distribution of auxin transport proteins. We have used laser scanning confocal microscopy to examine the tissue level and sub-cellular distribution of auxin efflux carriers PIN1, PIN2, and PIN3 that are translational fusions to GFP. When scd1-1 was grown at the nonpermissive temperature, the expression of these reporters was restricted to fewer cells and was reduced in intensity. The subcellular distribution of these three reporters was similar in wild-type and scd1-1 at 18°C with the expected asymmetrical pattern at the plasma membrane in wild type and scd1-1, consistent with these proteins defining the polarity of auxin transport. At 25°C, the PIN1 and PIN2:GFP fusion in scd1-1 were found at substantially lower levels in the plasma membrane, and accumulated in endomembranes. Yet, the membrane asymmetries of these proteins were generally maintained, although substantially weaker. The apparent structure and character of endomembranes that accumulated PIN1-GFP and PIN2-GFP were quite different. This result suggests that in the mutant, these two auxin transport proteins may be trafficked by different pathways in the different cell types in which they are found, causing them to be shunted to different structures where they accumulate. In contrast, PIN3-GFP looked qualitatively the same in both wild type and scd1-1 at the subcellular level, but was restricted to fewer cells at 25°C compared to wild type. Ongoing experiments focus on identification of the identity of the endomembranes that accumulate these PIN-GFP fusions by crossing mutant lines with fluorescent protein fusions that have distinct organelle locations.

A second line of experimentation is utilizing a pharmacological approach to transiently disrupt activity and/or targeting of auxin transport proteins to understand their role in gravitropic responses. We have examined compounds which inhibit protein phosphorylation and dephosphorylation, specifically staurosporine and cantharidin, respectively, and combined these inhibitors with mutants in specific kinase and phosphatase genes, to provide evidence of reciprocal regulation of auxin transport by protein phosphorylation and regulation of gravitropic responses (Sukumar et al., 2009). We are also examining the effect of a range of chemicals that disrupt auxin transport protein targeting and gravity responses isolated in a chemical genomic screen by Glenn Hicks and Natasha Raikhel at UC Riverside. We are focusing on the effect of a subset of these chemicals on auxin transport and the kinetics of root gravitropism. These experiments will first contribute insight into the function of these compounds to identify their targets of action. Second, we will use these compounds to disrupt the gravitropic response by altering auxin transport and then to use these to perturb gravitropic responses to provide insight into the dynamics of this process.

Training

This grant is currently supporting one graduate student and half of the salary of a post-doctoral researcher, and two undergraduates. The graduate student working on the project, Jonathan Isley, is performing the phenotypic characterization of gravitropic response and auxin transport in the scd1-1 mutant. The post-doctoral researcher, Dr. Tanya Falbel, is performing the crosses needed to examine reporters and look for mechanistic roles of auxin transport downstream of scd1-1. These two researchers are working collaboratively to image the reporter lines using laser scanning confocal microscopy with the goal of understanding the effect of scd1-1 on the localization of auxin transport protein:GFP fusions and endomembrane marker: fluorescent protein fusions. The pharmacological experiments are being performed in collaboration between Jonathan Isley and a graduate student, Michelle Brown, in the laboratory of Natasha Raikhel at the University of California at Riverside, and with a WFU undergraduate, Ryan Hughes. The gravitropic effects of flavonoids, which are endogenous regulators of auxin transport, is being performed by another WFU undergraduate, Stephanie Zhang.
