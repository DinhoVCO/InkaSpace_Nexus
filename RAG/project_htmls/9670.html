Task Description: Changes that occur to both the host immune system and pathogenesis of microbes during spaceflight represent a formidable challenge to the successful transition from short-to-long-duration spaceflight. This is a critical concern, since a) the immune system of the crew is dysfunctional during spaceflight, b) spaceflight uniquely increases the virulence and globally alters gene expression of the human pathogen Salmonella typhimurium when the bacteria are recovered post-flight and subsequently used to infect ground-based animal models, c) spaceflight-induced increases in S. typhimurium virulence are regulated by media ion composition, d) phosphate ion is sufficient to alter related pathogenesis responses in a ground-based spaceflight analogue model, and e) the evolutionarily conserved bacterial RNA chaperone protein, Hfq, serves as a master molecular regulator of many of these responses. While spaceflight has been shown to induce changes that can independently affect the host or the pathogen in a manner that is directly relevant to the development of infectious disease during spaceflight, none of these studies have been done when both the host and pathogen are simultaneously exposed to the spaceflight environment. To address this knowledge gap, we propose to challenge the human surrogate model Caenorhabditis elegans with S. typhimurium during spaceflight to study the role of the intestine in host-pathogen interactions in real-time, define virulence mechanisms, identify evolutionarily conserved responses, and test novel therapeutic strategies to prevent infectious disease. We hypothesize that the combination of a spaceflight-induced increase in virulence of the pathogen coupled with a blunted immune response of the host will result in a synergistic effect on the host-pathogen interaction such that the risk of infectious disease during spaceflight is exacerbated. We further propose that these responses represent evolutionarily conserved mechanisms at the level of both host and pathogen that are dependent on media ion composition, specifically the local concentration of phosphate.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2016 
Task Progress: Our spaceflight experiment (operationally called Micro-5/Phoenix â€“ Pathogen Host Enteric Infection Experiment) successfully launched on SpaceX-5 to the ISS (International Space Station) January 10, 2015 and was masterfully performed on orbit by Astronaut Terry Virts. Our experiment was returned on SpaceX Dragon which landed on February 11, 2015 and our payload was returned to us shortly thereafter. This experiment is the first to profile the entire infection process of any living organism in real time in microgravity and to measure virulence (disease causing potential) and gene expression under these conditions.

Following a successful spaceflight experiment, we have made significant progress in processing and analyzing the multi-dimensional data from this experiment, including:

A) Completed analysis of nematode survival (time-to-death 50/TD50) for several thousand real-time videos taken in spaceflight throughout the infection time course of the Micro-5 experiment for two media types.

B) Completed comparisons of TD50 in A (above) to synchronous ground controls for the same media types.

C) Completed isolation of high quality and high abundance RNA from the spaceflight and synchronous ground control samples (mentioned above in A) for gene expression analysis.

D) We are in the process of completing the same analyses (as in A-C above) for the other two media types from flight and ground samples and have been granted an extension from NASA to do so.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2015 
Task Progress: Previously published results from our lab showed that spaceflight uniquely increases the virulence and globally alters gene expression of the major human pathogen Salmonella typhimurium when the bacteria are recovered post-flight and subsequently used to infect laboratory-based animal models. In our efforts to determine the mechanism(s) behind the spaceflight-induced responses in Salmonella, we discovered that a) spaceflight-induced increases in Salmonella virulence are regulated by media ion composition, b) phosphate ion is sufficient to alter related pathogenesis responses in a spaceflight analogue model, c) the Hfq protein serves as a master molecular regulator of many of these responses in Salmonella, and d) Hfq also acts as a spaceflight-induced regulator acting across bacterial species, which to our knowledge, is the first report of such a master response regulator of the spaceflight-induced stimulon in bacteria. Hfq is a highly conserved bacterial RNA chaperone protein that binds to small non-coding regulatory RNAs thereby facilitating their association with mRNAs, the result of which plays a diverse role in prokaryotic gene expression (including regulation of ion response pathways), virulence, and physiology in response to stress. While spaceflight has been shown to induce changes that can independently affect the host or the pathogen in a manner that is directly relevant to the development of infectious disease during spaceflight, no studies have been published that meaningfully document the outcome when both the host and pathogen are simultaneously exposed to the spaceflight environment. Furthermore, the eukaryotic organism and human surrogate infection model, C. elegans, contains structural and functional homologues of Hfq proteins, which are a family of evolutionarily conserved RNA-binding proteins that are critical for immune function, cell differentiation/function, and responses to cellular stress. We hypothesize that the combination of a spaceflight-induced increase in virulence of the pathogen coupled with a blunted immune response of the host will result in a synergistic effect on the host-pathogen interaction such that the risk of infectious disease during spaceflight is exacerbated and may uncover common conserved responses in the host and pathogen that can be targeted for countermeasure development.

We have successfully completed the objectives below in support of the spaceflight investigation (operationally called MICRO-5/PHOENIX) which launched aboard SpaceX-5 to the International Space Station on January 10, 2015: - Successful completion of our EVT (Engineering Verification Test) in early July of 2014. EVT operations were initiated in early June and all operations were performed smoothly and successfully. Video analysis and RNA integrity assessment were completed in early July and proved to be of the high integrity and quality needed.

- The science team arrived at the Kennedy Space Center (KSC) on November 4, 2014 for initial laboratory set up, media preparations, and experimental setup in order to meet the initial December 9th 2014 launch attempt. *ACTUAL LAUNCH SLIPPED TO JANUARY 10, 2015.

- Operations on orbit as well as the coordinating ground controls (the latter performed by the science team at KSC) occurred one day later than anticipated due to the ammonia leak alarm and emergency evacuation of the crew. This delay (which fortunately turned out to be a false alarm) still allowed operations to begin within the timeline required to ensure experimental integrity. All operations were completed within the Microgravity Science Glovebox. The science team was able to view the on orbit operations in the Experiment Monitoring Area (EMA) at Kennedy Space Center. Viewing showed that astronaut Terry Virts followed step-by-step instructions from BioServe to complete each operation and utilized sterile technique while handling the hardware as trained by BioServe during the astronaut training sessions. The science team and BioServe were able to communicate with the crew in real time.

- Post-Flight sample return occurred in segments. Ground Control samples arrived to the PI (Principal Investigator) lab by hand carry from KSC personnel. The flight samples arrived at Long Beach Airport in CA on 2-11-15 and were met by BioServe and the science team at that time. All early destow samples and hardware were inspected prior to handover to the science team for hand carry back to the PI lab. Remaining samples were returned to BioServe in Boulder, Colorado, about 6 weeks after SpaceX-5 return. The science team traveled to Boulder to retrieve those samples and shipped them back to the PI lab.

- Samples are currently being processed*. *(Note: Completion of sample analysis, including gene expression profiling, is awaiting necessary fund transfers to support this work from NASA to the PI). Initial testing of the sample contents has indicated that the infection was completed correctly with the pathogen and control bacteria types placed into the correct wells and contained within the hardware both on orbit and on the ground. There were no biocompatibility anomalies to report. Both video analysis and post-flight inspections demonstrated that the bacterial cultures were activated and grew on orbit. We also observed that the nematodes were highly viable at the time of infection and were successfully activated out of the dauer state using the control (E. coli) and infection (Salmonella) bacterial cultures.
