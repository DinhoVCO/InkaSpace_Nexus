Task Description: Plants experiencing spaceflight are quite normal in appearance but can exhibit growth habits distinctly different from plants on Earth. Historically the spaceflight-induced differences were difficult to dissect due to changing hardware and flight conditions. With the Advanced Biological Research System (ABRS) and a consistent International Space Station (ISS) orbital environment, it is now possible to confidently approach a dissection of the molecular biology guiding the altered growth of plants in spaceflight. This proposal involves experiments that specifically address growth and molecular changes in Arabidopsis that occur during spaceflight, bringing the molecular and genetic tools of Arabidopsis to bear on fundamental questions of root morphology, growth, and cell wall remodeling. During the Advanced Plant Experiments on Orbit - Transgenic Arabidopsis Gene Expression System (APEX-TAGES) experiments, we observed a remarkable number of gene expression changes during spaceflight that are associated with cell wall restructuring, especially in roots. We also observed, with the ABRS/GIS (GFP Imaging System) imaging system, genetically dependent changes in root growth direction, movement, and structure. The current experiments will employ the ABRS and the GIS to dissect and better understand the molecular changes guiding growth restructuring on orbit. In particular we hypothesize that spaceflight conditions engender a constant state of root cell wall modification during the cell file rotations that characterize spaceflight-induced root skewing. The specific experiments will involve mutants in root growth and cell wall signaling pathways identified in our previous spaceflight molecular studies, as well as new Green Fluorescent Protein biosensor lines also based on the genes identified in the previous flight experiments.

The overall goal of our program is to understand fundamental molecular biological responses to extraterrestrial environments. This goal aligns with fundamental Space Biology goals to understand the impact of spaceflight on biological systems to better enable the exploration imperative. We have used molecular biology and genetically tagged plants as biological monitors of spaceflight and space-related environments with great science return, leading to the development of new biological and hardware tools to study the spaceflight response with even higher fidelity in a wider variety of spaceflight-related environments. The focus of the current proposal is to build on these data and other insights gained from the execution of the APEX-TAGES experiments in the ABRS /GIS, advancing science while also advancing the telemetric imaging hardware and biological experiment support for the ISS and future platforms

Gaining insights into such mechanisms is recognized as fundamental within the decadal study and underpins answers to some of the biggest questions in spaceflight plant biology.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: To gain fundamental insight into how plants perceive and process the signals that control tropism and adaptive physiology in novel and extreme environments. It also highlights how the unique research environment of the ISS can address biological questions that cannot be answered on Earth where gravity would mask many of the underlying phenomena.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2016 
Task Progress: Final Summary:

The APEX03 experiment was a natural follow-on of the APEX01 experiment, and is an outstanding example of how the ISS can be utilized as an exploration laboratory to more science forward in a given field. In APEX01 we showed that plants responded to the spaceflight environment in a highly tissue-specific manner, and that cell wall remodeling played a large role in the physiological adaptation to spaceflight. Further, we refuted the paradigm set forth by Darwin in the 1880s that plants require gravity to initiate the root behavior called “skewing,” and fluorescent reporter genes suggested that there were changes in auxin signaling over time on orbit. In APEX03 we set forth to dissect the molecular changes over a developmental time course, utilize mutants in critical skewing and cell wall remodeling signal transduction pathways, and also use targeted fluorescent imaging of auxin-regulated genes in the specific cells of the root tip that guide root navigation.

Although the transcriptome analyses are ongoing, preliminary data suggest that development age plays a substantial role in the physiological adaptation process Transcriptomes were first evaluated four days after germination, where there were numerous differentially expressed genes (compared to ground controls). A set of sister plants were evaluated 8 days after germination on orbit, and it was found that as the plants develop, the number of genes needed to satisfy the needs of the physiological adaptation process diminishes.

In the on orbit microscopy experiments (utilizing the Light Microscopy module – the LMM) we found that auxin distributions in the gravity-sensing cells of the primary root are not gravity dependent, even though these distributions are key to gravity responses on Earth. It is therefore likely that the role of gravity is minimal compared to the inherent developmental cues guided by auxin to define the architecture of the root tip. Those developmental auxin gradients were co-opted then to be sensitive to gravity responses and it seems logical then that the auxin signaling system in roots was present in plants as a developmental system before evolving additional roles in gravity responses. These data actually changed aspects of a long held paradigm in plant biology that gravity is central to setting up the gradient of auxin distribution in a vertically growing root – called the “reverse fountain” model of auxin distribution. The term “vertical” carries a direct association with the gravity vector. Without the ability to remove gravity from the plant environment, there was no reason to eliminate the term from the vernacular or the force of gravity from the concept. Our results show that the reverse fountain is neither reverse nor a fountain, as these terms imply that gravity is involved in the reversal of liquid flow. Rather, the auxin flow of the primary root tip is fundamentally no different from the auxin flow systems that guide other parts of plant primary development, except that it has been further engaged in the gravity response.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2015 
Task Progress: The major operations of the past year’s reporting period was the Science Verification Test II, conducted in the VEGGIE hardware in June 2014, the Payload Verification Test, conducted in VEGGIE hardware, and finally the flight experiment of TAGES-ISA APEX03 on the International Space Station.

The Science Verification test II (SVTii) was conducted in two parts – one at Kennedy Space Center (KSC) in the ISSES orbital environment simulator chamber, and a parallel study in the PIs’ laboratory at the University of Florida (UF-SVTii). These plants were evaluated using the general morphology of the plants (growth patterns, health, and vigor). We used 12 day old plants as the primary developmental stage (and the only developmental stage in the KSC-SVTii), but would use information gathered in SVTii to reduce the age of plants in later experiments. The VEGGIE compatibility test demonstrated that although a substantially different growth platform than ABRS, it could be adapted to the plant support requirements defined by this proposal. The Payload Verification Test (PVT) was carried out following with the adjustments made to the experiment after the SVTii in VEGGIE. PVT was analyzed for quality of RNA that was recovered. The results from the PVT indicated that the experiment was prepared for launch.

The final section of this report covers the preparations, launch and return of the APEX-03-02/TAGES-ISA experiment. Summary of planning and on orbit operations are included. All samples have been returned to the lab and are currently being processed and analyzed.

I. SVTii setup and Operations

SVT for APEX-03 was repeated (SVTii) to adjust to new hardware for the experiment. Due to the loss of ABRS/GIS unit onboard the ISS, accommodations were made to grow the APEX-03 plants in the VEGGIE unit. This caused minor changes to be made to the experimental design, including the ages of the plants and the way the plates are imaged.

Plate planting: Arabidopsis seeds were planted on square, nutrient agar Petri plates. Two types of plates were prepared – an imaging plate to be inserted in to the Light Microscopy Module (LMM) and plates that would be harvested with one seed type per plate. The LMM plate would be used for imaging on flight and not harvested. Since the SVT had been run previously further molecular analysis was not conducted. Minor changes were made to the experimental design after the SVTii, included shortening the 5 and 12 day ages to 4 and 11 days to allow for consistent development morphology compared to plants grown previously in ABRS. This difference was caused by higher light levels in the VEGGIE hardware.

II. PVT: Payload Verification Testing

Using the same procedures outlined in the SVTii above, plates were prepared for the Payload Verification Test (PVT). Due to the change of hardware from the GIS to VEGGIE, a number of changes were made to the operations. The length of growth time for plants was reduced from 5 days to 4 days and from 12 to 11 days while 8 day old plates remained unchanged. Since VEGGIE does not have imaging capabilities built into the system, each set of plates (4 Day, 8 Day, and 11 Day) were photographed after being removed from VEGGIE and before being placed into the KFT.

LMM: The Light Microscopy Module (LMM) on the ISS was to be utilized during flight in place of the GIS in the ABRS unit. The LMM imaging plate contained multiple GFP-transformed lines of Arabidopsis were used in order to observe protein localization in spaceflight.In order to provide ease of reference, small fluorescent beads were placed on the plate to help with imaging the plants. These beads ranged in size from 0.77microns to 100 microns. The PVT for the LMM was conducted separate from the experimental PVT at KSC. PVT for LMM was run at Glenn Research Center in Cleveland, Ohio. During the PVT, imaging was performed on all ages of plants that would be imaged in flight. Parameters that would be needed for flight operations were set and verified.

III. Flight Experiment

Flight operations were based in part on the lessons learned from the SVTs, and then tested in PVT operations. The launch and return of samples were successful, and the full report on the flight operations will be included in the final report for the grant to be submitted later in this year.

Operation Objectives: The objectives were to expose 27 petri plates of plant seedlings to the ISS environment, take quality photograph of the plates, harvest the seedlings to RNAlater, and return the samples for post-flight analysis. Plates were harvested at different time points to help elucidate the different molecular responses of the varied developmental ages. In addition, a single plate from each age group was used for imaging of GFP reporter genes in the LMM.

Growth Set-Up: Plates are activated on station by removing black wrapping. Plates are then placed in racks that are then placed in VEGGIE. VEGGIE has the ability to accommodate up to 30 plates at a time. However, due to a shared hardware chamber, not all the APEX-03 plates were run concurrently. The 11 day plates were grown first, followed by a second insertion of 8 and 4 day plates.

Analyses of flight samples are currently on-going.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2014 
Task Progress: The major operations of the past year’s reporting period was the Science Verification Test conducted in the ABRS / GIS hardware in the fall of 2013, and then the initial explorations into using alternative plant growth hardware (VEGGIE) in the early part of 2014.

The Science Verification test (SVT) was conducted in two parts – one at Kennedy Space Center (KSC) in the ISSES orbital environment simulator chamber, and a parallel study in the PIs’ laboratory at the University of Florida (UF-SVT). Three sets of evaluation were used: 1-general morphology of the plants (growth patterns, health and vigor), 2-RNA yield and quality, 3- expression of select genes. We used 12 day old plants as the primary developmental stage (and the only developmental stage in the KSC-SVT), but also included younger developmental ages in the UF-SVT ops (5 day and 8 day old plants). Plants were delivered (turned-over) as dormant, Duvetyne cloth-wrapped plates at ambient temperature. Ten plants were turned over: one imaging plate (position 1), five primary plates for positions 2-6, and four spares. Plates were launched 48 hours after turn over and inserted into GIS 5 days after Turnover. Plate 1 was images every 6 hours for duration of the experiment. The plates were harvested 12 days after insertion into GIS (one full plate to one KFT). The KFTs were allowed to set a room temperature for at least 12 hours, and transferred to -80 to mimic MELFI. The UF-SVT parallel study was designed solely for science and did not test on orbit operations. The VEGGIE compatibility test demonstrated that although a substantially different growth platform than ABRS, it could be adapted to the plant support requirements defined by this proposal.

Lessons Learned from SVT. Better Ground Control images that match orbital in quality. A large amount of information is derived from the images taken of the plants just prior to harvest; growth patterns, morphology and morphometric data, as well as a gauge of general health and vigor of the plants. It is imperative that the harvest photos taken on orbit match the ones taken on the ground. Facile data delivery system The Nomad system used for SVT looks like it will work for data transfers. It is important to set the system up ahead of time, test accessibility of off-site PIs and ensure that it is updated regularly. Light levels . It is clear from the morphology of the plants at harvest that the plants were not receiving the same functional light levels as those plants run in APEX-01. It appears that the top tier plates of the SVT received close to the same amount of light as the bottom tier plates in APEX-01. The light level reaching the second tier plates was inadequate to support healthy growth. The APEX-01 plates in Figure 15 were 13.5 days old at harvest. The lighting problem needing resolution for PVT. Video support . Need better video support for local and ISS actions. What can we view at UF better than the slow updates? Insights into the developmental stages of Arabidopsis. Given the results of our SVT-related experiments at UF with respect to plant age and gene expression profiles, we have requested, and have been approved for, a change in the harvest scenarios. The new harvest schedule for the three runs will be after 5 days, 8 days, and 12 days respectively. The ERD will be revised to reflect this change, and the PVT will also accommodate the new harvest schedule.

Conclusions from VEGGIE test. The standard Laboratory vertical plate form factor actually fits well within the VEG hardware. Three standard 10cm x 28cm growth racks fit within the base of VEGGIE; each rack has capacity for 10 10cm square plates. The habitat provided by VEGGIE is substantially different from that provided by ABRS; primary among the differences is the lighting quality and the internal environment. The internal environment freely exchanges with cabin air. Although the bellows walls provide some mitigation of relative humidity, gaseous mixtures (CO2, VOCs) cannot be controlled. Lighting is provided by a bank of red, blue and green LEDs located in the top of the unit. The intensity can be varied, but the quality and intensity cannot be tailored to match ABRS. However, initial measurements suggested that it well within the tolerances for arabidopsis.

The VEGGIE compatibility test demonstrated that although a substantially different growth platform than ABRS, it could be adapted to the plant support requirements defined by this proposal.

A number of the morphometric phenomenon observed on orbit in APEX-01 can be mimicked on the ground in specialized growth configurations. Pains were taken to establish standardized growing systems that could deliver consistent biology to run parallel studies to NASA flight and comparable ground control studies.

On orbit, the flight plants exhibited skewing and waving behavior that was previously thought to be a gravity-dependent phenomenon (Paul et al. 2012, BMC Plant Biol, 12: 232) and we correlated this to aspects of gene expression patterns on orbit (Paul et al. 2013 BMC Plant Biol.13: 112) . Waving and skewing can be easily reproduced in the terrestrial laboratory by growing plants along the surface of agar plates tilted to a 45° slant. We are using this growth set-up to screen mutants, characterize reporter genes, and run experiments that mimic other environmental conditions that could be encountered in an orbital experiment, and also to characterize seed lines being targeted for flight applications in 2014.

In addition, a variety of new mutant seed lines and gene reporters chosen on the basis of the previous flight experiment results are being evaluated in the ground studies of the first year of the grant. Several genes which were highly induced in spaceflight were chosen for mutation experiments to characterize the effects the deletion had on terrestrial growth patterns. Of special interest are genes involved in touch-wound-remodeling of cell walls, which are among the highest differentially expressed genes. There is also likely a strong connection between the skewing phenomenon seen on orbit and this group of genes.

A number of new reporter gene lines are under development as well, currently 17, which are in various stages of completion to fully homozygous, usable lines.
