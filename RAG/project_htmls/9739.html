Space Biology Element: (1) Animal Biology: Vertebrate
Space Biology Cross-Element Discipline: (1) Musculoskeletal Biology
(2) Neurobiology
Space Biology Special Category: (1) Translational (Countermeasure) Potential
Task Description: Background: We have demonstrated that loss of vestibular information following bilateral lesions in rats and mice decreases lower limb bone density, and that this effect may be mediated by activation of the sympathetic nervous system (SNS). Based on the knowledge that reduced vestibular information, increased sympathetic activation, and bone loss are common hallmarks of long-term space missions, we hypothesized that space-induced bone loss has a vestibular and sympathetic origin, and that vestibular stimulation might increase bone density.

The main objective of this proposal is to evaluate the effect of vestibular stimulation on bone mass, with the goal of designing a new therapeutic approach to prevent bone loss in space. The project is divided into two specific aims. The first one will define the short and long-term effects of vestibular hair cell dysfunction on the vegetative system and on bone mass in mice, in order to determine whether this effect is compensated with time. Catecholamine levels, brown adipose tissue Ucp1/Gapdh expression, 3D-microcomputed tomography, and quantitative static and dynamic bone histomorphometry will be used. The second specific aim will characterize the effects of vestibular stimulation on the vegetative system and bone. Different centrifuge protocols (continuous and discontinuous, 1.5G/2G/2.5G) and galvanic vestibular stimulation will be used. Centrifugations will be performed in Dr. Makishma (University of Texas Medical Branch (UTMB), USA) and in Dr. Besnard and Prof. Denise (University of Caen, France) laboratories. Galvanic stimulation will be performed in Dr. Elefteriou's laboratory, in collaboration with Dr. Wood (Houston/UTMB, USA). All the bone analyses will be performed at Vanderbilt University (Dr. Elefteriou, Vanderbilt University). The part of the study performed in France is supported by the University of Caen Basse-Normandie and by the Centre national d'études spatiales (CNES).

The ultimate goal of this multi-disciplinary effort is to determine whether vestibular stimulation could have beneficial effects on bone mineral density and sympathetic function during extended space missions. These findings may have application on Earth as well for the treatment or prevention of low bone mass conditions.

Research Impact/Earth Benefits: Our research seeks to understand if and how structural and functional alterations in the vestibular system lead to changes in bone remodeling. This work is expected to impact our understanding of microgravity-induced bone loss, which is relevant to extended travel duration of future space missions, but also to fracture risk in patients with vestibular disorders on Earth.

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2015 
Task Progress: Aim1. To define the short and long-term effects of vestibular loss on the vegetative system and on bone mass in mice.

This Aim is completed and results were reported in the Journal of Bone and Mineral Research in December 2014 (final published version June 2015-- http://dx.doi.org/10.1002/jbmr.2426 ).

Aim 2. To characterize the effects of vestibular stimulation on the vegetative system and bone.

a) Centrifugations. Four to five month-old het/+ (Control) and het/het mice (devoid of otoconia from birth) submitted to continuous centrifugation at 2G for 4 weeks in Dr. Besnard’s laboratory (France) did not experience any significant bone changes at the distal femur, as assessed by 3D computed microtomography (12 um resolution). Bone volume/tissue volume and tissue mineral density were indeed not significantly different between groups (n=10 per group).

After a setback of several months to get the centrifuge operational in Dr. T. Makishima’s laboratory, 4 month-old female WT mice have been submitted to intermittent centrifugation (2h/day) at 2G for 4 weeks. Here again, no differences were detected in terms of bone volume/tissue volume and tissue mineral density between control and treated group, as assessed by 3D computed microtomography (n=10 per group). Because of the negative nature of these results, this work has not been submitted for publication yet.

b) Galvanic vestibular stimulation. The Hindlimb Suspension (HS) model has been set up successfully in collaboration with Dr. D. Perrien at Vanderbilt. Following our discussions with Dr. Wood and Dr. Makishima in Galveston, we have determined the conditions of GVS stimulation that can induce nystagmus, using an eye tracker recording system. Then, WT mice have been tested for their response to daily GVS stimulations, in absence or presence of hindlimb suspension (HS). HS, as expected, triggered a significant bone loss in distal femurs of WT mice, as assessed by 3D computed microtomography. However, daily GVS stimulations for 21 days did not significantly alter bone mass. Daily GVS stimulations under HS conditions did not affect bone mass either (no protection from bone loss nor further bone loss).

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2014 
Task Progress: Aim1. To define the short and long-term effects of vestibular loss on the vegetative system and on bone mass in mice.

Vestibular lesions (VBX) have been performed in all proposed groups for bone analyses 1, 3, and 6 months post-lesion.

Group A (1 month VBX) has been analyzed by uCT and histomorphometry. Significant bone loss and architectural changes were observed in the distal femoral epiphyses of VBX mice compared to sham-operated mice (both genders). Histomorphometry analyses revealed a significant reduction in the bone surfaces covered by osteoblasts in VBX mice, and increase in osteoclast number. These results validate the effect of arsenylate lesions on bone in mice, and serves as our main reference to assess the effect of time on VBX-induced bone loss. The second and third group of mice (3 and 6 months post lesion) have been analyzed as well. uCT analyses did not reveal significant bone changes. These results indicate that the effect of vestibular lesions on bone is transient and compensated for with time.

We also collected brown adipose tissues to assess Ucp1 expression as readout of sympathetic outflow. Ucp1 expression was found upregulated in the VBX group 1 month post-lesion but not 3 and 6 months post-lesion, consistently with the bone data. These results suggest that peripheral sympathetic outflow is increased in response to VBX and contributes to VBX-induced bone loss.

Lastly, we collected bone marrow stromal cells (BMSCs) from sham and VBX mice to assess a possible effect of VBX on osteoblast progenitor differentiation or proliferation. Cfu-ap (=osteoblast commitment) and cfu-ob (osteoblast differentiation) assays were performed using BMSCs collected 1 month post-lesion. No significant changes were detected in both assays. These negative results suggest that VBX induces bone loss likely by reducing osteoblast proliferation, differentiation, or function in vivo rather than by reducing commitment to the osteoblasts lineage (in that latter case a reduction of cfu-ap colonies would have been observed). These results are in line with previous results and with a contribution of the sympathetic nervous system in VBX-induced bone loss.

Aim 2. To characterize the effects of vestibular stimulation on the vegetative system and bone.

a) Centrifugations. We have met with Dr. T. Makishima at the 2013 NSBRI meeting in Galveston to discuss the details of the centrifugation experiments, and also kept regular contact with Dr. Besnard in France to coordinate our efforts. The centrifuge (with 2 arms, hence double capacity) in Galveston is still not operational, but is expected to be early summer 2014. The centrifuge in Dr. Besnard laboratory is now operational and pilot experiments using WT and Het-/- mice are ongoing.

b) Galvanic vestibular stimulation. The first phase of this experiment, which consists in establishing the Hindlimb Suspension (HS) model, has been completed, in collaboration with Dr. D. Perrien at Vanderbilt. IACUC protocol, custom cages, and mouse care are now in place, and our pilot experiment confirmed proper animal care and unloading-induced bone loss with our new setting. We have met Dr. Wood and Dr. Makishima in Galveston to discuss the details of the GVS stimulation experiment. The stimulator is now in our possession and the occurrence of nystagmus with this apparatus as been confirmed by Dr. Makishima, using eye tracker recording system in place in Galveston. WT mice are currently tested for their response to daily GVS stimulation, in absence of hindlimb suspension (HS). Depending on the results of this experiment, the second phase of the proposed experiment will be performed (GVC+HS).

Task Progress & Bibliography Information FY2013 
Task Progress: Aim1. To define the short and long-term effects of vestibular loss on the vegetative system and on bone mass in mice.

Vestibular lesions have been performed in all proposed groups for bone analyses 1, 3, and 6 months post-lesion.

Group A (1 month VBX) has been analyzed by uCT and histomorphometry. Significant bone loss and architectural changes were observed in the distal femoral epiphyses of VBX mice compared to sham-operated mice (both genders). These results validate the effect of arsenylate lesions on bone in mice, and serves as our main reference to assess the effect of time on VBX-induced bone loss.

The second group of mice (3 months post lesion) has been partly analyzed as well. uCT analyses did not reveal significant bone changes. These preliminary results suggest that the effect of vestibular lesions on bone is transient and compensated for with time. The third group of mice (6 months post VBX) will allow us to substantiate further this conclusion.

We collected vestibular biopsies to investigate vestibular histology following VBX. These data will allow us to know whether the arsenylate model affects bone remodeling because of vestibular structural or functional alterations. We also collected brown adipose tissues to assess Ucp1 expression as readout of sympathetic outflow. Lastly, we collected bone marrow stromal cells (BMSCs) from sham and VBX mice to assess a possible effect of VBX on osteoblast progenitor differentiation or proliferation. Cfu-ap (=osteoblast commitment) and cfu-ob (osteoblast differentiation) assays were performed using BMSCs collected 1 month post lesion. No significant changes were detected in both assays. These negative results suggest that VBX induces bone loss likely by reducing osteoblast proliferation, differentiation or function in vivo rather than by reducing commitment to the osteoblasts lineage (in that latter case a reduction of cfu-ap colonies would have been observed). These results are in line with previous results and with a contribution of the sympathetic nervous system in VBX-induced bone loss. However, the lack of effect of VBX on osteoclast formation and bone resorption was unexpected and could be explained by either inappropriate analysis time points (the osteoclastogenic induction induced by VBX and increased SNS outflow might be transitory and happening before the one month time point) or additional mechanism(s) (SNS independent) may be involved.

We have met with Dr. Makishima at the 2013 NSBRI meeting in Galveston to discuss the details of the centrifugation experiments, and also kept regular contact with Dr. Besnard in France to coordinate our efforts. The centrifuge (with 2 arms, hence double capacity) will be operational this summer, and a pilot study is scheduled at that time. The centrifuge in Dr. Besnard laboratory is now operational. WT and Het-/- mice are breeding to be used in this experiment, which will be done this summer

b) Galvanic vestibular stimulation. The first phase of this experiment, which consists in establishing the Hindlimb Suspension (HS) model, is ongoing, in collaboration with Dr. Perrien at Vanderbilt. IACUC protocol, custom cages, and mouse care are now in place, and a pilot experiment is ongoing to confirm unloading-induced bone loss with our new setting. We have met Dr. Wood and Dr. Makishima in Galveston to discuss the details of the GVS stimulation experiment, which we will start in August during our visit in Dr. Makishima’s laboratory. In the meantime, the electrodes have been adapted to mice and the GVS system is currently tested using ocular responses as readout (nystagmus assay using eye tracker recording system in place in Galveston).
